# Robert Johansen & Vic - Mount Isa Quartz & Silicon Processing - Summary and Insights

**Date:** 2025-10-20

---

## Summary and Insights

### Project Overview and Strategic Direction
Shane Kelly outlined that the Mt Isa Research Centre feasibility study evolved from a scoping study conducted approximately 12 months prior by the Strategic Minerals Institute (UQ), initially focused on rare earths but now encompassing broader critical minerals (4:11). The key differentiator for this phase is a **commercially focused approach** rather than purely academic, with Mount Isa City Council deliberately choosing external consultants over SMI to ensure the Centre can operate sustainably without ongoing government support (4:11-5:56). The Centre aims to provide **complex metallurgical testing capabilities** that are currently unavailable in the region, preventing companies from having to send samples interstate or overseas for specialized work (6:10-8:30).

### Quartz Processing and Testing Requirements
Robert Johansen confirmed that Mount Isa Quartz is currently drilling in the region, and if the project progresses, there will be demand for sample testing work (8:40-9:56). He distinguished between routine assay work for metallurgical-grade quartz—which existing labs like ALS can handle—and **more complex process testing for ultra-high purity quartz**, which currently must be sent to Germany and represents a capability gap in Australia (9:56-10:36). The discussion revealed that Australia lacks domestic capability for downstream processing of ultra-high purity quartz into fused silica and low-alpha products, and potentially for polysilicon process testing as well (10:49-12:06).

### Ultra-High Purity Quartz Testing Gap
A specific example highlighted the scale of the testing challenge: one company has samples with contaminants that ALS cannot process in Australia, requiring the samples to be sent first to Sweden, then Germany, at a cost of **three quarters of a million dollars** (7:54-8:30). Johansen identified **Dorfner Anzaplan in Germany** as the company currently conducting complex quartz testing work across a wide range of mineral types (14:48-15:02). This established the clear market need for bringing this specialized capability to the Australian market.

### Intellectual Property Considerations
A critical issue raised by Johansen concerns **IP management and ownership** for any Research Centre that develops processing capabilities (13:19-16:49). He noted that companies like Dorfner naturally want to protect their IP and maintain their service monopoly where possible (15:38-16:10). The question of how IP would be managed—both existing industry IP used in the facility and new IP developed at the facility—was identified as "not necessarily a showstopper, but a question that would need to be answered" before companies would commit to using such a Centre (16:10-16:49). Kelly acknowledged this was an important consideration they hadn't fully addressed yet (15:40-15:53).

### Common User Facility for Quartz Processing
The concept of a **common user crushing, washing, and bagging plant** in Mount Isa was explored, with Johansen indicating that their downstream Townsville facility would likely take a portfolio approach with multiple quartz suppliers—"more than one, less than 10" potential suppliers in the region (17:42-18:28). However, he expressed caution about the commercial viability, noting that scalability and timing uncertainty make it difficult to assess feasibility at this early stage (19:10-20:22). He pointed to industry examples like the Pilbara and Gladstone LNG where parallel facilities weren't necessarily the most efficient approach, suggesting that aligning varying interests would be "quite a tough piece of work" (20:26-21:39). Additionally, Johansen noted that basic crushing and screening is **not high-value or specialized work** with low barriers to entry, meaning companies could easily set up their own small plants rather than relying on a common facility (21:39-21:55).

### Timeline and Production Targets
Johansen provided specific timeline targets for the **MetSilicon Townsville facility**: they aim to have production capacity by **2030**, with a development application planned for Q1 2026 (first quarter of next year), construction commencing mid-2027, and commissioning by end of 2029 (23:18-24:04). The initial facility would be a **50,000 tonne smelter** drawing quartz from Mount Isa prospects and other sources, with sequential expansion of additional smelters and polysilicon production planned thereafter (24:04-25:12). Johansen indicated that the services described by the Research Centre would likely be needed **"towards the end of this decade"** (25:12).

### Confirmed Market Demand
Despite uncertainties around scale and timing, Johansen provided a qualified endorsement of the Centre concept, stating there is "**certainly... probably yes**" a need for detailed quartz processing capability in the Mount Isa region, subject to exploration variables and mining project success (21:57-22:25). He concluded that "if you're at the very early stages of figuring out whether there's a need for a Research facility that has capability in quartz, the answer is probably yes" (22:25-22:42), providing validation for the feasibility study to proceed.
