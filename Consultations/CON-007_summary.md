# Birgit Tegethoff & Werner Business Finland - Summary and Insights

**Date:** 2025-11-05

---

## Summary and Insights
### Business Finland's Role and Mandate
Business Finland is a 100% government-owned innovation funding and export promotion agency with an ambitious target to reach 4% of GDP for R&D funding by 2030, currently hovering around 3% compared to Australia's approximately 1.4% (23:33, 26:11). The agency deploys approximately €1 billion annually in strategic R&D funding, typically using a co-funding model where they might provide €20 million while corporates contribute €30 million for three to four-year projects (26:11). They can deploy additional funding to encourage larger corporates to bring in SMEs, scale-ups, and startups into their ecosystems—potentially doubling the total project funding to include up to 100 additional players (26:11). The organization maintains a strong commercial focus, targeting Technology Readiness Levels 6-9 and emphasizing market-ready products and services rather than research that ends up "in a drawer" (26:11, 27:16).
### Finnish Research Capabilities and Infrastructure
GTK (Geological Survey of Finland) and VTT (government-owned research institute, the largest in Northern Europe) provide sophisticated research capabilities with a pragmatic, commercially-minded approach (10:49). Both organizations excel at operating in the "Valley of Death" to achieve commercial scale outcomes (27:16). A standout facility is their digital underground test mine at 1.4 kilometers deep, equipped with a 5G network installed by Nokia, where major Australian mining companies including BHP pay to test mining equipment, drones, trucks, and explosives (27:16). The Finnish research landscape is characterized by very strategic and focused work that isn't "research for the sake of research" but maintains clear commercial objectives (9:06).
### Existing Australia-Finland Collaborations
Extensive collaboration already exists between GTK and the Sustainable Minerals Institute (SMI) at the University of Queensland, with multiple ongoing projects and regular personnel exchanges between Finland and Australia (7:11). Some professors have spent significant time in Finland, and there's even trilateral collaboration involving SMI's subsidiary in Chile (7:11). The EU-Australia Critical Raw Materials MOU signed in May 2025 provides a formal framework, with a non-public roadmap containing specific work packages where Finland has committed to play an active role despite being a small country (20:43, 21:31). Finland specifically identified research development and tailings reprocessing as one of three priority buckets for collaboration (21:31).
### EU Critical Raw Materials Act Requirements
The EU Critical Raw Materials Act (not just a directive, but actual law) mandates that by 2030, no more than 65% of critical raw materials can be imported from any single country, requiring supply chain diversification across multiple nations (20:43, 28:38). The Act also requires recycled content in battery value chains and includes mandatory traceability and provenance verification requirements (28:38). Finland maintains strong ESG criteria and remains committed to carbon neutrality by 2035, which influences all their partnerships and projects (28:38). These requirements create clear opportunities for collaboration with like-minded countries including Australia, particularly Queensland (28:38).
### Technical Feasibility Work Opportunity
The Mt Isa Research Centre project is seeking technical expertise to ensure appropriate equipment selection for planned services and to understand service delivery costs (9:06). Shane Kelly indicated readiness to appoint a technical expert or company for this feasibility work, which doesn't require a public tender process (8:08). Tuli Kelly Asalo from GTK was identified as a potential contact through SMI connections (10:49). The scope has expanded beyond the initial rare earths focus to include broader critical minerals capabilities, bulk sampling, advanced metallurgical testing, piloting, and tailings reprocessing to ensure the facility is future-proof and commercially viable at scale (4:54, 35:45).
### Collaborative Operational Model
A key insight from stakeholder consultations is that research centers are "only as good as the people that are in the Centre," with strong trust relationships and quality assurance being critical success factors (18:27). Rather than Mount Isa City Council attempting to recruit and run the facility independently, a collaborative model involving GTK, SMI, and JK Tech would provide immediate credibility, established expertise, and robust QA/QC from day one (18:27). This model recognizes that "you don't have to reinvent the wheel" and can leverage existing capabilities while adapting them to local needs (16:30). The concept aligns well with GTK's approach and could potentially involve both technical feasibility support and ongoing operational involvement (9:06).
### Commercial and Research Balance
The Mt Isa Research Centre aims to avoid becoming "another glorified lab" by maintaining both commercial and research focus (4:54). The facility will support practical applied research on local geology while also maintaining capability across copper, zinc, lead, and phosphates to ensure a sufficiently broad market (4:54). The design philosophy emphasizes building at commercial scale from the outset, allowing for potential future operation by commercial partners like Core or ALS once the facility reaches certain maturity milestones (35:45). This approach ensures the facility remains relevant and sustainable long-term.
### Tailings Reprocessing Focus
Tailings reprocessing emerged as a significant opportunity area given the very large volumes of tailings in the Mt Isa region (4:54). This capability aligns perfectly with EU Critical Raw Materials Act requirements for recycled content in battery value chains by 2030 (20:43). Finland has specific expertise in tailings reprocessing, with GTK personnel actively gathering information and building connections in the Australian market (16:30). The facility plans to incorporate bulk sampling and larger piloting programs specifically to support companies looking to reprocess regional tailings (4:54).
### Practical Next Steps and Site Visit Proposal
Shane Kelly committed to providing a one-pager outlining the technical work scope for Business Finland and GTK to review internally (18:27). Werner Merzeder raised the possibility of a benchmarking visit to Finland to view their facilities firsthand (33:45). Given the project's "very good travel budget," this approach could be more practical than bringing Finnish experts to Mount Isa for the technical planning work, potentially allowing the refinement of plans to occur in Finland where the reference facilities can be directly observed (35:45). This would also reduce costs from the Finnish side while leveraging project budget availability. The seasonal nature of exploration work in Mount Isa (November through early March is non-operational due to heat) was noted as a factor in facility operations planning (38:08).
### Strategic Timing and Funding Environment
Multiple participants agreed that "timing couldn't be better" for a critical minerals research facility given current geopolitical and market dynamics (11:50, 27:32). Shane Kelly expressed confidence that if the feasibility study demonstrates viability, funding is "more likely than not" to be secured (4:54). The project benefits from co-funding between Critical Minerals Queensland and Mount Isa City Council, with additional support from the Queensland government, which has been "quite supportive and open" to these types of initiatives (36:07). The sovereign capability dimension and "flavour of the month" status of critical minerals creates a favorable environment for the project's success (4:54).
