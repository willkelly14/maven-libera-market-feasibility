# Dieter Kluger & Tony Piccaro (CMQ) - QR Cuff Facility & Beneficiation Planning - Summary and Insights

**Date:** 2025-11-05

---

### GTK Finland as a Model and Technical Feasibility Partner
Business Finland representatives introduced GTK Mintech as a potential technical feasibility partner for the Mt Isa Research Centre (4:31-4:56). The GTK model emerged as a compelling template: it operates as a self-funded R&D facility that functions essentially as a "showroom" for Outokumpu, the equipment manufacturer located next door (7:35-8:24). GTK identifies problems at mine sites globally, conducts research and pilot studies to develop solutions, locks down designs at demonstration scale, and then Outokumpu manufactures and exports the equipment—creating a complete commercialization pathway that supports Finnish industry (7:35-9:05). The facility began as a fully government-subsidized entity and evolved over 30-50 years to become self-sufficient and self-funding (14:25-14:58), demonstrating the critical role of sustained government investment in building this type of capability.
GTK's expertise in beneficiation and their experience with technology readiness levels 4-8 makes them well-positioned to support the Mt Isa feasibility work (5:00-5:46, 16:03-16:33). However, there was discussion about how to frame the engagement carefully—if approached as "we want to replicate your model," GTK might be reluctant to share 50 years of intellectual property for a consulting fee, so the scope should focus on the more bounded technical feasibility questions around equipment sizing and operating costs rather than concept development (15:05-16:33).
### Australia's Critical Gap in Technology Commercialization
Tony Piccaro highlighted a fundamental weakness in Australia's innovation ecosystem: while the country excels at mineral processing R&D and competes globally at technology readiness level 3 (university research), there is a significant capability gap between TRL 4-8—the commercialization and scale-up phase where technologies move from lab to deployment (12:04-12:23). Australia lacks the small and medium enterprises that build critical and novel processing equipment, unlike Europe, Japan, and South Korea where strong manufacturing sectors support technology transfer (12:23-13:18). This gap is compounded by the reality that successful technology commercialization takes 20-30 years of sustained effort, making expectations management critical for government stakeholders (13:43-14:25). The implication for Mt Isa is that building genuine commercialization capability—not just an R&D facility—requires long-term commitment and a different design philosophy than a typical research centre.
### Highly Sensitive QR Cuff Beneficiation Relocation Concept
Dieter Kluger shared highly sensitive internal planning: Critical Minerals Queensland is actively considering relocating the beneficiation half of the Townsville QR Cuff facility to a Mount Isa centre of excellence, potentially via truck convoy, while retaining the hydromet operations in Townsville (22:07-23:47). This has not been communicated to Sedgman (who is building the facility) or broader government and carries significant political risk (25:12-26:42). The driver is pragmatic: the Townsville facility is severely space-constrained, attempting to fit both large-scale beneficiation and complex hydromet into one site (23:44-24:00, 30:30-30:46). Separating the two would allow Townsville to focus on hydromet innovation (which requires smaller feed volumes of concentrate) and give Mt Isa the large-scale beneficiation capability with room to grow and diversify equipment over time (30:37-32:23).
The economics are compelling: vanadium ore contains only about \$20 of vanadium per tonne, but disposing of the 800 kg of beneficiation waste (out of 1,000 kg feed) as Category 1 waste in Townsville costs approximately \$1,000 per tonne due to chemicals added during processing (28:36-29:44). Locating beneficiation near mine sites in the west, where waste disposal into tailings facilities is far cheaper and ore doesn't need to be trucked east, makes substantial economic sense (27:42-27:55). The timeline is uncertain—commissioning is planned for late 2026, and CMQ suspects operational issues with waste management may accelerate the pivot (24:47-25:12).
### Facility Sizing and the Importance of Plug-and-Play Design
Determining the "right size" for a beneficiation research facility is inherently complex: there is no single correct answer because appropriate scale depends on the commodity, ore characteristics, grade, and whether the goal is R&D, pilot, or demonstration work (17:13-17:33, 35:57-36:47). GTK's approach has been to accumulate diverse equipment over decades, with the advantage of having Outokumpu's manufacturing yard next door to borrow, test, and return or sell equipment as projects evolve (18:48-19:26). For Mt Isa, the implication is designing for a large flexible floor plan—essentially hardstand area and lab space that can accommodate equipment brought in and swapped out, including a spare area for external parties to bring their own pilot gear for short-term trials (19:26-20:15).
Critically, the facility design must prioritize modularity and "plug-and-play" capability, which GTK has refined over years of iteration (33:45-34:13). Equipment must be designed so that swapping components in and out is straightforward—analogous to a car that's easy to service versus one requiring half the engine to be removed to change a filter (34:00-34:13). GTK recently completed a new facility expansion after spending several years rethinking layout based on accumulated experience (34:00-34:29), underscoring that this design work is non-trivial and could be valuable to include in the feasibility scope (34:13-34:29).
### Scale Definition as a Fundamental Design Decision
The distinction between R&D scale, pilot scale, and demonstration scale emerged as perhaps the most important unresolved design question (58:25-59:02). These scales require vastly different equipment sizes, support infrastructure (water, waste, utilities), and operational models (35:57-36:47, 21:26-21:45). The QR Cuff facility in Townsville attempted to "do everything" for all critical minerals and found the cost would run to hundreds of millions; scaling down to vanadium alone and reducing scope has still left the facility constrained and with unproven functionality (59:40-1:00:30). 
For Mt Isa, the decision hinges partly on how it integrates with the broader ecosystem: if the QR Cuff hydromet plant (designed to process 200 kg/hour of concentrate) needs feed for a six-month trial, the beneficiation plant in Mt Isa would need to produce thousands of tonnes of concentrate at a scale well beyond lab or small pilot (46:42-47:00, 58:42-59:13). Tony emphasized that the facility will inevitably evolve organically and should target one commodity initially—beneficiation around the Isa region is the right focus—but being explicit upfront about whether the core function is R&D, pilot, or demonstration is essential to scoping infrastructure, waste systems, and cost (59:40-1:00:43, 45:46-46:10).
### Waste Management and Water Systems as Critical Infrastructure
Beneficiation at any scale beyond lab work generates substantial waste streams and complex water management requirements (38:05-39:03). On-site tailings storage is necessary, though there may be opportunity to transport some waste to Glencore's existing facilities (38:17-38:23). Water recycling is not optional: commercial mineral processing plants minimize fresh water use and recycle process water from tailings, which builds up chemical impurities that affect processing behavior—test work must replicate these conditions or it won't translate to commercial reality (39:03-40:13). This requires appropriately sized tanks, water treatment systems, and sufficient waste storage because environmental approvals often require analysis before off-site disposal, which can take a week, necessitating buffer capacity (37:55-38:05).
Finding expert advice on waste strategy has proven difficult for both CMQ and the Mt Isa project: while consultants exist who can characterize ore for acid drainage or design tailings facilities, there's a gap in expertise around understanding the full process—how impurities build up through the beneficiation circuit and what that means for waste characterization and operational design (40:50-44:22). Shane proposed a working session with CMQ to map out considerations and define a tight scope for any external waste consultant (43:13-43:49), and Tony noted that even Sedgman's waste strategy for QR Cuff was high-level and generic because it lacked access to specific ore characteristics (42:23-42:32).
### Commercial Model: Ecosystem, Not Competition
The facility cannot and should not attempt to compete with existing commercial laboratories; instead, the model must incorporate labs and equipment suppliers into an ecosystem (50:11-50:31). Most commercial labs (ALS, etc.) have minimal capability in the region—the Mt Isa ALS is essentially a sample depot, with everything shipped south or west for testing (50:32-50:59). Building local capability is valuable, but the commercial arrangements—whether ALS or another provider operates lab services via tender, for example—should be determined through a transparent process (51:11-51:40). Paul Holden had raised concerns about encroaching on commercial lab services, but the counter-argument is that if the facility is scaled appropriately, it could eventually be operated by a commercial entity like Core or ALS if that proves to be the best model (49:00-50:11).
The GTK model again offers guidance: it's not just miners and juniors using the facility, but equipment suppliers demonstrating their technologies in a vendor-agnostic way, with different suppliers offering competing flotation cell designs suited to different applications (18:48-19:26). Shane noted that companies like Glencore have demonstration equipment sitting unused in yards that could be contributed to a shared facility (32:00-32:23). The vision extends to a learning centre with training labs for laboratory technicians and process technicians, potentially in partnership with universities and TAFE to develop courses aligned with the Research Centre's needs (54:37-54:56). Local content requirements could ensure that external operators recruit and train locally, building genuine regional capability rather than flying expertise in and out (53:57-54:37).
### Early Anchor Projects and Operational Model
Having a few anchor projects identified early would help dictate equipment sizing, waste requirements, and the overall design, rather than designing from a blank page (47:08-48:04). A tailings reprocessing focus was suggested as a potential anchor theme (47:08-48:08). The facility must be designed to handle multiple simultaneous projects—not operating one at a time—because supporting explorers and juniors with assay and test work will generate high throughput and demand (48:04-48:41). Shane acknowledged the risk: "if you do it right you'll be overwhelmed... if you do it wrong you'll have no one" (48:38-48:41). 
Northwest Phosphates has already expressed strong interest in using the lab for value-add research—moving phosphate from fertilizer into animal feeds markets—representing a potential early large lab project that could help prove the facility (52:45-53:13). Similarly, established miners working on gold, copper, and base metals (which often contain critical minerals as by-products) could provide the "bread and butter" research work that sustains operations while critical minerals projects scale up (52:03-53:00).
