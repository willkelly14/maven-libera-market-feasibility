# Gavin Loyden - QEM Vanadium Processing & Metallurgy - Summary and Insights

**Date:** 2025-11-10

---

## Summary and Insights
### Gavin Loyden's Background and Transition
Gavin Loyden has recently retired from QEM and is currently considering board positions while taking time for property upgrades and holidays (1:36-2:22). Prior to his work with vanadium, Loyden was raising capital for other mining projects before recognizing the future potential of vanadium, particularly for renewable energy applications in China (17:22-18:08).

### Project Context and Scope Evolution
The Mt Isa Research Centre project originated approximately a year ago when SMI was brought in by Mount Isa City Council with support from Critical Minerals Queensland and Paul Holden (2:22-2:44). The scope has expanded from an initial focus on rare earths to encompass broader critical minerals capabilities, including copper and other minerals in the region (2:44-3:56). Loyden confirmed this is a useful dovetail to the Townsville facility, with the Mt Isa centre focusing more on specialist minerals and rare earths as a distinct basket from vanadium-focused work (3:56-4:06, 16:45-17:03).

### Bulk Sampling and Regional Coordination
A key differentiator for the Mt Isa centre is the planned large-scale beneficiation capability to support tailings reprocessing (3:56). Shane noted that this would allow beneficiation work to occur closer to mine sites, enabling the transport of concentrates rather than bulk ore to facilities like Townsville (4:06-4:49). The Mt Isa location offers advantages in terms of available space for expansion without the urban constraints of Townsville (5:11-5:45).

### R&D Tax Registration and Sovereign Capability
Loyden strongly supported the centre being R&D registered, noting that QEM had to send samples overseas for years and received no tax advantage through the R&D Tax Incentive for that offshore work -- it was "all straight out of your pocket" (7:13-7:28). He emphasized that R&D registration would make the centre far more attractive to explorers and investors, providing another credential tick for projects (7:34-8:14). The strategic value lies in building sovereign capability for advanced metallurgy, positioning Australia as a global centre of excellence for critical minerals rather than having to send samples to the UK, Finland, Sweden, Germany, the US, or China (7:06-7:20).

### QEM's Vanadium Project Technical Details
QEM's deposit at Julia Creek is relatively shallow, averaging 65 metres depth, suitable for open-pit mining with backfilling (8:26-8:40). The deposit runs like a coal seam through the entire project area in the Toolebuc shale formation, which extends throughout much of the state (14:41-14:44). QEM targeted the "fresh rock" zone with hydrocarbons intact, deeper than competitors who work in the oxidized zone closer to surface, pursuing a dual-commodity approach extracting both oil (for diesel production) and vanadium from the residue (8:48-9:51). The project is approximately 250 kilometres from Mount Isa and is well-positioned with road and rail infrastructure (15:21-15:41).

### Metallurgical Testing Challenges and Overseas Dependencies
QEM built a small pilot plant (constructed in India) for hydrocarbon extraction and sent the resulting material to the UK for vanadium extraction testing through GSA Environmental, a company associated with a UK university (9:48-10:17, 20:22-21:09). During COVID, the UK work faced extreme delays -- "months" -- due to lab shutdowns, as the GSA lab was located inside the university (18:57-19:18, 20:22). In contrast, QEM's work with HRL in Melbourne continued uninterrupted during COVID because of HRL's work in coal and other critical elements (21:09). Another example cited involved ultrafine quartz testing in China, where there was no visibility on turnaround times and concerns about result reliability (19:38-20:22).

### Vanadium Industry Complexity and Critical Mineral Economics
Loyden emphasized that vanadium metallurgy is "very challenging work" that differs significantly from location to location and even within individual projects over short distances, creating technical hurdles for all companies in the sector (9:58-10:22). He noted that companies like RVT previously did work in China but can no longer do so due to critical minerals considerations, describing this as a "two-edged sword" (10:22-11:20). Fundamentally, Loyden argued that critical minerals projects will be "marginal at best as commercial operations" unless fully government-backed, citing China's fully subsidized rare earths production where projects only work as by-products of other operations (11:20-12:31). His consistent message to government has been about the "criticality of critical" -- for the US, vanadium is essential for weapons (no jet or missile can go supersonic without it), while for Australia it's about renewable energy, specifically vanadium redox flow batteries as the only viable large-scale, long-duration energy storage technology in Australian conditions (11:20-12:31, 17:22-18:08).

### Government Engagement and Timing Pressures
Loyden recounted discussions with the US Department of Defense in Sydney and observed that Minister Madeleine King went from being unfamiliar with vanadium to "spruiking nothing but vanadium" at the World Mining Congress after their meeting (18:08-18:43). He noted that the critical minerals agenda "has landed now" with government and that US funding may be forthcoming (12:31-13:16). Shane mentioned feeling pressure to accelerate his feasibility work timeline in response to this urgency (12:49).

### Infrastructure Dependencies
Copper String power transmission is a critical enabler for the region -- QEM designed a gigawatt-scale renewables project that has been sold but is now on hold pending the Copper String decision (16:45-23:47). Without Copper String, new projects cannot proceed because diesel plants would place them in the top emitter category (24:08-24:18). Water availability was also flagged as a key infrastructure consideration beyond the project itself (16:45). The change in state government has created uncertainty, though there are whispers that Copper String is "more likely than less likely" to proceed, driven in part by Glencore's need for power (23:47-24:08).

### Proposed Centre's Role in Third-Party Verification
Shane outlined a potential stable revenue stream: providing independent third-party verification of concentrate characteristics for suppliers to Glencore, which currently requires independent testing alongside Glencore's own analysis to determine payment terms (24:18-24:54). Loyden agreed this was "a great idea" (24:54).

### Rare Earths in Tailings
Loyden noted that rare earths are found in tailings both in Western Australia and in the Queensland region, already in a semi-concentrated and relatively easy-to-access form, offering an environmental upside (24:56-25:22). However, he expressed strong skepticism that Australia would get involved in rare earths processing beyond concentrate stage, emphasizing the need for secure supply chain relationships for downstream work (25:22-25:42).

### Facility Delivery Model and Benchmarking
The Townsville pilot plant was delivered through a competitive tender process run by the department, with front-runners including CORE Resources and Sedgman (Sedgman ultimately won), involving both build and operate proposals (21:17-22:59). Shane indicated a similar approach may be taken for the Mt Isa centre (21:22-21:49). Loyden suggested CORE Resources as a valuable contact for Shane's consultation process (21:17).

### Timeline and Strategic Outlook
Loyden emphasized that "if you want to move this thing forward quickly," sovereign lab capability is essential, noting that "Europe should have at least started 10 years ago" to avoid their current predicament (20:26-20:38). He observed there is currently "a dearth of lab capabilities" in the region, with ALS no longer doing significant testwork locally -- everything gets sent south (20:38-20:53). His view is that with sufficient funding critical minerals projects will happen, but launching nascent industries that "no one's ever heard of before" remains challenging (17:03-17:17). The timing question remains open, but the strategic direction is increasingly clear (23:57-24:05).
