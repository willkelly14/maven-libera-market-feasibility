# Atiqullah Amiri - Copper Metallurgy & Testing Consultation - Summary and Insights

**Date:** 2025-11-19

---

## Summary and Insights
**Project Background and Evolution**
Shane provided background on the Mt Isa Research Centre project, explaining that it originated from Mount Isa City Council's response to Glencore's closure, with the Sustainable Minerals Institute (SMI/UQ) initially conducting a scoping study focused on a rare earths Research Centre (2:07-2:51). Following successful demand assessment, the project has progressed to a more detailed feasibility with much deeper consultation (2:51-3:20). Shane noted that over the past six months, global critical minerals supply security concerns have generated significantly more interest and broadened the Centre's scope (3:20-3:41). The Centre will now be a commercial capabilities lab with a focus on critical minerals and rare earths while also supporting copper and other base metal explorers and active miners with exploration programs in the region (3:41-4:15). A learning centre component will enable skills training and professional development locally rather than requiring personnel to travel away (4:15-4:30). The core objective is building sovereign capability in critical minerals research at a local Mount Isa level (4:30-4:40).

**Complex Metallurgical Testing Challenges**
Shane emphasized that the Centre is not intended to be a high-volume assay lab but rather to address complex metallurgy challenges currently handled overseas or at significant distance, with samples being sent to Chile, China, Sweden, Germany, and the US (4:40-5:58). Atiq confirmed that Austral Resources has sent a fair amount of sample material to Canada for mineralogical assessment (6:39-6:53). This testing assessed copper mineralogy across different sections of the mine, which dictates recoveries during concentrate processing and determines penalty elements or other unwanted elements in the copper concentrate based on the initial head mineralogy (6:53-7:54). Atiq indicated this work was done overseas because no equivalent capability existed in Australia; if it had been available locally, they would have used it to reduce logistics and transport costs (7:54-8:19).

**Copper Speciation and Deposit Complexity**
Austral's deposit comprises three distinct copper speciations: significant oxide mineralization, a very small transition zone, and massive sulphides underneath (8:22-9:01). Copper speciation and mineralogical testing throughout the mine is critical for determining final concentrate grade, recovery rates, and related parameters (9:01). Shane noted that discussions with other copper explorers suggest an area where the Research Centre could provide value through pure research into extraction methods, particularly reducing acid consumption in copper oxides and developing more cost-efficient extraction approaches (9:01-9:28).

**Current Metallurgical Challenges: Penalty Elements and Credits**
Atiq described one of Austral's current-stage challenges: identifying methods to minimize bismuth, uranium, and thorium in the concentrate while simultaneously maximizing gold and silver recovery (9:49-10:34). He emphasized that having very quick turnaround capability to run test batches on different sample types from different parts of the deposit would be extremely valuable (10:34-10:49). Atiq mentioned that Sedgman (Core Sedgman) has built algorithms for recovery calculations, though he expressed uncertainty about their accuracy (10:49).

**Bulk Sampling and On-Site Laboratory Considerations**
Discussion turned to bulk sampling and pilot-scale testing between exploration and production phases (10:49-11:14). Atiq explained that Austral has discussed establishing an on-site lab to conduct testing at every stage of the process—from the mine site (pit or underground) through stockpile management, including extensive blending because of grade and lead content variability—to ensure the concentrate matches intended specifications for sale (11:14-12:03). While there has been internal discussion about building on-site lab capabilities, Atiq was unsure of feasibility, cost, or the current status of that process (12:03-12:17).

**Research Centre Operating Model for Bulk Work**
Shane outlined the feasibility-stage thinking for the Centre: companies would have the opportunity to rent space or rent personnel to conduct testing at scale (12:17-12:45). He emphasized that bulk sample work could be done in Mount Isa because sending bulk samples overseas or interstate is very expensive (12:45-13:01). Examples include leach testing (heap leach) and beneficiation for commodities like vanadium, where concentrates could be produced locally and then transported to facilities like Townsville's pilot plant, avoiding the need to transport all ore and then dispose of waste at an industrial estate (13:01-13:20).

**Current Sample Distribution and Lab Turnaround**
Austral has conducted heap leaching tests in Brisbane, though Atiq noted the work took considerable time (13:20-13:52). Normal exploration samples are sent all over the country—to Perth and Intertek (confirmed as the primary provider alongside ALS)—because local capacity is insufficient (13:52-14:04).

**Commercial Lab Satellite Model**
Shane described the evolving concept for the Centre: it needs to be large enough to potentially become a purely commercial facility over time, with scale sufficient to host a commercial-scale assay lab (14:04-14:47). For research purposes, only a small assay capability would be needed to avoid delays, addressing a problem JK Tech in Brisbane experiences where small research sample batches get deprioritized by commercial labs like ALS that have thousands of samples backed up, causing significant research delays (14:47-15:32). Shane used a GP clinic/pathology analogy: just as a medical pathology service operates within a GP building, a commercial operator like ALS could establish dedicated capability within the Centre's lab space, using it to set up regional operations and bringing personnel in seasonally to conduct assay work locally rather than shipping all samples away, potentially saving explorers weeks of turnaround time (15:32-16:30).

**Input from Feasibility Studies and Scoping Requirements**
Atiq and Russell (Dwyer) offered to provide input from Austral's feasibility studies to help scope expected sampling and testing requirements across the region, recognizing that Shane needs to document anticipated demand on paper (16:42-17:06). Shane confirmed he will compile a list of required Centre capabilities, after which a technical expert will specify necessary equipment (beneficiation gear, crushing, grinding, lab equipment) and develop a capital cost estimate running into the millions of dollars (17:06-17:54). Shane framed this as building sovereign capability to reduce reliance on other countries for project advancement, expecting industry support for the concept (17:54).

**Basic Infrastructure Needs: Sample Preparation**
Atiq emphasized that even basic infrastructure such as a regional crushing facility and sample preparation capability would be extremely helpful (17:54-18:12).

**Comminution Testing Capabilities**
Shane proposed including drop-weight testing and a comminution area for grind tests and hardness testing, specifically referencing bond index testing for hardness and noting that bankable feasibility studies require drop-weight testing, bond index testing, and similar parameters (18:12-19:40). Atiq confirmed that Austral has already completed extensive comminution tests and composite sample programs (not bulk samples for leaching, but numerous composite samples for various tests) (19:40-20:00).

**Exploration Budget and Final Investment Decision Dynamics**
When asked about plans for heavier exploration in the coming year after a relatively quiet current year, Atiq explained the dynamic is complex: if the final investment decision (FID) occurs in the next year, the exploration budget will shrink as resources shift toward construction, contingent on raising the necessary capital; however, if the FID is delayed further, exploration expenditure will continue (20:23-21:05). Austral is primarily copper-focused with gold and silver as by-products, making the current commodity environment favorable (21:05-21:28).

**Timing, Funding, and Sovereign Capability Imperative**
Shane expressed confidence that if the feasibility holds up, the timing is very favorable for the Centre to secure funding, noting strong support at the regional, state, and federal levels (21:28-21:59). He emphasized that any initiative building sovereign capability would be difficult for governments to reject (21:59-22:02). Atiq directed Shane to follow up with Russell Dwyer, noting that as the engineer responsible for that part of the business, Russell would have deeper knowledge and be able to provide more detailed assistance than Atiq could offer (22:02-22:22).
