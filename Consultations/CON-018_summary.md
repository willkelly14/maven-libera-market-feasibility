# Nick Algie (ASQ) - Quartz Projects & Metallurgical Testing - Summary and Insights

**Date:** 2025-11-20

---

**Project Context & ASQ's Quartz Focus**
Nick Algie confirmed ASQ's primary focus on high-purity quartz and silica sand projects, with operations in both Western Australia and Queensland **(0:48–1:57)**. The discussion centered on understanding ASQ's metallurgical testing challenges and whether the proposed Mt Isa Research Centre could support their testwork requirements, particularly for complex metallurgical programs currently being sent interstate or overseas **(3:00–4:27)**.
**Quinbrook Polysilicon Project & Specification Challenges**
ASQ had been working toward supplying feedstock from Quartz Hill (Mount Surprise) to Quinbrook's proposed polysilicon plant in Townsville. However, the project faced significant challenges when Quinbrook brought on new German consultants who dramatically tightened the chemical composition specifications for the quartz feedstock **(5:41–6:51)**. The new specifications required extremely clean quartz (no scope for chemical treatment like acid leaching) at very large volumes (350,000–500,000 tonnes per year for a 20+ year operation), making it difficult to find deposits of sufficient size and purity **(6:51–8:10)**. ASQ defined a resource but couldn't quite meet the elevated specifications, and Quinbrook has since moved on to fund exploration work with another company near Mount Isa **(9:44–11:00)**.
**Metallurgical Testing Challenges: Contamination in Quartz Processing**
Nick highlighted the extreme contamination challenges inherent in quartz testwork. Quartz is highly abrasive, and any contact with drilling equipment, crushing gear, or grinding media introduces metal contamination through filings and smearing **(8:10–8:15)**. For the Quinbrook resource definition, ASQ used RC drilling with screening, then crushed samples using tungsten carbide equipment at SGS Townsville to minimize contamination. Diamond drilling core was subsequently sent to Sydney and then Germany for optical sorting trials **(8:15–9:22)**.
Optical sorting using Tomra technology proved effective at beneficiating the ore—these machines can process 100 tonnes per hour, photographing golf-ball-sized lumps in real time and using high-pressure air jets to separate material **(9:37–10:20)**. Despite this success, the final product still fell short of Quinbrook's revised specifications **(9:44–10:20)**.
**Polysilicon Production Process (Arc Furnace to Solar Wafers)**
Nick explained the full polysilicon production chain. Metallurgical-grade quartz is fed into large arc furnaces along with a high-carbon reductant (coal, biochar, or other carbon sources). The furnaces are industrial-scale, fed by front-end loaders, and tap molten silicon metal at the bottom—transforming white quartz rocks into metal **(11:00–12:00)**. This silicon is then further refined into monocrystalline silicon ingots, which are sliced into wafers for solar panel production **(21:29–22:21)**.
**High-Purity Quartz R&D Program**
ASQ has been running a multi-year R&D program (4–5 years) targeting ultra-high-purity quartz suitable for crucibles used in monocrystalline silicon production **(12:20–13:00)**. Specifications for crucible-grade quartz are extremely stringent. The R&D involves:
- **Electronic fragmentation** of rock rather than mechanical crushing, using an arc discharge in a vessel to break material at grain boundaries, which helps liberate contaminants **(13:00–14:00)**
- **Calcination** to change the phase of quartz, making it more amenable to impurity removal **(14:00–14:30)**
- **Hot chlorination** using chlorine gas, along with extensive acid treatment and salt washes **(12:20–13:00)**
ASQ has made enough progress to continue investing, but the work is extremely challenging due to limited global expertise and the ease of lab-introduced contamination **(14:28–15:04)**.
**Need for Dedicated HPQ Facilities & Hot Chlorination Capability**
Nick emphasized that ultra-high-purity quartz work requires almost sealed-room conditions to prevent contamination **(15:04–15:12)**. Hot chlorination is likely unavoidable for true HPQ production, but there is currently **no facility in Australia** capable of performing this testwork **(15:40–15:42)**. The process involves chlorine gas in enclosed spaces, making it hazardous **(15:42)**. Critical Minerals Queensland had discussions about establishing such a facility a couple of years ago, but Nick was unsure of progress **(15:42–16:09)**.
ASQ's testwork has been conducted with Anzitlan, Dorfner (Germany), Chinese labs, and currently a Perth-based lab **(16:13–16:32)**. Building local HPQ capabilities would be valuable but requires dedicated equipment and contamination-free processing lines **(16:32–17:02)**.
**Assay & Pulverization Challenges**
Assaying HPQ material presents a catch-22: either take very small samples for complete digestion (unrepresentative), or pulverize larger samples (which introduces contamination) **(17:02–17:30)**. Even specialized milling media cause problems:
- **Agate mills** (~95% silica, 5% aluminum) introduce aluminum contamination, a key impurity to eliminate **(17:30–18:00)**
- **Tungsten carbide** theoretically should work but introduces iron contamination **(18:00–18:16)**
- **Zirconia ball and puck** limits sample size significantly **(18:00–18:16)**
For sand projects ASQ uses zirconia; for HPQ work they've avoided pulverizing entirely, relying on full-digest assays **(18:00–18:30)**.
**Global HPQ Supply Chain Vulnerability**
Nick revealed that **Sibelco in North Carolina is the only producer globally** of ultra-high-purity quartz, operating under highly protected, clandestine processes **(18:29–19:09)**. When a hurricane shut down their operations for 2–3 months six months prior, ASQ received numerous panic calls from companies suddenly realizing their supply chain was extremely vulnerable **(19:09)**. While there are rumors of Russian and Norwegian production, Sibelco remains the dominant (essentially sole) commercial source **(19:53–20:00)**.
The strategic importance is immense: **every solar panel in the world** uses monocrystalline silicon drawn from HPQ crucibles. Each crucible requires ~250 kg of ultra-high-purity quartz, lasts only 2–3 weeks at ~1,800°C, and then must be replaced **(20:00–21:00)**. The purity of the crucible quartz directly affects crucible longevity—higher purity means less cracking/crazing, less contamination absorption, and reduced production downtime **(20:42–21:29)**. There are three grades of crucible quartz (inner, middle, outer) with progressively lower specs; the inner material commands **~$20,000 per tonne** **(22:00–22:21)**.
Sibelco's feedstock comes from the Spruce Pine pegmatite in North Carolina (a complex mixture of quartz, feldspar, and micas that requires separation), with preliminary processing on-site and finishing in Norway, likely due to cheap hydroelectric power **(22:48–23:09, 23:36)**. This represents the one critical material the U.S. supplies that China depends on **(22:21–22:33)**.

**Insights on Working with Metallurgical Labs**
Nick provided candid feedback on lab selection based on ASQ's experience with multiple providers **(25:41–26:51)**:
**Worst-case scenario:** Labs that claim they can do everything, provide lump-sum quotes, operate as a "black box" with no client access to processes or staff, and return flimsy reports with no transparency about what happened to samples **(25:41–26:40)**.
**Preferred approach:** Labs that proactively communicate whenever questions arise, allow client involvement throughout the process (including on-site visits when possible), and maintain open dialogue. ASQ finds large companies like SGS very capable but too difficult to access the right technical staff **(26:40–26:51)**.
This feedback underscores the importance of transparency, accessibility, and collaborative problem-solving in metallurgical testwork services—key design principles for the Mt Isa Research Centre.
**Mt Isa Research Centre Opportunities**
Several potential capabilities were discussed that could serve ASQ and similar companies:
1. **Quartz bagging facility** in Mount Isa for washing and bagging lump quartz (50–80 kg bags or 1–2 tonne bulk bags) to serve multiple companies, mentioned in context of Quinbrook's feedstock needs **(28:24–29:40)**
2. **Dedicated quartz research line** with contamination controls, pending decisions about scope and capability **(16:32–17:02, 27:50–28:04)**
3. **Core sample storage** as a practical revenue-generating service **(29:40–30:30)**
4. **Research into improved extraction methods** (less acid usage, pressure oxidation, etc.) to improve project viability **(29:40–30:30)**
5. **Skills training and professional development** for local workforce, though less relevant to ASQ given their distance from Mount Isa **(24:35–25:02)**
The Centre concept envisions a hybrid model combining commercial activities (bulk sample beneficiation, routine testwork) with pure research capabilities, potentially through university-industry collaboration **(27:50–28:04)**. The facility would be sized with room to grow rather than being constrained from the start **(28:04)**.
