## Summary and Insights
**Overseas Metallurgical Testing: BV Canada Program**
Russell Dwyer described KGL's experience sending samples to Bureau Veritas in Vancouver, Canada for specialized mineralogical assessment. **(1:53-2:03)** The primary test work was flotation testing conducted by Core Resources in Albion, Brisbane, but the specialized ChemScan and X-ray diffraction work had to be done overseas. **(2:03-3:03)** Dave Way from Sedgman, who led KGL's test work program, specifically chose BV Vancouver due to both a particular expert there and the availability of specialized equipment not readily accessible in Australia. **(2:30-3:03)**
The testing focused on sulphur deportment, copper deportment, bismuth deportment, fluorine, and mineral liberation and association. **(4:25-4:53)** The ChemScan work was particularly valuable for understanding what minerals the copper was associated with. **(4:53-5:04)** The samples were air freighted rather than sea freighted because they were reasonably small, **(5:29-5:33)** and the total cost of the test work was approximately $50,000. **(5:39-5:56)**
An unexpected benefit was that BV recommended a primary grind size (P80 of 150 microns rather than the 120 microns KGL was using), representing nearly 20% less cost since grinding becomes exponentially more expensive as particle size decreases. **(6:13-7:30)** Russell emphasized this was very valuable information given that the comminution circuit is usually the limiting factor in a process plant and a major energy consumer. **(7:00-7:17)**
Regarding logistics, Core Resources in Brisbane packaged everything, making the process relatively hands-off for KGL. **(3:44-3:58)** However, there was significant focus on ensuring all necessary samples were included in the program because organizing and shipping to Canada was quite a lengthy process and considerable effort. **(3:58-4:25)**
**Intertek Alice Springs Closure Impact**
Russell discussed a significant operational challenge when Intertek closed their Alice Springs laboratory. **(8:06-8:12)** Intertek had been a long-term test partner who looked after KGL very well, but they suffered from a lack of staff in Alice Springs and couldn't maintain their laboratory despite it being profitable. **(8:19-9:00)** The issue was specifically a staffing problem rather than profitability. **(9:00-9:05)**
Prior to the closure, KGL's workflow was efficient: they would take pallets of core into Alice Springs, where Intertek would pulp the material, and then send pulps either to Darwin or Townsville depending on workload. **(9:05-9:25)** Intertek had crushers and pulverisers in Alice Springs to produce pulps, which could then be air freighted quickly. **(9:25-9:40)** KGL would cut and quarter the core at site, bring bags of quartered core into Alice Springs for pulping, and then ship to the final assay lab. **(9:40-9:55)**
After the closure, KGL had to ship pallets of quartered core directly to Darwin for pulverising, representing significant additional cost. **(9:55-10:10)** Russell emphasized that if they could access a closer lab, they would prefer that option. **(10:05-10:10)**
**Proposed Research Centre: Sample Prep and Commercial Assay Lab Model**
Shane outlined a concept for the research centre that addresses the sample preparation gap. **(10:10-10:40)** His ideal scenario involves having a small assay lab on-site so research work can be tested immediately with next-day results rather than sending samples off-site. **(10:30-10:40)** Russell agreed this would be much more efficient. **(10:37-10:40)**
Shane's approach is to build a commercial-size footprint for an assay lab, using a medical pathology clinic analogy: just as a GP clinic might have a pathologist in the corner, the centre could host a commercial assay lab operated by established providers like ALS or Core Resources. **(10:40-11:16)** This would be complementary rather than competitive with existing labs. **(11:16-11:19)**
The model would allow the centre to do sample preparation, with a commercial operator running the actual assay lab out of the facility. **(11:19-11:45)** Shane emphasized they don't want to compete with commercial labs, but this could provide an opportunity for assay and prep work to be done in the region. **(11:19-11:45)** The centre could potentially generate revenue through this arrangement, **(11:45-11:50)** and commercial operators would likely be happy to have someone else handle the comminution and sample prep. **(11:50-11:53)**
Russell confirmed a one-stop shop would be better for KGL, particularly since they already have concentrate trucks traveling from the mine to a port, providing opportunity to transport laboratory samples off-site easily. **(11:53-12:05)** However, establishing an on-site laboratory with providers like BV or Intertek operating 24/7 represents significant cost - approximately $600-1000 per day just for staff accommodation on remote sites. **(12:05-12:40)** KGL would prefer to give work to a local business rather than maintain an on-site lab, though at present it doesn't seem quite feasible and having one on-site is easier. **(12:40-13:05)** Russell expressed willingness to revisit this approach. **(13:00-13:05)**
Shane noted the model isn't a pure common use facility like Townsville's, but would allow space for companies to embed their own team members to work on their samples in the lab. **(13:05-13:39)** Russell highlighted the logic: why spend significant capital on something only needed for short periods, which then becomes a sunk cost when finished. **(13:39-13:57)**
**Core Storage Considerations**
Shane raised the question of how explorers store cores and whether a centralized storage facility would make sense from a cost perspective. **(13:58-14:12)** He noted there are chain of custody requirements and asked whether KGL would be interested if such a facility existed. **(14:12-14:52)**
Russell explained KGL stores cores in an organized way on pallets on-site, maintaining a physical core library. **(14:52-15:10)** For core that's quartered and sent for testing, the residual material (pulps) is generally returned to them and kept in either drums or IBCs at site. **(15:10-15:27)** Shane confirmed they get all the sample bags back. **(15:27-15:31)**
KGL pays the penalty for transporting samples back because it's cheaper in the long run to keep them on-site in a couple of 40-foot containers, allowing them to re-assay quite readily if needed. **(15:31-16:00)** Russell noted that other companies he's worked for handle it differently, leaving material with the lab where it becomes a long-term rental cost. **(16:00-16:07)** Shane observed that companies are already paying rental costs for some storage, so there's existing demand. **(16:07-16:20)** He noted centralized storage would be implemented only if there's sufficient demand. **(16:20-16:26)**
Russell made an important point about early-stage exploration: some sites don't have long-term storage available because exploration teams arrive, do their campaign, send samples for test work, and then pack up and leave entirely - no sheds, no roads, nothing. **(16:26-16:46)** Shane recognized this means centralized storage would likely be most appealing for early-stage exploration. **(16:46-17:00)**
Regarding core retention practices, Shane asked whether KGL retains entire drill holes. **(17:01-17:13)** Russell confirmed they do, though not everyone does, and it's been an internal debate. **(17:13-17:30)** He emphasized that their core library is the most valuable thing they have - it's the entire basis of roughly $100 million in drilling investment. **(17:30-17:43)**
Shane noted he'd learned from reading about state core libraries that if a company relinquishes a deposit or lease, they must give their cores to the government. **(17:43-17:58)** Russell confirmed this, noting it's very valuable information for the government who value it highly. **(17:58-18:05)** Shane observed that given current knowledge about critical minerals and rare earths, some very valuable leases were probably relinquished in the past. **(18:05-18:18)**
**Pyrite Roaster Discussion and Precinct Concept**
Shane mentioned discussions with Aeon Metals about historical consideration of putting a pyrite roaster in Mount Isa. **(18:38-18:54)** Russell indicated awareness of pyrite roasters but no specific knowledge of those discussions. **(18:54-18:58)** Shane explained the context: Glencore (previously Incitec) is the main sulphuric acid supplier in the region, and there's substantial pyrite available, particularly from Aeon. **(18:58-19:30)** If there's demand for acid, the pyrite resource is available. **(19:30-19:38)**
Shane explained his vision of having a large land area that could develop into a precinct over time as demand emerges. **(19:38-19:45)** This could include activities like quartz bagging (bringing in quartz, sizing it appropriately, and bagging for processing) or potentially a pyrite roast facility. **(19:45-20:00)** Russell agreed this was interesting. **(20:00)**
**Bulk Sampling Focus and Capabilities**
Shane emphasized the centre should focus on bulk sample work, which cannot be done at facilities like Core Resources or in industrial lab areas. **(20:00-20:30)** He envisions the centre having bulk sampling capability, with concentrates then going to pilot facilities elsewhere, or allowing space for companies to bring in their own short-term pilot programs. **(20:30-20:55)** Companies could bring their own pilot plant, set it up, run bulk samples through it for three months, then dismantle and remove it when finished. **(20:55-21:11)**
**Heap Leaching Requirements**
Russell raised the specific need for leaching test work programs. **(21:11-21:17)** He explained KGL requires several different types of leaching processes, with one being full-scale NAF (Non-Acid Forming) and PAF (Potentially Acid Forming) testing to understand how waste rock will behave long-term. **(21:17-21:40)** This is key for all mine sites - understanding the long-term kinetics of waste dumps and tailings storage facilities and how they produce different metals over time. **(21:40-21:55)**
Additionally, as copper and gold prices rise, lower-grade deposits amenable to leaching all need test work. **(21:55-22:10)** While lab-based leach testing is useful, scale is important to understand how material actually behaves under real leaching conditions. **(22:10-22:30)** Russell emphasized there's significant opportunity in this area. **(22:30-22:54)**
Shane then shared his concept drawings, which already included heap leaching areas. **(23:02-23:05)** The plan shows bulk sample beneficiation tied to Townsville operations for vanadium processing. **(23:05-23:42)** Shane explained much of the vanadium is at Julia Creek, and rather than transporting all feed material to Townsville where Veolia must be paid to dispose of waste, bulk material would be beneficiated at Mount Isa with only concentrates going to Townsville. **(23:42-23:56)** Russell agreed this would be much more efficient. **(23:56-23:58)**
The design allows for a tailings dam and heap leach area that could accommodate six or seven simultaneous programs, each with their own sumps. **(23:58-24:38)** Shane noted the concept is evolving based on demand, with bulk sampling and tailings reprocessing clearly emerging as priorities. **(24:38-24:55)** This opens opportunities beyond small-scale testing - successful small-scale results can progress to bulk trials to see actual outcomes. **(24:55-25:00)**
**On-Site Accommodation and Learning Centre**
Shane's plan includes on-site accommodation for both staff and visitors to avoid needing to travel into town, helping keep costs down. **(24:55-25:30)** The design also incorporates a learning centre to bring skills training to Mount Isa rather than people having to travel away for training. **(25:30-25:40)**
The learning centre could serve multiple purposes: if a company like KGL brings investors for a site visit, there's currently nowhere on remote sites for proper presentations. **(25:40-25:49)** The facility would allow site trips followed by meetings, presentations, or even board meetings at the centre - a multi-purpose space. **(25:49-25:55)** Russell confirmed the value of such facilities. **(25:49-25:51)**
Shane noted he's traveling to Mount Isa on Monday and Tuesday to look at potential sites, **(25:51-26:05)** with a call scheduled afterward with an infrastructure builder to understand construction costs in the area. **(26:05-26:18)** He emphasized the facilities are essentially sheds rather than elaborate buildings. **(26:18-26:26)**
**Facility Design Philosophy and Competitive Positioning**
Russell emphasized the facilities should be agricultural-style setups rather than high-comfort facilities. **(26:26-26:40)** The key requirement is tightness in material control, procedural rigor, and methodology - particularly separation of liquor and ensuring JORC chain of custody is maintained. **(26:40-27:00)** He noted many mining companies already have worse conditions on actual sites, and the focus should be maximizing shareholder value through test work quality, not personal comfort. **(27:00-27:20)**
Shane agreed completely, emphasizing that success still requires good people (researchers, metallurgists), good quality assurance and control, and appropriate pricing. **(27:20-27:48)** He argued that with good people, good processes, good plant and equipment, and good facilities, there's already a place-based competitive advantage. **(27:48-28:05)** The goal is to build skills capability in the region so work doesn't need to go to Canada, Germany, or China, keeping expertise and employment local. **(28:05-28:23)** Russell strongly agreed, noting nobody sends work overseas by choice. **(28:23-28:28)**
