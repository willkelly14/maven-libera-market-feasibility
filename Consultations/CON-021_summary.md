# Paladin Energy - Tim Rose (MICC CEO), Mayor Peter McRae, Anthony, Alistair - Summary and Insights

**Date:** 2025-11-24

---

**Paladin's Current Strategic Focus and Australian Position**
Paladin Energy's current corporate priorities are the Langer Heinrich mine ramp-up in Namibia (nearing completion) and the PLS mine development in Saskatchewan, which represents a five to six year horizon and will consume significant corporate resources. **(4:00-5:00)** Due to the effective moratorium on uranium mining in Australia, the company has deliberately limited its focus on Australian assets, with Anthony noting "for want of a better description, what's the point?" **(4:40)** However, since joining the company approximately 18 months ago, Anthony has reactivated Paladin's engagement with industry advocacy groups including the Minerals Council, Chamber of Minerals, and AMEC. **(5:30-6:00)**
**Advocacy Commitment and Constraints**
Paladin is willing to participate in advocacy efforts to lift the uranium mining moratorium but cannot make firm commitments to development timelines. **(6:00-6:30)** The company's approach is "we will assist but we can't make a commitment" because they must complete the Canadian mine first before considering new Australian projects. **(6:20)** Paladin's value in advocacy comes from two areas: holding significant ground like Mount Isa, and being one of few uranium producers globally who can provide evidence that uranium mining "can be done, can be done safely, can be done for long periods of time, does employ people, it does make an economic contribution." **(7:00-7:30)**
**Current Mount Isa Activity**
Limited work is underway at Mount Isa. A minor mineralogy study commenced or will soon commence in the current financial year to re-examine the deposit's mineralogy and improve understanding of mineral extraction methods. **(7:30-8:50)** The work is being conducted by a laboratory (either Queensland or Western Australia-based), and Anthony characterized it as "not a big piece of work" but rather "refreshing ourselves on the data" given that drilling occurred many years ago and no current company personnel have familiarity with the resource. **(8:50-9:20)** The deposit has been extensively drilled and the resource is well understood, but the historical work means institutional knowledge has been lost. **(9:10)**
**Saskatchewan Mine Economics**
The PLS mine in Saskatchewan provides a useful benchmark for potential Australian development. The publicly stated development cost is \$1.2 billion, though Paladin paid \$1 billion to acquire the companies owning the mine, bringing the real total to \$2.2 billion, with additional costs beyond that suggesting a realistic range of approximately \$1.5 billion for a similar greenfield project. **(15:00-16:00)** The mine will employ 600-700 workers at peak construction and 200-300 during operations. **(16:00)** By comparison, the Langer Heinrich mine in Namibia employs 400 Paladin staff plus 400-500 contractors. **(16:10-16:20)**
**Advocacy Network and Resources**
Anthony offered several avenues for advocacy collaboration. He suggested connecting with other uranium companies including Boss Energy (Honeymoon Mine, South Australia), Deep Yellow, Cameco (the world's largest uranium company with potential Western Australian projects), and Cauldron (Western Australian projects). **(18:00-19:00)** He committed to sharing Paladin's parliamentary submission to a Western Australian inquiry, which provides comprehensive market and demand data useful for advocacy, noting that while Deep Yellow and Cauldron's submissions focus on specific projects they want to develop, Paladin's submission "looks more at the market and the demand side" and includes supply-demand projections, product flows, and economic benefits data. **(19:00-20:50)**
**Royalty and Revenue Considerations**
Uranium royalty rates are believed to be approximately 5%, similar to rates in Western Australia and South Australia (which already produces uranium). **(21:30-22:20)** This compares to Queensland's 6% copper royalty (reduced to 5% with downstream processing like smelting). **(21:40)** Anthony noted he would confirm the exact rate. Tim Rose observed that Mount Isa already handles uranium as a byproduct in Glencore's copper smelter operations, where BHP copper contains uranium impurities that end up in tailings arrangements. **(22:35-23:00)**
**South Australian Government Support**
A significant advocacy opportunity exists through the South Australian Premier, who has indicated willingness to speak with and advocate to other state Premiers about uranium production benefits. **(23:00-23:30)** While he prefers not to take a public stage role, he is willing to work behind the scenes with Labor colleagues in other states, particularly Western Australia. **(23:30-23:50)** This is valuable given South Australia has been producing uranium for close to 40 years "without any particular drama." **(23:40)**
**Critical Minerals and Uranium Co-occurrence**
Shane Kelly noted that many critical minerals deposits in the region will have uranium present, meaning companies will be "dealing with uranium as a by-product in most cases." **(24:30-24:42)** Anthony agreed, highlighting "this sort of slightly mad situation where you may or probably will be getting a level of uranium out of the ground. You're just not allowed to mine it in a purebred way and you're not allowed to export it." **(24:42-25:00)**
**Development Timeline Realities**
Paladin consistently communicates to analysts and the public that it takes "10 plus years" to start a uranium mine. **(40:30-40:55)** Anthony emphasized that "even if everyone was in a great mood today, you're probably looking 10 years away," noting that while gold mines might be slightly quicker, Australia has less institutional knowledge of uranium development. **(40:55-41:15)** This timeline reality underscores why long-term planning is essential even if policy changes occur soon.
**Historical Context: 2013 Moratorium Lift**
When David Crisafulli's previous Liberal government lifted the moratorium around 2013, no companies proceeded with development—a fact that creates political hesitancy today. **(43:00-43:15)** However, Anthony provided crucial context: that period coincided with the post-Fukushima uranium price collapse, making development financially unviable regardless of regulatory approval. **(43:45-44:30)** The same pattern occurred in Western Australia, where four approvals were granted but "there was no financial road forward" due to the industry-wide contraction and pricing collapse. **(44:15-44:45)** Anthony emphasized this is "fixed in the collective memory" of both politicians (who see companies not proceeding) and companies (who see the period as when "the industry started winding back"). **(44:30-44:50)**
**Current Market Fundamentals**
The uranium market has transformed dramatically. Prices have risen from approximately \$50 per pound (or below) when Paladin was reconsidering Langer Heinrich several years ago to currently "pushing around \$80," with recent global leadership discussions projecting prices around \$100 per pound in the next year or two. **(45:10-46:05)** These projections are characterized as "in the more realistic zone" compared to "really silly predictions." **(46:00)**
The supply-demand imbalance is "getting so big and so long term" that by the mid-2030s the industry should be in "pretty robust condition." **(44:50-45:05)** Many reactors are being built globally with very little new supply coming online. **(46:10)** Paladin's Saskatchewan mine won't enter the market until the early 2030s, and another Australian company (NextGen, also in Saskatchewan) has similar timing. **(46:20-46:40)** The convergence is that demand will surge in the 2030s while new supply, though entering the market, "won't close the gap"—meaning a substantial supply deficit will persist through the end of the 2030s. **(46:40-46:55)**
Anthony's key submission point is that "if it takes 10 years to get a mine up and running, if you start in the next couple of years, you'll be delivering straight into this huge imbalance." **(44:55-45:05)**
**Climate Change as Fundamental Driver**
The biggest change since 2011-2013 is the "quantum shift" in global focus on climate change and decarbonization, which is driving nuclear reactor construction worldwide. **(49:30-50:00)** Anthony emphasized this is "really what's driving everything now" and "the view is that's not likely to go away in any foreseeable future. So that will underpin the demand for decades to come." **(49:55-50:10)** This represents the fundamental difference between the current opportunity and the failed 2013 moratorium lift.
**Mount Isa Context and Regional Diversification**
Tim Rose outlined Mount Isa's economic situation: the city population has declined from 28,000 in the 1970s-80s to 11,500, with Glencore's copper mine closure eliminating approximately 500 jobs from an initial estimate of 1,200. **(26:15-27:00)** Loss of the copper smelter would reduce population below 10,350 due to cascading effects (the smelter employs \~400, the sulfuric acid plant \~50, and Phosphate Hill mine \~650 jobs that depend on the sulfur supply). **(27:00-28:00)** The city serves as a district center for police and health services with approximately 4,000 Indigenous residents, making the mining employment base critical for attracting other professional workers. **(28:00-28:30)**
The Northwest Mineral Province contains approximately \$680 billion in resources between Mount Isa and Cloncurry. **(28:40)** The proposed research center represents part of a broader diversification strategy to reduce dependence on a single mining company's decisions, including projects around robotics, new technologies, battery anode materials from graphite, pyrite roasting for sulfuric acid, fertilizer plants, Green Gravity's shaft-based energy storage, and Flying Whales air transport. **(29:00-32:00)** The research center will focus on bulk sampling (expensive to transport away from Mount Isa) and research specific to local geology, with capabilities supporting the Queensland Common Use Facility's demonstration plant in Townsville. **(32:00-32:40)**
**Forum Participation Opportunity**
Tim Rose invited Paladin to participate in a forum with the U.S. Consulate in Mount Isa (March or May 2026) that will include major fund managers and Queensland Investment Corporation. **(48:00-48:10)** The role would be framed as explaining "what uranium production is all about" and discussing global operations and fundamentals, rather than making specific commitments. **(48:35-49:00)** Shane Kelly emphasized the value of explaining "when the approvals came through last time, that piece around that everything's got to line up for things to work" and how "the fundamentals have changed since 2013," characterizing this as "a pretty important message." **(49:10-49:30)**
