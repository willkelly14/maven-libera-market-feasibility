# Global Critical Minerals Demand Outlook

*Section 01: Macro Market Context — North West Mineral Province Feasibility Study*

---

## The Critical Minerals Imperative

The global energy transition is reshaping mineral demand at an unprecedented pace. According to the International Energy Agency's Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2025, demand for key energy transition minerals continued to grow strongly in 2024, with lithium demand rising by nearly 30% and demand for nickel, cobalt, graphite and rare earths increasing by 6-8%. [01-001] The energy sector accounted for 85% of total demand growth for battery metals over this period, driven by electric vehicles, battery storage, renewables and grid networks. [01-002]

Looking ahead, the scale of projected demand growth is striking. Under the IEA's Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS), lithium demand grows fivefold to 2040, graphite and nickel demand double, and cobalt and rare earth element demand increases by 50-60%. [01-005] Meeting this demand will require around USD 500 billion in new mining investment between now and 2040 -- yet investment momentum weakened in 2024, with real spending growth of just 2% after cost inflation. [01-006, 01-007]

The supply chain concentration challenge is equally concerning. The average market share of the top three refining nations rose from 82% in 2020 to 86% in 2024, with China the leading refiner for 19 of the 20 critical minerals analysed, holding an average 70% market share. [01-003, 01-004] Some 55% of critical minerals are now subject to export controls, and 15 of the minerals exhibit greater price volatility than oil. [01-008]

## Copper: The Backbone of Electrification

Copper is arguably the single most critical mineral for the energy transition and broader economic modernisation. Global copper demand reached 26,717 kilotonnes in 2024, of which 29% (7,737 kt) was driven by clean energy technologies. [01-009] The IEA projects demand will rise to 31,348 kt by 2030 and 34,137 kt by 2040 under STEPS. [01-009]

S&P Global's January 2026 study projects an even higher figure: 42 million tonnes by 2040, a 50% increase driven by AI infrastructure, electrification of transport and buildings, and defence modernisation. [01-011] The study identifies a potential supply gap exceeding 7 million metric tons by 2040. [01-011]

On the supply side, the outlook is sobering. The IEA warns that the current mine project pipeline points to a potential 30% supply shortfall by 2035, constrained by declining ore grades, rising capital costs, limited new discoveries and long project lead times. [01-010] Recycling could offset some of this gap, reducing the need for new mine development by 35% by 2050, but this still leaves a substantial deficit requiring new mine supply. [01-012]

## Zinc: Galvanizing the Energy Transition

Zinc is a foundational industrial metal whose demand is increasingly intertwined with the energy transition. Global zinc mine production is projected to reach 12.43 million tonnes in 2025 (up 4.3%) and 12.8 million tonnes in 2026 according to the ILZSG. [01-013] Galvanizing accounts for the dominant share of consumption, supporting construction, infrastructure and — increasingly — renewable energy installations where galvanized steel structures are essential for solar arrays and wind towers. [01-014]

Zinc demand from renewable energy technologies specifically is forecast to more than triple over the decade, growing from 109,300 metric tons in 2020 to 364,000 metric tons by 2030. [01-015] The market remained tight through 2025, with LME prices at USD 3,187/tonne and concentrate treatment charges recovering from record lows, reflecting persistent supply-side constraints. [01-016]

## Lead: Steady Demand Anchored in Energy Storage

Lead remains a critical metal for energy storage, with lead-acid batteries accounting for over 80% of global consumption. [01-018] Applications span automotive starting batteries, telecommunications backup power and off-grid energy storage — markets that remain large even as lithium-ion technology captures growth. [01-018]

The ILZSG reports global lead mine production of 4.54 million tonnes in 2024, with refined lead demand of 13.13 million tonnes. [01-017] The market is in modest surplus (63,000 tonnes in 2024, rising to 121,000 tonnes in 2025), but demand remains structurally supported by the automotive sector and emerging markets where lead-acid batteries remain the cost-effective storage solution. [01-017]

## Silver: Solar's Hidden Dependency

Silver occupies a unique position at the intersection of industrial demand and precious metal investment. Total demand reached 1.16 billion ounces in 2024, with industrial fabrication hitting a record 680.5 million ounces — up 4% year-on-year and the fourth consecutive annual record, driven by green economy applications. [01-019, 01-020]

Solar photovoltaics now accounts for more than 30% of industrial silver demand, making the metal a critical enabler of renewable energy deployment. [01-019] The market has been in structural deficit for four consecutive years, with the 2024 shortfall at 148.9 million ounces. [01-020] Looking to 2030, academic research suggests silver supply may meet only 62-70% of projected demand, with the solar industry consuming 29-41% of total supply. [01-021]

Industrial fabrication is forecast to surpass 700 million ounces for the first time in 2025, underscoring silver's growing role as an industrial commodity alongside its traditional monetary function. [01-022]

## Cobalt: Battery Metal Under Pressure

Cobalt is a critical input for lithium-ion battery cathodes and a strategic mineral for the energy transition. Global production surpassed 300 kilotonnes in 2024, a 30.7% increase from 2023, driven primarily by the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), which produced approximately 220,000 metric tons (roughly 70% of global output), and Indonesia. [01-023, 01-024]

The IEA projects cobalt demand to increase 50-60% by 2040 under its STEPS scenario, with the energy sector — particularly EV batteries — as the primary growth driver. [01-025] Recycling could eventually reduce the need for new mine development by over 20% by 2050, but significant new primary supply will still be required. [01-025]

The cobalt market faces two key structural challenges. First, extreme supply concentration: the DRC dominates mining while China controls nearly 80% of refining, creating a single-point-of-failure supply chain. [01-024] Second, the current market is in oversupply, with prices declining to around USD 30,000/tonne in 2024 and stockpiles building in DRC warehouses — discouraging the investment needed to meet long-term demand growth. [01-026]

## Vanadium: The Long-Duration Storage Opportunity

Vanadium occupies a dual role in the minerals landscape. Its traditional market is dominated by metallurgical applications — over 90% of consumption goes to high-strength steel alloys used in infrastructure, automotive and aerospace. [01-028] Global production is approximately 100,000 metric tonnes per year, heavily concentrated in China (70%), Russia (21%) and South Africa (8%). [01-027]

However, vanadium's emerging role in long-duration energy storage is attracting growing attention. Vanadium redox flow batteries (VRFBs) offer advantages over lithium-ion for grid-scale storage: longer cycle life, non-degrading electrolyte that can be recycled indefinitely, and suitability for 4-12 hour discharge durations needed for renewable energy integration. [01-029]

The VRFB market is estimated at USD 0.92 billion in 2025 and projected to reach USD 2.09 billion by 2030, growing at nearly 18% CAGR. [01-029] The vanadium segment holds a 61.5% share of the broader flow battery market. [01-030] A milestone was reached in December 2024 when Rongke Power energised the world's largest VRFB — a 175 MW/700 MWh system — in Wushi, China, demonstrating the technology's scalability. [01-030]

## Rare Earth Elements: Strategic Vulnerability

Rare earth elements (REEs) are indispensable for permanent magnets used in electric vehicle motors, wind turbine generators and defence applications. Global production reached 390,000 metric tons in 2024, but the supply chain presents the starkest concentration risk of any critical mineral group. [01-031]

China dominates at every level: approximately 69% of mining, 91% of separation and refining, and 94% of permanent magnet manufacturing — a share that has nearly doubled from roughly 50% two decades ago. [01-031, 01-032] The IEA projects rare earth demand to increase 50-60% by 2040, with China still supplying around 80% of refined material by 2035. [01-033] Among individual elements, neodymium accounts for 36% of demand, followed by cerium (31%), lanthanum (18%) and praseodymium (7%). [01-035]

The concentration risk became acutely real in 2025. In April, China introduced export controls on seven heavy rare earth elements, causing European prices to reach up to six times domestic Chinese levels. [01-034] Controls were expanded in October 2025 to cover five additional elements and processing equipment. [01-034] These actions have accelerated diversification efforts, with Australia emerging as a key alternative supplier, but meaningful supply chain rebalancing will take years.

## Molybdenum: The Steel Backbone

Molybdenum is a speciality metal whose primary market is as an alloying agent for high-strength steel. The global market was 319,700 metric tonnes in 2024 and is projected to reach 397,200 tonnes by 2030, growing at 3.7% CAGR. [01-036] Steel applications dominate consumption, expected to reach 271,600 tonnes by 2030. [01-036]

Beyond steel, molybdenum's exceptional heat resistance and corrosion-proof properties make it indispensable in energy infrastructure — pipelines, drilling equipment and nuclear reactors. [01-037] The IEA notes that geothermal power, despite representing less than 1% of low-carbon power capacity additions, accounts for nearly half of total molybdenum demand from the power sector. [01-038]

China is the largest and fastest-growing consumer, forecast to reach 176,500 tonnes by 2030 (4.8% CAGR), driven by infrastructure investment and industrial modernisation. [01-036] Supply deficits and volatile pricing have characterised the market in recent years.

## Phosphate: Feeding the World and Powering Batteries

Phosphate sits at the intersection of two megatrends: global food security and the battery revolution. Global phosphate rock production totalled 240 million metric tons in 2024, with China (110 million tonnes) as the dominant producer. [01-039] Agriculture remains by far the largest end-use, representing 95% of total phosphate demand, driven by the need to feed a global population expected to reach 9.7 billion by 2050. [01-042]

The IFA projects world phosphate fertiliser demand to increase to 63-72 Mt P2O5 by 2050, with total rock production requirements of 78-100 Mt P2O5. [01-041] Supply is concentrated: China, Morocco, the United States and Russia collectively account for nearly 80% of global phosphate fertiliser production. [01-039]

A new dimension of phosphate demand has emerged with the rapid adoption of LFP (lithium iron phosphate) batteries, which now dominate EV battery chemistry in China. The IEA highlights that China produces 75% of the world's purified phosphoric acid — the key input for LFP cathode material — creating a strategic dependency that extends phosphate's importance well beyond agriculture. [01-040]

## Gold: Safe Haven Meets Record Demand

Gold's inclusion in the NWMP mineral portfolio reflects its role as a store of value and its enduring demand from central banks, investors and the technology sector. Total gold demand exceeded 5,000 tonnes for the first time in 2025, yielding an unprecedented value of USD 555 billion (up 45% year-on-year). [01-043] The LBMA gold price set 53 new all-time highs during the year. [01-043]

Central bank purchases remained historically elevated at 863 tonnes in 2025, led by Poland (102 t), Kazakhstan (57 t) and Brazil (43 t). [01-044] A remarkable 95% of central banks surveyed by the World Gold Council expect gold reserves to increase, reflecting strategic de-dollarisation and geopolitical hedging. [01-044]

Investment demand surged, with global gold ETF holdings growing by 801 tonnes (the second strongest year on record) and bar and coin demand reaching a 12-year high. [01-045] Technology demand — spanning electronics, medical devices and AI infrastructure — remained stable at 323 tonnes, providing a steady industrial floor beneath the investment-driven price. [01-045]

## Implications for the North West Mineral Province

The demand outlook for every mineral in the NWMP portfolio is structurally positive. Copper faces the most acute supply deficit, with projected shortfalls of 30% by 2035 (IEA) and a gap exceeding 7 million tonnes by 2040 (S&P Global). [01-010, 01-011] Zinc demand from renewable energy is tripling this decade. [01-015] Silver is in persistent structural deficit driven by solar PV. [01-020, 01-021] Cobalt and rare earth element demand is set to grow 50-60% by 2040. [01-025, 01-033] Vanadium's emerging role in long-duration energy storage adds a new dimension to traditional steel demand. [01-029] Phosphate benefits from both food security imperatives and the LFP battery revolution. [01-040, 01-042] Gold continues to attract record investment and central bank purchasing. [01-043, 01-044]

At the same time, supply chain concentration — with China dominating refining for 19 of 20 critical minerals — creates both risk and opportunity. [01-004] Western governments and their allies are actively seeking to diversify supply chains, creating a strategic premium for mineral production in stable, allied jurisdictions like Australia. The NWMP, with its diverse endowment of precisely the minerals the world increasingly needs, is well positioned to benefit from this structural shift.
