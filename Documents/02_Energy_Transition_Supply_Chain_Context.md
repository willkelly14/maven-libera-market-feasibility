# Energy Transition and Supply Chain Context

The global energy transition is generating an unprecedented structural shift in demand for critical minerals. As the world electrifies its transport fleet, expands renewable energy generation, and modernises its grid infrastructure, the physical requirements for minerals such as lithium, cobalt, copper, rare earths, vanadium, and nickel are scaling at rates that have no historical precedent. This document examines the key demand pathways, the geopolitical risks inherent in today's concentrated supply chains, and the policy responses that are creating opportunities for mineral-rich nations with sovereign processing capability.

## Battery Storage and Electric Vehicles

The electrification of transport represents the single largest demand pathway for critical minerals in the energy transition. A typical electric car requires six times the mineral inputs of a conventional car [01-081], creating an unprecedented structural shift in commodity demand as the global vehicle fleet transitions from internal combustion to electric drivetrains.

### The Scale of EV Adoption

Global electric car sales exceeded 17 million in 2024, reaching a market share of more than 20% [01-082]. China has established a commanding lead, with electric cars accounting for almost half of all car sales in 2024 and over 11 million units sold — more than total global EV sales just two years earlier [01-083]. Looking ahead, the IEA projects that global EV sales will exceed 20 million in 2025, and the EV share of total car sales is expected to surpass 40% by 2030 under current policy settings [01-084].

### Mineral Demand Acceleration

This rapid EV deployment is translating directly into mineral demand. Lithium demand rose by nearly 30% in 2024, significantly exceeding the 10% annual growth rate seen in the 2010s, and is projected to grow fivefold by 2040 under the IEA's Stated Policies Scenario [01-085]. Demand for nickel, cobalt, graphite and rare earths increased by 6-8% in 2024, with the energy sector accounting for 85% of total demand growth for battery metals [01-086].

Total energy sector battery demand reached 1 TWh in 2024, with EV battery demand exceeding 950 GWh — 25% more than in 2023 — and projected to surpass 3 TWh by 2030 [01-087]. The shift toward lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistries is reshaping the mineral demand mix: LFP batteries made up nearly half of the global EV battery market in 2024 and are almost 30% cheaper per kWh than nickel-cobalt-manganese alternatives [01-088].

### Cobalt and the Chemistry Shift

Cobalt demand is projected to increase 50-60% by 2040 under STEPS [01-089], though the accelerating adoption of cobalt-free LFP chemistries is moderating growth compared to earlier forecasts. This chemistry shift has significant implications for supply chain risk — reducing dependence on the DRC's concentrated cobalt supply while increasing dependence on lithium and phosphate.

### The Overall Demand Picture

Overall mineral demand for clean energy technologies is projected to double between now and 2030 under the STEPS scenario and almost triple by 2030 and quadruple by 2040 under the Net Zero Emissions scenario [01-090]. This demand growth is not a forecast of what might happen — it reflects the physical mineral requirements of technologies that are already being deployed at scale and accelerating.

## Grid Electrification and Renewable Energy Infrastructure

The second major demand pathway for critical minerals runs through the unprecedented global build-out of renewable energy generation and the grid infrastructure required to deliver it. This expansion is creating sustained, long-term demand for copper, rare earth elements, zinc, and other minerals across the electricity value chain.

### The Renewable Energy Surge

Global investment in clean energy reached USD 2.2 trillion in 2025, twice the USD 1.1 trillion directed to fossil fuels [01-091]. This investment is delivering results at record scale: global renewable energy capacity grew by 585 GW in 2024, the largest annual increase on record, with renewables accounting for 92.5% of all global power additions [01-092]. Solar and wind accounted for 97.5% of all net renewable additions, with solar increasing by 453 GW and wind by 114 GW [01-093].

### Mineral Intensity of Clean Energy

Every unit of renewable energy capacity requires significantly more minerals than its fossil fuel equivalent. An onshore wind plant requires nine times more mineral resources than a gas-fired plant, and since 2010 the average amount of minerals needed for a new unit of power generation capacity has increased by 50% [01-094]. This mineral intensity means that as the world adds hundreds of gigawatts of renewable capacity each year, the demand for copper, rare earths, zinc, and other minerals scales in lockstep.

### Copper: The Backbone of Electrification

Copper sits at the centre of grid electrification. Global copper demand is projected to rise 50% by 2040 to 42 million metric tons, with a supply deficit of 10 million metric tons (25% below projected demand) expected unless significant new mining development occurs [01-095]. The IEA's current mine project pipeline points to a potential 30% copper supply shortfall by 2035 due to declining ore grades, rising capital costs, limited resource discoveries and long lead times [01-096].

The demand pull is coming from every direction. Annual copper demand for electricity grids alone is projected to grow from 5 Mt in 2020 to 7.5 Mt by 2040 under STEPS and to nearly 10 Mt under the Sustainable Development Scenario [01-097]. Grid modernisation, the expansion of transmission networks to connect remote renewable generation sites, and the electrification of transport and industry are all competing for the same constrained copper supply.

### Rare Earths in Wind Energy

Demand for rare earth elements in wind turbines — neodymium and praseodymium in particular — is set to more than triple by 2040 [01-098], driven by the expansion of permanent-magnet direct-drive turbines, especially in offshore wind installations. The offshore wind industry consumes approximately 220 tonnes of neodymium-praseodymium oxide per GW of installed capacity, and with global offshore wind capacity expected to grow substantially, this creates a concentrated demand channel for these specific minerals.

### The Grid Infrastructure Gap

Global electricity demand is projected to rise by around 40% to 2035, while battery storage additions reached over 75 GW in 2024 [01-099]. However, a critical gap has emerged: annual grid spending stands at USD 400 billion worldwide, compared with around USD 1 trillion spent on generation assets [01-100]. This infrastructure gap means that grid-related mineral demand — particularly for copper and aluminium — will need to accelerate substantially to keep pace with generation growth.

## Geopolitical Supply Chain Risks

The energy transition's growing mineral requirements intersect with a deeply concentrated global supply chain, creating strategic vulnerabilities that have moved from theoretical risk to operational reality in 2025.

### China's Processing Dominance

The scale of China's dominance across critical mineral supply chains is difficult to overstate. China is the dominant refiner for 19 of the 20 minerals analysed by the IEA, holding an average market share of around 70% [01-101]. Far from diversifying, this concentration has been increasing: the average market share of the top three refining nations rose from around 82% in 2020 to 86% in 2024, with some 90% of supply growth coming from the single top supplier [01-102].

In rare earths — the minerals essential for permanent magnets in wind turbines and EV motors — China's position is overwhelming. China's share of sintered permanent magnets has risen to 94%, and it represents about 91% of global production for magnet rare earths [01-103]. In battery supply chains, China dominates with shares of 80% or more in many key areas, reaching near-monopoly levels of 95% or above in precursor cathode materials and LFP cathode materials [01-106]. China also produces 75% of the world's purified phosphoric acid for LFP batteries and 95% of high-purity manganese sulphate, while two-thirds of global battery recycling capacity growth since 2020 has been in China [01-110].

### Export Controls: From Risk to Reality

In April 2025, China converted this market position into geopolitical leverage by introducing export controls on seven heavy rare earth elements and all related compounds, metals and magnets, subsequently expanding controls in October and November 2025 to cover parts, components, assemblies, and additional elements [01-104]. The impact was immediate and severe: European prices reached up to six times those in China, and many carmakers in the US, Europe, and elsewhere were forced to cut utilisation rates or temporarily shut down factories [01-105].

More than half of a broader group of energy-related minerals are now subject to some form of export controls, and supplies outside the leading producer meet on average only half of the remaining demand in 2035 [01-109]. This means that even with diversification efforts underway, the world remains structurally dependent on a single nation for the minerals essential to the energy transition.

### Cobalt and the DRC

The concentration risk extends beyond China. The DRC produced approximately 220,000 tonnes of cobalt in 2024 out of global production of 290,000 tonnes (about 76%), and in June 2025 banned all cobalt concentrate exports before shifting to a quota system limiting exports to 96,600 tonnes annually for 2026-2027 [01-107]. This single-country concentration creates both supply security risks and ethical sourcing challenges that are driving demand for alternative supply sources and processing capabilities.

### The Economic Stakes

The vulnerability of this concentrated supply chain carries significant economic consequences. The IEA estimates that a sustained supply shock for battery metals could increase global average battery pack prices by as much as 40-50% [01-108], potentially undermining the competitiveness of electric vehicles and slowing the energy transition itself. The stakes extend beyond any single technology — they strike at the economic viability of the entire decarbonisation programme.

## Western Sovereign Capability Response

The geopolitical reality of concentrated supply chains has catalysed an unprecedented policy response from Western governments. Nations are now treating critical mineral sovereignty as a core national security priority, deploying significant capital and diplomatic resources to build alternative supply chains.

### International Cooperation Architecture

In February 2025, 55 foreign delegations convened at the US-led Critical Minerals Ministerial and established FORGE (Forum on Resource Geostrategic Engagement), a new international forum succeeding the Minerals Security Partnership [01-111]. The US also entered into a US-Australia Critical Minerals Framework, committing USD 1 billion to joint minerals production projects, alongside partnerships with Saudi Arabia on a rare earths refinery and additional agreements with Cambodia, Malaysia and Thailand [01-113].

The EU has pursued a parallel strategy through its RESourceEU Action Plan, which sets out a EUR 3 billion investment drive to accelerate its Critical Raw Materials Strategy and reduce strategic dependencies, focusing on de-risking and scaling projects in extraction, processing, and recycling [01-112].

### Building Physical Capability

Translating policy ambition into physical processing capacity is proving to be the harder challenge. Several new permanent magnet manufacturing plants began operations in 2025, notably in the United States (MP Materials) and Estonia (Neo Performance Materials), but average mining project lead times remain approximately eight years [01-114]. About 85% of global battery manufacturing capacity is in China, though committed projects would reduce China's share to about two-thirds by 2030 as the EU and US each almost quadruple their manufacturing capacity [01-117].

### Investment Challenges

Despite the strategic imperative, investment momentum in critical mineral development weakened in 2024, with spending rising by just 5%, down from 14% in 2023, amid falling commodity prices that saw lithium prices drop over 80% since 2023 [01-115]. This creates a paradox: the strategic need for diversified supply chains is increasing precisely as market conditions are discouraging the private investment needed to build them.

### The Market Opportunity

The combined market value of key energy transition minerals — copper, lithium, nickel, cobalt, graphite, and rare earth elements — stands at around USD 325 billion and is projected to more than double to USD 770 billion by 2040 under the Net Zero Emissions scenario [01-116]. This represents a generational market opportunity for countries with mineral endowments and the processing capability to serve it. Australia, with its significant reserves across multiple critical minerals, is strategically positioned to capture a meaningful share of this value — particularly as Western governments actively seek to diversify away from Chinese-dominated supply chains.

## Synthesis

The energy transition is not a distant prospect — it is a present-tense industrial transformation that is already reshaping global mineral demand at unprecedented scale. Electric vehicle sales have passed the 20% market share threshold, renewable energy is accounting for more than 90% of new power generation capacity, and grid electrification programmes are expanding across every major economy.

This demand growth intersects with supply chains that are more concentrated, not less — a structural vulnerability that China's 2025 rare earth export controls have converted from theoretical risk to operational reality. The result is an urgent strategic imperative for Western governments to build sovereign processing capability, backed by significant policy commitments and capital deployment.

For Australia — and for the mineral-rich regions of Queensland in particular — this convergence of surging demand, supply chain vulnerability, and policy-driven investment creates a compelling window of opportunity. The question is not whether critical mineral processing capacity will be built outside China, but where and how quickly it can be established.
