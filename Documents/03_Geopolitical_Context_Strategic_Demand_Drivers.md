# Geopolitical Context and Strategic Demand Drivers

Critical minerals have crossed the threshold from commodity market to national security domain. What was once a commercial question about supply and demand has become a strategic contest between great powers, with control over mineral processing capacity functioning as geopolitical leverage in an era of renewed great-power competition. This document examines the five dimensions of this transformation: the US-China strategic rivalry, the global resurgence of resource nationalism, the concrete defence applications that make mineral supply a military readiness issue, the formation of allied mineral security blocs, and Australia's distinctive strategic premium as a trusted supplier.

## US-China Strategic Competition

The centrepiece of the current geopolitical landscape in critical minerals is the strategic competition between the United States and China. This is not a future risk -- it is a present-tense contest that intensified dramatically through 2025 and into 2026.

### China's Structural Dominance

China's position in critical mineral supply chains is the product of three decades of deliberate, sustained state investment. China conducts the overwhelming majority of global heavy-rare-earth processing and permanent magnet manufacturing, having locked up global upstream supply through strategic investments across the entire critical minerals ecosystem [01-118]. China holds a roughly 70% market share for at least 19 critical minerals and about 90% for cobalt, graphite, and rare earths, a dominance pursued through sustained strategic investment over decades [01-126].

This dominance is not merely a market position -- it is a strategic weapon. China's global dominance gives it control over pricing mechanisms that have put market-based mining companies out of business around the world [01-119]. By flooding markets with excess supply to push prices below the viability threshold for Western producers, China has systematically eliminated potential competitors, creating a structural dependency that is now being exploited [01-155].

### The Escalation of Export Controls

The export control escalation of 2025-2026 has converted this structural dominance from latent leverage into active coercion. The wave of export controls China introduced in 2025, targeting germanium, gallium and rare earths, was largely a retaliatory action responding to US restrictions on advanced chip exports and unilateral tariffs [01-120]. In October 2025, Beijing expanded export controls to include five additional rare earth elements critical to magnets and defence: erbium, europium, holmium, thulium, and ytterbium, building on the April 2025 controls on seven heavy rare earth elements [01-121].

### The US Strategic Response

The United States has responded with an unprecedented mobilisation of industrial and financial resources. In February 2026, the Trump administration announced Project Vault, a $12 billion initiative combining a $10 billion EXIM loan with $1.67 billion in private capital to establish a US Strategic Critical Minerals Reserve for materials including gallium, cobalt, rare earths, and heavy minerals [01-122]. Project Vault operates as a demand-led public-private partnership described as a "Costco for critical minerals," where participating manufacturers including Boeing, GM, Stellantis, GE Vernova, Corning, and Google identify required materials and commit financially, with full access permitted during major supply disruptions [01-123].

Beyond stockpiling, Vice President JD Vance proposed creating a critical mineral trading bloc among allies, stating: "What is before all of us is an opportunity at self-reliance that we never have to rely on anybody else except for each other, for the critical minerals necessary to sustain our industries and to sustain growth" [01-124]. The US Department of Energy has allocated $500 million for battery materials processing and recycling, plus $134 million for rare-earth element recovery technologies, while ARPA-E committed $40 million to wastewater recovery initiatives [01-125].

The scale of this response signals that the US now treats critical mineral security as a first-order strategic priority, comparable in urgency to semiconductor independence and energy security.

## Resource Nationalism

Beyond the US-China contest, a broader wave of resource nationalism is reshaping critical mineral supply chains globally. Producing nations across the developing world are leveraging their mineral endowments for greater economic returns and geopolitical influence, with implications that extend far beyond any single bilateral relationship.

### Indonesia: The Template for Downstream Capture

Indonesia's nickel strategy represents the most consequential case of resource nationalism in the critical minerals space. Indonesia implemented a nickel ore export ban in January 2020, which drove a 207.9% increase in downstream investment from USD 3.56 billion in 2019 to USD 10.96 billion in 2022, overwhelmingly financed by Chinese companies investing approximately USD 30 billion in nickel processing [01-132].

The transformation has been dramatic. Indonesia's nickel smelter capacity expanded from just 2 operational smelters prior to 2014 to 43 operating smelters by July 2023, with 28 under construction and 24 in planning [01-127]. Indonesia now accounts for over 50% of global nickel production. In 2024, Indonesia imposed a moratorium on new nickel pig iron smelters to preserve nickel resources and prioritise higher-value-added industries like batteries, signalling a second phase of resource nationalism focused on capturing even more value from the supply chain [01-138].

Indonesia's success has provided a template that other nations are now following -- demonstrating that export bans, while disruptive to existing supply chains, can effectively force the relocation of processing investment.

### Latin America: Lithium Nationalisation

In Latin America, lithium -- the metal at the heart of the battery revolution -- has become the focal point of nationalisation movements. Chile launched its National Lithium Strategy in April 2023 under President Gabriel Boric, establishing a National Lithium Company with state majority participation in all future lithium projects, while honouring existing contracts with SQM and Albemarle set to expire in 2030 and 2043 respectively [01-128].

Mexico went further, nationalising its lithium industry outright in April 2022 by modifying its mining law to give the state exclusive rights to explore, exploit, and use lithium, declaring it a strategic mineral and property of the Nation, and establishing the state company LitioMx to manage the entire lithium value chain [01-129]. Under Mexican law, no concessions, licences, contracts, permits, or authorisations will be granted for lithium activities outside state control.

### Africa: Raw Mineral Export Bans

African nations are also asserting control over their mineral resources. Zimbabwe suspended exports of all raw minerals and lithium concentrates with immediate effect in late February 2026, accelerating a ban originally scheduled for January 2027 [01-130]. Zimbabwe holds Africa's largest lithium reserves and exported 1.128 million metric tonnes of lithium-bearing spodumene concentrate in 2025, up 11% year-on-year, with most concentrate exported to China for processing into battery-grade materials [01-131]. The ban aims to force domestic processing rather than allowing value to be captured offshore.

### The Structural Impact

Whether states intervene for geopolitical reasons or to exercise market power, resource nationalism results in higher costs that are difficult to avoid because substitution possibilities for critical minerals are limited [01-137]. While enforcement remains imperfect -- intermediaries are circumventing China's antimony export controls by selling restricted Chinese-origin antimony oxides into US markets from Thailand and Mexico [01-136] -- the cumulative effect of these policies is a global supply chain that is becoming more fragmented, more expensive, and less predictable.

For countries like Australia that can offer processed minerals from stable, rule-of-law jurisdictions, resource nationalism elsewhere creates a competitive advantage and a supply premium that allied nations are willing to pay.

## Defence Applications of Critical Minerals

The national security case for critical mineral sovereignty is not abstract -- it is grounded in specific, quantifiable dependencies across modern military platforms. Understanding these dependencies is essential to appreciating why governments are treating mineral supply as a defence priority rather than a commercial matter.

### Rare Earths in Combat Platforms

The most acute military dependency involves rare earth elements in advanced combat platforms. A single F-35 Lightning II fighter jet requires more than 900 pounds of rare earth materials for its engines, weapons-tracking systems, and major subsystems [01-139]. A Virginia-class attack submarine contains approximately 9,200 pounds of rare earth materials and incorporates large permanent-magnet motors for quieter electric propulsion, with sonar arrays and guidance systems also utilising rare earth magnets [01-140].

China refines over 85% of the world's rare earths and produces nearly 90% of high-performance rare earth magnets, giving it direct leverage over the production of these platforms [01-141]. The vulnerability is not hypothetical: an unclassified war game indicated the US military would run out of many munitions within a week in a Taiwan Strait conflict scenario, while a GAO report estimated the cost to fix critical mineral supply chain gaps at $18.5 billion [01-145].

### Antimony: The Ammunition Mineral

Antimony provides a particularly stark illustration of defence mineral vulnerability. China accounts for 48% of global antimony production and supplies 63% of US antimony imports, while the US has had zero domestic antimony mining since the Sunshine Mine closure in 2001 and holds only 1,100 tons in strategic stockpile against 23,000 tons of annual consumption [01-133]. Antimony is a critical material for armour-piercing ammunition, night vision goggles, infrared sensors, and precision optics, and China's ban on antimony exports to the United States in December 2024 caused prices to surge above $40,000 per ton as shipments were slashed by 97% [01-134].

In response, the Pentagon deployed $7.5 billion to fortify domestic critical mineral production and stockpiling, including $500 million for cobalt, $245 million for antimony from US Antimony Corporation, $100 million for tantalum, and $45 million for scandium [01-135].

### Titanium: The Aerospace Chokepoint

Russia and China dominate the global supply of aerospace-grade titanium, with Ukraine's Zaporizhzhia titanium sponge plant out of operation since Russia's invasion, leading to titanium lead times extending to 5x longer than pre-2020 levels [01-146]. The real chokepoint is not ore availability but metal-grade production capacity, especially aerospace-certified sponge and titanium alloys -- precisely the kind of processing capability that a minerals research centre could help develop alternative approaches to.

### Copper and Naval Electrification

The US Navy describes naval electrification as "a critical part of the kill chain," advancing electric drive propulsion for warships including the DDG 1000 Zumwalt Class and Columbia-class submarine, driving demand for copper in high-power radars, directed-energy weapons, and silent-running propulsion systems [01-147]. Russia's 11 million artillery shells fired in 2022 in Ukraine contained approximately 5,500 tonnes of copper, equivalent to the copper in roughly 1,170 wind turbines, illustrating how military rearmament competes directly with the energy transition for critical mineral supply [01-143].

### The Pentagon's Response

The scale of military mineral consumption underscores the urgency: the US Department of Defense used 750,000 tons of minerals in 2017 during peacetime operations, and the US is 100% import-reliant on 12 critical minerals and over 50% import-reliant on an additional 31 [01-142]. The Pentagon aims to eliminate Chinese rare earth dependencies by 2027, including investing $400 million for a majority stake in MP Materials' rare earth processing expansion [01-144].

## Allied Bloc Formation

The geopolitical imperative to secure critical mineral supply chains has catalysed the most significant restructuring of international mineral trade architecture since the post-war era. Allied nations are forming overlapping, reinforcing coalitions designed to create alternative supply chains outside Chinese control.

### FORGE: The New Multilateral Architecture

At the February 2026 Critical Minerals Ministerial, Secretary Rubio announced FORGE (Forum on Resource Geostrategic Engagement) as the successor to the Minerals Security Partnership, with 54 countries and the European Union participating and South Korea serving as the first rotating chair until June 2026 [01-148]. FORGE represents a significant expansion from the MSP's 14 members, reflecting the broadening recognition that mineral security requires collective action.

FORGE is supported by a Critical Minerals Preferential Trade Group, a price floor mechanism, and a strategic reserve plan, forming a combined strategy of alliance, rules, and reserves to reduce China's dominance [01-149]. The price floor mechanism is particularly significant: a key obstacle to minerals security is China's deliberate manipulation of global markets by flooding them with excess supply to push prices so low that mines in countries like the United States and Australia become unviable [01-155]. Price floors would provide the market certainty needed to justify the capital investment required to develop alternative supply chains.

### The Quad Critical Minerals Initiative

In July 2025, the Quad (Australia, India, Japan, US) launched the Quad Critical Minerals Initiative to secure and diversify critical mineral supply chains [01-150]. The four members share 20 minerals in common across their respective critical minerals lists, providing a starting point for coordinated action. The initiative prioritises electronic waste recovery and critical minerals reprocessing alongside primary supply chain diversification.

### AUKUS and Defence Minerals

UK Industry Minister Chris McDonald stated that critical minerals were "very much" part of the AUKUS partnership, indicating integration of mineral security with the broader trilateral defence and security pact [01-151]. This positions critical minerals not as a standalone commercial arrangement but as an element of the deepest tier of allied defence cooperation.

### The US-Australia Bilateral Framework

The most consequential bilateral agreement in this space was signed on 20 October 2025, when President Trump and PM Albanese signed the US-Australia Framework for Securing of Supply in the Mining and Processing of Critical Minerals and Rare Earths, committing at least USD $1 billion from each nation within six months, with a total project pipeline of USD $8.5 billion [01-152].

The Framework includes commitments to protect domestic critical minerals markets from non-market policies and unfair trade practices through standards-based trading systems and a pricing framework including price floors [01-153]. It established a Critical Minerals Supply Security Response Group co-chaired by the US Secretary of Energy and Australian Minister for Resources to identify priority minerals, assess supply vulnerabilities, and coordinate delivery of processed minerals [01-154].

## Australia's Strategic Premium

Australia occupies a unique position in the global critical minerals landscape -- one that creates what can be described as a strategic premium for its mineral products and processing capabilities. This premium derives from the intersection of geological endowment, geopolitical alignment, institutional stability, and proactive government investment.

### Geological Endowment

Australia has identified 31 resource commodities as critical minerals, selected by assessing Australia's geological endowment against global technology needs of partner countries including the United States, European Union, India, Japan, South Korea, and the United Kingdom [01-156]. Australia is the world's largest lithium producer, responsible for 50% of global lithium extraction, with the world's second-largest lithium resources, and is ranked in the top five globally for economic resources of cobalt, tungsten, manganese, and vanadium [01-157].

More broadly, Australia possesses over 40 of the minerals identified as critical by the US Geological Survey [01-158]. Approximately 80% of Australia remains under-explored for critical minerals, with the Australian Government investing A$566.1 million over 10 years for geoscience mapping and discovery tools, indicating significant untapped potential [01-164].

### Geopolitical Alignment

Australia's value as a mineral supplier is amplified by its position within the Western alliance architecture. As a Five Eyes intelligence partner, AUKUS defence partner, Quad member, and longstanding US ally, Australia offers a level of geopolitical trust that few other major mineral-producing nations can match. This trust translates directly into preferential access to allied supply chains, government-backed financing, and collaborative R&D programmes.

Australia's resources industry has built a reputation in Asia as a reliable supplier of minerals, with the Quad, AUKUS, and bilateral agreements positioning Australia for friend-shoring as allies seek to diversify supply chains away from China and build more value-adding capability onshore [01-163]. The Australian Stock Exchange ranks second globally for mining company financing, and three Australian universities rank in the world's top six for mining engineering [01-158].

### Government Investment Architecture

The Australian Government has built a substantial investment framework to support critical minerals development. The 2025-26 federal budget committed A$1.2 billion to establish a Critical Minerals Strategic Reserve, planned to be operational from the second half of 2026 [01-159]. The broader investment framework includes A$4 billion through the Critical Minerals Facility, A$500 million from the Northern Australia Infrastructure Facility for NT, Queensland and WA projects, A$1 billion from the National Reconstruction Fund for mining technologies, and a 10% production tax incentive on processing and refining costs from 2027-28 to 2039-40 [01-160].

### Bilateral Partnerships

Australia's bilateral mineral partnerships are becoming more concrete and financially significant. Under the US-Australia Framework, priority projects include the Alcoa-Sojitz Gallium Recovery Project in Western Australia, producing 10% of global gallium supply with up to USD $200 million in Australian concessional equity, and the Arafura Nolans rare earth project in the Northern Territory, producing 5% of global rare earths supply with USD $100 million in Australian equity [01-161]. Japan and Australia have established a bilateral critical minerals partnership through the Japan-Australia Energy and Resources Dialogue, including co-financing exploration projects and a trilateral gallium partnership with the US [01-166]. The Australian Government has also invested A$1.25 billion as a concessional loan to Iluka Resources for the development of a rare earth refinery in Western Australia [01-165].

### Investment Readiness

PwC Australia identified 30 investment-ready critical minerals projects spanning lithium, nickel, cobalt, graphite, copper, vanadium, and rare earths, with an average post-tax internal rate of return of 26%, significantly outperforming solar and wind development projects [01-162]. This pipeline of commercially viable projects, backed by government co-investment and allied bilateral frameworks, positions Australia as the premier destination for critical minerals investment in the Western bloc.

## Synthesis: Implications for the MICMRC

The convergence of US-China strategic competition, resource nationalism, defence mineral dependencies, allied bloc formation, and Australia's strategic premium creates an exceptionally favourable environment for a critical minerals research and testing facility in Australia's North West Minerals Province. Several conclusions emerge:

**The demand signal is structural, not cyclical.** The geopolitical drivers documented in this report -- great-power competition, defence dependencies, allied supply chain restructuring -- are multi-decade trends that will sustain demand for critical minerals processing capability regardless of short-term commodity price fluctuations.

**Processing capability is the strategic bottleneck.** Across every dimension examined -- from China's rare earth dominance to titanium lead times to antimony shortages -- the chokepoint is not ore availability but processing and refining capacity. A research centre focused on developing and demonstrating mineral processing techniques directly addresses the most critical gap in allied supply chains.

**Australia is the preferred partner.** The bilateral frameworks with the US, Japan, and the EU, combined with Australia's Five Eyes and AUKUS membership, position Australian-based mineral processing capability as strategically preferred by the Western alliance. This creates a policy tailwind and potential funding pathway that facilities in other jurisdictions would not enjoy.

**The North West Minerals Province offers unique alignment.** Located in a region rich in copper, vanadium, rare earths, cobalt, and other critical minerals, and supported by Queensland's Critical Minerals Zones designation and dedicated infrastructure investment, the MICMRC would sit at the intersection of geological endowment and strategic demand in a way that few other locations globally can match.
