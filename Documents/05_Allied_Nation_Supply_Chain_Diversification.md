# Allied Nation Supply Chain Diversification: Australia's Position

Australia has emerged as the most strategically sought-after critical minerals partner among allied nations. Between 2022 and 2026, Australia signed bilateral critical minerals agreements with at least nine partner nations and the European Union, embedding itself at the centre of allied efforts to diversify mineral supply chains away from Chinese dominance. [01-211] This document examines the scope, commitments, and demand-shaping implications of these frameworks for Australian mineral producers — and for the business case for mineral processing research and testing infrastructure in Australia.

## 1. US-Australia Critical Minerals Framework

The bilateral relationship between Australia and the United States on critical minerals has evolved rapidly from technical cooperation into a comprehensive strategic framework. The trajectory began with a 2019 agreement between Geoscience Australia and the US Geological Survey for joint geological mapping, escalated through the May 2023 Climate, Critical Minerals and Clean Energy Transformation Compact under President Biden, and culminated in the landmark October 2025 Framework for Securing of Supply in the Mining and Processing of Critical Minerals and Rare Earths, signed by President Trump and Prime Minister Albanese at the White House. [01-168]

### The 2023 Compact: Foundation Setting

The May 2023 Compact advanced Australia's treatment as a "domestic source" under the US Defense Production Act of 1950, opening pathways to US government financing and procurement for Australian critical minerals projects. [01-175] This designation placed Australian mineral producers on a similar footing to domestic US suppliers for defence procurement purposes — a significant commercial advantage. The Compact also established a ministerial-level Critical Minerals Taskforce to coordinate bilateral supply chain strategy. [01-175]

### The 2025 Framework: Scale and Ambition

The October 2025 framework represents a step change in scale. Both governments committed to invest at least USD $1 billion each in financing to critical minerals projects within six months, anchoring an USD $8.5 billion pipeline of priority mining and processing projects. [01-168] [01-169] The US Export-Import Bank issued seven letters of interest totalling more than USD $2.2 billion in potential financing to advance critical minerals projects in Australia, which could support up to USD $5 billion in total investment. [01-170]

The framework goes beyond financing to include structural market-shaping mechanisms: price-support systems including price floors to counter market manipulation by adversarial nations, coordinated investment screening to prevent adversarial mining acquisitions, accelerated permitting for mining operations, and leveraging of existing strategic stockpiles. [01-173] A US-Australia Critical Minerals Supply Security Response Group, led by the US Secretary of Energy and Australian Minister for Resources, provides ongoing governance. [01-174]

### Anchor Projects

Two projects anchor the framework's initial implementation:

- **Alcoa-Sojitz Gallium Recovery Project** (Wagerup, Western Australia): The Australian Government committed up to USD $200 million in concessional equity finance. [01-171] This trilateral partnership with Japan and the United States is expected to deliver approximately 10% of global gallium supply. Japan has provided 50% of the project costs to date. [01-171] [01-193]

- **Arafura Nolans Project** (Northern Territory): The Australian Government invested USD $100 million in equity. Once operational, the project will produce approximately 5% of global rare earths supply. [01-172]

Additionally, Iluka Resources received a $1.25 billion Australian Government loan for its rare earth refinery, with allied offtake agreements ensuring production serves allied nation supply chains. [01-176]

### Australia's Comparative Position

Australia's position as the United States' preferred critical minerals partner rests on substantial foundations. Australia hosted 45% of worldwide rare earth exploration investment in 2024, totalling approximately USD $64 million, with 89 active exploration projects. [01-177] In May 2025, Lynas Rare Earths became the first company outside China to produce commercial quantities of dysprosium oxide — a key heavy rare earth element for permanent magnets in defence and clean energy applications. [01-208]

## 2. Inflation Reduction Act: Structural Demand for Australian Minerals

The US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 created a powerful market mechanism that directly benefits Australian mineral producers. Under Section 30D, electric vehicles qualify for a USD $3,750 tax credit if the applicable percentage of critical minerals in the battery are extracted or processed in the United States or a country with a free trade agreement — and Australia is explicitly listed as an eligible FTA partner. [01-178]

### Escalating Thresholds

The IRA's critical mineral sourcing requirements escalate over time, creating increasing structural demand for non-Chinese mineral sources: [01-179]

| Year | Minimum FTA-sourced percentage |
|------|-------------------------------|
| 2023 | 40% |
| 2024 | 50% |
| 2025 | 60% |
| 2026 | 70% |
| 2027+ | 80% |

By 2027, 80% of critical mineral value in EV batteries must come from FTA countries or be recycled in North America. This escalating threshold creates a structural ratchet favouring Australian mineral producers over time. [01-179]

### Foreign Entity of Concern Rules

From 2025, vehicles cannot contain any critical minerals extracted, processed, or recycled by a "foreign entity of concern" (FEOC), defined as entities incorporated in or controlled by China, Russia, Iran, or North Korea, or where 25% or more of voting rights, board seats, or equity interests are held by governments of those nations. [01-180] This effectively excludes Chinese-processed minerals from the US EV supply chain.

### Impact and Challenge

Australian mining companies have reportedly benefited from approximately US$13 billion in IRA-related deals with US car manufacturers. [01-181] However, a significant structural challenge remains: approximately 90% of all lithium produced in Australia is currently refined in China. [01-182] Under the FEOC rules, EVs with batteries containing Australian-mined, Chinese-refined minerals are ineligible for the tax credits. This creates a powerful incentive for Australia to develop domestic processing capacity — directly supporting the business case for mineral processing research and testing infrastructure such as the MICMRC.

## 3. EU Critical Raw Materials Act

The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA), in force since May 2024, establishes the most comprehensive regulatory framework for critical mineral supply diversification among Australia's trading partners. Its structured approach to reducing dependency on single suppliers creates direct opportunities for Australian mineral exports.

### Benchmarks and Targets

The CRMA establishes concrete capacity benchmarks for the EU by 2030: [01-183]

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| EU extraction | At least 10% of annual consumption |
| EU processing | At least 40% of annual consumption |
| EU recycling | At least 25% of annual consumption |
| Single-country cap | No more than 65% from any one country at any processing stage |

The 65% single-country diversification cap is particularly significant. Given China's current dominance across multiple mineral processing stages, this regulatory requirement creates structural demand for alternative suppliers. The CRMA also projects significant demand growth: rare earth metals demand increasing 7-fold and lithium demand increasing 21-fold by 2050. [01-186]

### EU-Australia MoU

Five days after the CRMA entered force, the EU and Australia signed a Memorandum of Understanding on 28 May 2024 establishing a bilateral partnership for sustainable critical and strategic minerals. [01-184] The MoU covers the entire value chain — exploration, extraction, processing, refining, recycling, and processing of extractive waste — and extends to cooperation in third countries where both parties share mutual interests. [01-184]

### Australian Strategic Projects in the EU

Australian-linked companies have already secured a meaningful foothold in the EU's strategic project pipeline. ASX-listed companies account for over 10% of the first 47 Strategic Projects approved under the CRMA, selected from 170 applications, with an expected total capital investment of EUR 22.5 billion. [01-185] Approved Australian-linked projects include:

- **Vulcan Energy** — Lionheart lithium project (Germany): geothermal lithium extraction with net-zero emissions
- **Talga Group** — Natural graphite mine (Sweden): supplying anode material to European battery manufacturers
- **European Metals** — Cinovec lithium project (Czech Republic): Europe's largest hard rock lithium resource
- **Euro Manganese** — Chvaletice manganese project (Czech Republic): waste-to-value reprocessing
- **Jervois Finland** — Cobalt refinery expansion (Finland)

Strategic Projects receive accelerated permitting — 27 months for extraction and 15 months for processing and recycling — reducing barriers for Australian companies operating in the EU. [01-187]

### Bilateral Agreements with EU Member States

Beyond the EU-level MoU, Australia has pursued bilateral agreements with major EU member states. Australia signed a bilateral agreement with France in September 2023 to deepen cooperation on critical mineral supply chains, and with Germany in April 2023 to conduct joint feasibility studies on establishing sustainable, resilient critical-minerals value chains. [01-188]

## 4. United Kingdom

The AUKUS security partnership provides a unique framework for Australia-UK critical minerals cooperation, linking mineral supply security directly to advanced defence technology development.

Australia and the United Kingdom signed a Joint Statement of Intent on critical minerals collaboration on 4 April 2023. [01-189] The statement commits both nations to increase investment links for critical minerals projects, support sovereign downstream processing and manufacturing capabilities, enhance R&D collaboration, promote high ESG standards, and encourage skills and expertise exchange between Australian and UK firms. [01-189]

In November 2024, the partnership expanded further when Australia and the UK announced the Australia-UK Climate and Clean Energy Partnership on the sidelines of COP29, enhancing bilateral cooperation including on opportunities in "green metals." [01-190]

The UK's critical minerals strategy targets reducing reliance on any single country to a maximum of 60% for any critical mineral by 2035, designating Australia as a "priority partner" within this diversification framework. Given AUKUS Pillar II technologies — including quantum computing, hypersonics, and advanced undersea capabilities — all depend on critical mineral supply chains currently dominated by China, the strategic imperative for UK-Australia mineral cooperation extends well beyond commercial interests.

## 5. Japan

Japan is one of Australia's most established critical minerals partners, with cooperation predating the current wave of bilateral agreements. The partnership reflects Japan's acute vulnerability as a resource-poor advanced manufacturing economy that depends on imported minerals for its electronics, automotive, and defence industries.

Japan and Australia signed a formal Critical Minerals Partnership in October 2022, establishing a framework for secure supply chains. [01-191] Key cooperation areas include co-financing critical minerals projects of mutual interest, joint R&D initiatives, legislative and regulatory information exchange, and combating "disinformation against our respective companies' efforts to diversify supply chains." An officials-level bilateral working group on critical minerals operates under the Japan-Australia Energy and Resources Dialogue (JAERD), involving government agencies, companies, research institutions, and financial organisations. [01-192]

Japan's commitment is demonstrated through concrete investment. Japan has provided 50% of the costs to date for the trilateral US-Japan-Australia gallium recovery project at Alcoa's Wagerup facility in Western Australia — a project expected to deliver 10% of global gallium supply. [01-193] This willingness to co-invest in Australian mineral processing infrastructure underscores Japan's long-term strategic commitment to the bilateral supply chain.

## 6. South Korea

The Australia-South Korea critical minerals partnership is notable for having moved beyond policy frameworks into tangible industrial cooperation. Built under the 2021 comprehensive strategic partnership, the bilateral relationship has produced concrete commercial outcomes that demonstrate real demand-shaping effects.

Export Finance Australia and South Korea's Export-Import Bank signed a memorandum of understanding in September 2023 to strengthen co-financing in critical minerals, renewable energy, and supply-chain resilience projects. [01-194] This institutional mechanism provides the financial backbone for project-level cooperation.

At the commercial level, bilateral cooperation is producing real supply chain outcomes:

- **Lithium**: Wesfarmers Chemicals, Energy and Fertilisers signed a February 2024 agreement to supply up to 85,000 tonnes of lithium concentrate to South Korean battery company LG Energy Solution. Pilbara Minerals and POSCO completed a lithium hydroxide conversion facility in South Korea processing Australian spodumene concentrate. [01-195]
- **Rare Earths**: Australian Strategic Materials achieved its first commercial sale of heavy rare earth metals (terbium and dysprosium) at its Korean Metals Plant in Cheongju, South Korea — demonstrating non-Chinese processing capability. [01-196]
- **Graphite**: Australia, South Korea, and Tanzania jointly launched the Mahenge graphite project as a flagship Minerals Security Partnership project, led by Black Rock Mining with POSCO International participation. [01-198]

South Korea's strategic motivation is clear: the nation aims to reduce its China-dependent critical minerals imports from 80% to 50% by 2030 and achieve a 20% recycling rate for critical minerals by 2030. [01-197] This creates significant and growing demand for Australian-sourced minerals as an alternative to Chinese supply.

## 7. India

The India-Australia critical minerals partnership is the newest of Australia's major bilateral relationships but is developing rapidly across multiple dimensions. The partnership leverages complementary strengths: Australia's resource endowment and India's manufacturing scale and growing clean energy demand.

India and Australia established a Critical Minerals Investment Partnership (CMIP) in March 2022, with Australia committing A$5.8 million over three years to encourage Indian investment in Australian critical minerals projects. [01-199] The CMIP identified five target projects for potential joint investment — two lithium and three cobalt — to build supply chains for key battery minerals. [01-201]

In 2024, bilateral cooperation expanded significantly with two R&D partnerships: the India-Australia Critical Minerals Research Partnership (AUD $12.2 million) focusing on sustainable supply chains, and the India-Australia Green Steel Research Partnership (AUD $10.4 million) aimed at reducing greenhouse gas emissions in steelmaking. [01-200]

The foundation for trade was strengthened by the 2022 Australia-India Economic Cooperation and Trade Agreement (ECTA), which eliminated tariffs on critical minerals and resources. [01-203] India and Australia share 24 minerals on both nations' critical minerals lists — including lithium, cobalt, rare earths, graphite, nickel, copper, manganese, titanium, and vanadium — providing a broad base for bilateral supply chain cooperation. [01-202]

## 8. Canada and Multilateral Frameworks

### Canada

Australia and Canada signed a Joint Declaration of Intent on critical minerals cooperation in November 2025, covering ESG standards, supply chain traceability, joint R&D across geoscience, green mining technology, mineral processing, and battery supply chains. [01-204] In March 2026, Canadian PM Mark Carney elevated the partnership further, welcoming Australia into the Critical Minerals Production Alliance launched under Canada's G7 Presidency — an initiative specifically designed to expand critical minerals production and processing capacity and diversify supply chains from mine to market. [01-205]

### Minerals Security Partnership

Australia participates in the Minerals Security Partnership (MSP) alongside 14 nations and the EU, including Canada, Finland, France, Germany, India, Italy, Japan, Norway, Sweden, the United Kingdom, the United States, the Republic of Korea, and Estonia. [01-210] The MSP coordinates allied action on critical mineral supply chains and has produced concrete outcomes, including the Mahenge graphite project as a flagship MSP initiative. [01-198]

The financial commitments attached to Australia's bilateral and multilateral critical minerals frameworks are substantial and growing:

The aggregate scale of these commitments — well in excess of AUD $15 billion in direct bilateral financing and project pipelines — represents a structural shift in the economics of Australian critical mineral development. Government co-financing, concessional equity, and guaranteed offtake arrangements reduce the commercial risk that has historically deterred investment in mineral processing.

## 10. Assessment of Demand Shaping

### The Structural Case

These bilateral and multilateral frameworks create structural demand for Australian-sourced minerals through several reinforcing mechanisms:

1. **Regulatory demand**: The IRA's escalating FTA sourcing thresholds (reaching 80% by 2027) and FEOC exclusions make Australian minerals increasingly necessary for IRA-compliant EV supply chains. [01-179] [01-180] The EU CRMA's 65% single-country cap similarly requires diversification away from Chinese processing. [01-183]

2. **Institutional demand**: Bilateral working groups, co-financing mechanisms, and joint governance bodies (such as the US-Australia Supply Security Response Group) create ongoing institutional momentum that sustains demand beyond individual project cycles. [01-174]

3. **Defence demand**: AUKUS, the Quad, and the broader allied defence posture create demand for secure mineral supply chains that is insulated from commercial price competition — defence procurement can tolerate price premiums that commercial markets cannot.

4. **Strategic stockpiling**: Australia's $1.2 billion Critical Minerals Strategic Reserve, designed to secure mineral rights and on-sell to allied partners, represents a government mechanism to guarantee demand even during market downturns. [01-206] [01-207]

### Australia's Position Relative to Competitors

Australia is not the only supplier nation courting allied demand. Canada, Chile, Brazil, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and Indonesia all have significant mineral endowments. However, Australia holds several structural advantages:

- **FTA coverage**: Australia has free trade agreements with the United States, Japan, South Korea, and other major consumers, qualifying its minerals for IRA credits and similar preferential treatment. [01-178]
- **Five Eyes membership**: As a member of the Five Eyes intelligence alliance and AUKUS, Australia enjoys a level of strategic trust that no other major mineral producer outside North America can match.
- **Regulatory and ESG alignment**: Australia's mining regulatory framework, environmental standards, and governance transparency align with allied nation requirements in ways that many competing producer nations cannot match.
- **Processing ambition**: Unlike many resource-rich nations content to export raw materials, Australia is actively investing in domestic processing capacity — Lynas, Iluka, and the Alcoa gallium project demonstrate this trajectory. [01-208] [01-176] [01-171]

### Limitations and Risks

The demand-shaping effects of these frameworks should not be overstated:

- **Non-binding nature**: Most bilateral MOUs and partnership statements explicitly note they "do not create legally binding rights or obligations." Commitments can be scaled back with changes in government.
- **Processing gap**: The fact that 90% of Australian lithium is still refined in China means the IRA's FEOC rules could exclude Australian minerals until domestic or allied processing capacity catches up. [01-182]
- **Commercial viability**: As one analyst noted, "genuine progress will require practical policy innovation and shared risk-mitigation mechanisms, not just high-level political declarations." European and Australian cost structures remain higher than competing jurisdictions.
- **Protectionist risks**: The US has shown willingness to prioritise domestic producers over allied suppliers. The Trump administration's rejection of a reciprocal access deal in March 2025 demonstrates that allied status does not guarantee market access.

### Implications for the MICMRC

These bilateral frameworks create a favourable demand environment for mineral processing research and testing infrastructure. The collective push to develop non-Chinese processing capacity across allied nations generates demand for:

- Metallurgical testing of new processing routes for rare earths, lithium, gallium, and other strategic minerals
- Bench-scale and pilot-scale process development for projects seeking allied financing
- Quality assurance and certification services required for ESG-compliant, IRA-eligible supply chains
- R&D capability aligned with bilateral research partnerships (Japan, India, South Korea, Canada)

The MICMRC's proposed capabilities — bench-scale mineral processing, commercial assays, and environmental testing — are directly relevant to the processing capacity bottleneck that these frameworks are designed to address. The framework explicitly provides for engagement with third parties to ensure supply chain security [01-209], creating opportunities for Australian research infrastructure to serve the entire allied minerals network.
