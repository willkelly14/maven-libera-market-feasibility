# AI & Data Centre Infrastructure Mineral Demand

## Introduction

The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence and data centre infrastructure represents a powerful new demand pathway for critical minerals — one that compounds the already-significant requirements of the energy transition. As the world's largest technology companies invest hundreds of billions of dollars in AI compute capacity, the mineral supply chains that underpin this buildout face unprecedented pressure. This document examines the scale of projected data centre growth, quantifies the mineral intensity of this infrastructure, and assesses how AI-driven demand interacts with existing supply and demand dynamics for minerals relevant to the North West Minerals Province (NWMP).

For the Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre (MICMRC), this demand pathway is directly relevant: copper, rare earth elements, and silver — all minerals produced or explored in the NWMP — are among the most material-intensive inputs to data centre construction, cooling systems, and semiconductor manufacturing. The convergence of energy transition demand and AI infrastructure demand creates a structural case for expanded mineral processing research and testing capacity.

## 1. Global Data Centre Capacity Growth

### 1.1 Current Scale and Growth Trajectory

Global data centres consumed approximately 415 TWh of electricity in 2024, accounting for roughly 1.5% of global electricity consumption, having grown at approximately 12% annually over the preceding five years [01-212]. This growth is accelerating. The International Energy Agency (IEA) projects data centre electricity consumption will double to approximately 945 TWh by 2030 in its Base Case scenario, representing just under 3% of total global electricity consumption [01-213].

Goldman Sachs Research estimates global data centre power capacity will reach approximately 122 GW by the end of 2030, up from around 55 GW in 2023 — a 165% increase [01-215]. S&P Global provides an even wider range, estimating the sector could add between 69 GW and 141 GW from 2025 through 2030, with total capacity potentially reaching 200 GW by 2030 [01-218].

### 1.2 AI as the Primary Growth Driver

AI workloads are the dominant force behind this expansion. In 2023, AI accounted for 14% of global data centre power usage, with cloud computing at 54% and traditional workloads at 32% [01-216]. By 2027, Goldman Sachs projects AI will grow to 27% of overall demand at 84 GW total [01-216]. The IEA projects electricity consumption in AI-accelerated servers will grow at 30% annually in its Base Case, compared with 9% for conventional servers [01-217].

The scale of investment is staggering. The top five US hyperscale technology companies are expected to invest US$736 billion in data centre infrastructure in 2025-2026 alone, while Goldman Sachs estimates approximately US$720 billion in grid spending through 2030 will be needed to support data centre expansion [01-219]. McKinsey estimates data centres will require US$6.7 trillion in cumulative investment worldwide through 2030, with annual capital expenditure expected to approach US$1 trillion by 2030 [01-245].

### 1.3 Regional Concentration

The United States and China account for nearly 80% of global data centre electricity consumption growth to 2030 [01-214]. The US alone is expected to add approximately 240 TWh (+130%), while China adds 175 TWh (+170%) [01-214]. Europe, Japan, and Southeast Asia are also expanding significantly but from smaller bases.

### 1.4 Uncertainty Range

The IEA's sensitivity analysis illustrates the wide range of possible outcomes: the Lift-Off Case projects data centre electricity demand could reach 1,700 TWh by 2035 (4.4% of global electricity), while the Headwinds Case puts it at just 700 TWh (less than 2%) [01-244]. Even in the conservative scenario, data centre power demand roughly doubles from current levels.

## 2. Mineral Intensity of Data Centre Infrastructure

### 2.1 Overall Mineral Footprint

Each megawatt of data centre capacity embeds approximately 60-75 tonnes of minerals, concentrated primarily in power and cooling systems rather than in the servers themselves [01-220]. As AI-dedicated facilities rise from approximately 25% of data centres today to more than 60% by 2035, material intensity increases due to higher rack densities, heavier electrical backbones, and more robust cooling requirements [01-224].

### 2.2 Copper: The Dominant Mineral Input

Copper is the single most material-intensive mineral in data centre construction. BloombergNEF estimates the industry average at approximately 27 tonnes of copper per megawatt of AI data centre capacity [01-222]. A detailed case study of Microsoft's 80 MW Chicago data centre showed approximately 2,100 tonnes of copper were used — 26 tonnes per MW including on-site and near-site power connections, with facility-only copper intensity of roughly 12 tonnes per MW [01-221]. Copper is used extensively in power distribution units, busbars, cabling, transformers, switchgear, uninterruptible power supplies, and cooling system components, representing up to 6% of total data centre capital expenditure [01-243].

### 2.3 Steel and Structural Materials

Large hyperscale data centre campuses require between 10,000 and 200,000 tonnes of steel depending on scale and architecture, with smaller facilities needing approximately 10,000 tonnes for 75,000 square metres and multi-building campuses requiring over 200,000 tonnes for approximately 1.7 million square metres [01-228].

### 2.4 Key Minerals by Component

The US Geological Survey identifies key minerals required for data centres across four functional categories [01-229]:

- **Serverboards and circuitry:** copper, silver, gold, tin, tantalum, palladium, platinum
- **Heat sinks:** aluminium, copper
- **Semiconductors and microchips:** silicon, germanium, gallium, indium, arsenic
- **Magnets and data storage:** rare earth elements, barite, boron

The United States is 100% import-dependent for gallium, germanium, indium, arsenic, and tantalum — all critical semiconductor and data centre minerals — with 80% import dependence for rare earth elements and 45% for copper [01-230].

## 3. Copper Demand and Supply Dynamics

### 3.1 Data Centre Copper Demand Projections

Multiple authoritative sources project substantial copper demand growth from data centres:

- **BloombergNEF:** Copper demand from data centres will average around 400,000 tonnes annually over the next decade, peaking at 572,000 tonnes in 2028 [01-222]. Cumulative copper locked into data centres could surpass 4.3 million tonnes by 2035 [01-223].
- **Macquarie:** Data centres will consume between 330,000 and 420,000 tonnes of copper annually by 2030, with a midpoint of 375,000 tonnes [01-248].
- **BHP:** Copper demand from data centres could rise from approximately 1% of global consumption today to 7% by 2050, reaching 3 million tonnes annually — a six-fold increase [01-241].

### 3.2 Interaction with Broader Supply Constraints

Data centre copper demand arrives at a time of acute supply pressure. S&P Global projects total global copper demand will reach 42 million metric tons by 2040 — a 50% increase from current levels — with a projected supply deficit of 10 million metric tons as global primary production peaks at approximately 33 million metric tons around 2030 [01-225]. S&P Global describes copper as "the connective artery linking physical machinery, digital intelligence, mobility, infrastructure, communication and security systems" [01-227].

The International Copper Study Group projects the refined copper market will shift from a surplus of approximately 178,000 tonnes in 2025 to a deficit of 150,000 tonnes in 2026, as mine production growth is constrained by lower copper concentrate availability [01-240]. BloombergNEF warns the global copper supply gap could swell to 6 million tonnes by 2035 [01-223].

The IEA's Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2025 identifies copper as one of two minerals (alongside lithium) where expected mined supply from announced projects falls furthest short of projected demand by 2035, with an implied deficit of 30% in the Stated Policies Scenario [01-247].

### 3.3 AI and Energy Transition Demand Convergence

S&P Global projects that AI and data centres combined with defence will account for approximately 4 million metric tons of copper demand by 2040, with both sectors roughly tripling their consumption [01-226]. This sits alongside energy transition demand projected at 15.7 million metric tons by 2040 [01-227]. Installed data centre capacity alone is projected to exceed 550 GW by 2040 — more than five times 2022 levels [01-226].

## 4. Silver: A Growing Technology Metal

### 4.1 Record Industrial Demand

Global silver industrial demand reached a record 680.5 million ounces in 2024, rising 4% year-on-year for the fourth consecutive record [01-237]. This growth was driven by structural gains in the green economy, including photovoltaic applications, vehicle electrification, grid infrastructure, and AI-driven consumer electronics growth [01-237]. Silver demand for photovoltaic applications alone has surged approximately 139% from 2016 to 2025, with solar PV's share of total silver industrial demand rising from 11% in 2014 to 29% in 2024 [01-239].

### 4.2 Structural Supply Deficit

The global silver market has experienced a structural supply deficit for five consecutive years (2021-2025), with the combined deficit reaching 678 million ounces during 2021-2024 alone — equivalent to 10 months of global mine supply [01-238]. The 2025 deficit is estimated at approximately 95 million ounces [01-238].

### 4.3 Data Centre and AI Applications

Silver's superior electrical and thermal conductivity makes it essential in data centre electronics: serverboard connections, high-performance solder joints, and thermal interface materials all rely on silver [01-229]. Each AI server relies on thousands of high-performance connections where silver's conductivity is critical [01-239]. As data centre construction scales up and AI hardware density increases, silver demand from this sector adds incrementally to the already-strained supply picture driven by solar PV and electronics.

## 5. Rare Earth Elements and Semiconductor Minerals

### 5.1 Rare Earths in Data Centre Cooling

Neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB) permanent magnets are essential components of data centre cooling systems, powering the electronically commutated (EC) fan motors and variable-speed pumps that manage heat in modern facilities [01-235]. These magnets deliver 10-20% efficiency gains at part load compared to older induction motor systems [01-235]. The global data centre cooling market was valued at approximately US$12.7 billion in 2023 and is projected to reach US$17.8 billion by 2028 [01-236].

### 5.2 Gallium and Germanium: Semiconductor Bottlenecks

The AI boom depends critically on compound semiconductors made from gallium nitride (GaN) and gallium arsenide (GaAs), which outperform silicon in high-frequency, high-power applications. China controls 98% of global primary gallium production and 60% of germanium refining, creating supply chain vulnerabilities that FP Analytics argues "dwarf even the well-documented risks in battery supply chains" [01-231].

These vulnerabilities have already materialised. In December 2024, China functionally banned the export of gallium, germanium, antimony, and superhard materials to the United States — the most stringent export controls China has imposed on critical minerals [01-242]. Earlier restrictions in 2023 raised gallium prices 50-100% outside China, with Chinese gallium exports plunging from 28,000 kg in H1 2023 to 16,000 kg in H2 2023 [01-232].

### 5.3 Demand Projections

By 2030, data centres are projected to require an additional 512,000 tonnes of copper, with global demand increases of approximately 2% for copper, 3% for rare earth elements, and up to 11% for gallium attributable to AI infrastructure buildout [01-233]. By 2033, germanium demand is projected to increase 37% and gallium demand 85% [01-234]. A 30% disruption in gallium supplies alone could cause a US$600 billion reduction in US economic output, equating to more than 2% of GDP [01-234].

## 6. Supply Chain Implications for NWMP Commodities

### 6.1 Compounding Demand Pathways

The AI and data centre demand pathway does not replace energy transition mineral demand — it compounds it. Copper, rare earths, and silver face simultaneous demand pressure from:

1. **Energy transition:** EVs, battery storage, grid expansion, wind and solar infrastructure
2. **AI and data centres:** Power distribution, cooling, servers, semiconductors
3. **Defence:** Electronics, communications, advanced systems

This convergence creates a structural demand case that exceeds what any single pathway would justify.

### 6.2 Supply Response Constraints

Mine development timelines averaging 16 years from discovery to production mean that even with strong price signals, new supply cannot arrive fast enough to match the pace of data centre construction [01-246]. With global copper primary production projected to peak around 33 million tonnes in 2030 [01-225], the supply gap widens rapidly through the 2030s and into the 2040s.

### 6.3 Implications for MICMRC

The mineral demand implications of AI infrastructure strengthen the business case for the MICMRC in several ways:

- **Copper processing research:** As copper's role in digital infrastructure grows, demand for advanced metallurgical testing of copper concentrates — including from NWMP deposits — will increase [01-225] [01-227].
- **Rare earth characterisation:** The growing importance of NdFeB magnets in cooling systems [01-235], combined with rare earth processing concentration in China [01-231], reinforces the strategic case for Australian rare earth testing and processing capability.
- **Silver and by-product recovery:** Silver is often a by-product of base metal mining. Research into improved silver recovery from polymetallic ores — common in the NWMP — could unlock additional value from existing operations [01-237] [01-239].
- **Concentrate testing for new demand channels:** As data centre operators seek to secure mineral supply chains, they will look to diversify sourcing. NWMP producers will need advanced testing infrastructure to demonstrate concentrate quality for these new end-use applications.

## Conclusion

The AI and data centre infrastructure buildout represents a transformational demand pathway for critical minerals. With global data centre capacity projected to grow from 55 GW to 122-200 GW by 2030 [01-215] [01-218] and potentially 550 GW by 2040 [01-226], the mineral requirements are enormous: 400,000+ tonnes of copper per year [01-222], growing rare earth demand for cooling magnets [01-235], and critical semiconductor minerals facing acute supply concentration risks [01-231]. This demand arrives on top of — not instead of — energy transition mineral requirements, creating compounding pressure on already-constrained supply chains [01-225] [01-247]. For the NWMP and the proposed MICMRC, this convergence of demand pathways underscores the strategic importance of expanding mineral processing research and testing capacity in Australia.
