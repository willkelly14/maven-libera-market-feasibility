# Peer Nation Infrastructure Benchmarking

## Purpose

This document profiles how peer mining nations support their critical minerals sectors with shared-use research and testing infrastructure. It answers the implicit question from funders and stakeholders: *Do other countries do this?* The answer is unequivocally yes. Government-supported, shared-use mineral processing R&D facilities are a well-established model across every major mining jurisdiction globally.

The proposed Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre (MICMRC) is not a novel experiment — it follows a proven template used by Canada, Finland, the United States, South Africa, and Chile to bridge the gap between exploration and commercial-scale mineral processing. Each of the facilities profiled below provides metallurgical testing, pilot-scale processing, and analytical services on a shared-use basis, enabling mining companies of all sizes — from junior explorers to major producers — to de-risk processing flowsheets without building their own laboratories.

## 1. Canada — CanmetMINING and the Saskatchewan Research Council

### CanmetMINING

Canada operates the most direct structural analogue to the proposed MICMRC through CanmetMINING, a federal government science and technology laboratory under Natural Resources Canada. CanmetMINING is a substantial operation with over 500 research scientists and engineers across seven campuses, conducting research to make Canada's mining industry innovative and sustainable. [07-036]

**Services and capabilities.** CanmetMINING's mineral processing facility in Ottawa conducts comprehensive research and testing of all aspects of a mineral processing plant, including comminution, separation, and flotation, with a pilot plant capacity of 20 kg/hr for flowsheet testing. [07-037] Key equipment includes a Denver flotation machine, Eriez HydroFloat for coarse particle recovery, SELFRAG high-voltage rock liberation systems, and a Sepro Falcon concentrator for gravity separation. The facility also houses Canada's only ISO 17025-accredited dynamometer facility for diesel engine certification for underground mine use, and produces over 75 certified reference materials supplied to laboratories worldwide. [07-039]

**Funding and governance.** CanmetMINING is funded through the federal government budget as part of Natural Resources Canada. It operates through cooperative and cost-recovery business agreements, meaning industry clients share costs for collaborative research projects. [07-038] This hybrid model — government-funded base with fee-for-service supplementation — is directly comparable to the funding model proposed for the MICMRC.

**Users.** The facility serves a diverse client base spanning the full spectrum of the mining industry, with industrial partners including major producers (Glencore, Freeport-McMoran, Agnico Eagle), mid-tier companies (Avalon, Quest), and technology developers (GeoMega). [07-038]

**Companion facility: CanmetMATERIALS.** Canada also operates CanmetMATERIALS in Hamilton, Ontario — the country's largest research centre dedicated to fabricating, processing, and evaluating metals and materials. This LEED Platinum certified facility encompasses approximately 16,500 square metres (177,280 square feet) of laboratory and support office space, focusing on downstream value-added processing including pilot-scale casting, rolling, welding, corrosion testing, and mechanical testing. [07-040]

### Saskatchewan Research Council — Rare Earth Processing Facility

Canada has also invested heavily in purpose-built critical minerals processing infrastructure through the Saskatchewan Research Council (SRC). The SRC Rare Earth Processing Facility in Saskatoon has received CAD 71 million from the Government of Saskatchewan and CAD 30 million from the Government of Canada since 2020. [07-041] In 2024, it became the first facility in North America to produce rare earth metals at commercial scale. [07-041]

The facility is fully integrated with hydrometallurgy, separation, and metal smelting stages, producing magnet-grade neodymium praseodymium (NdPr) metal at 10 tonnes per month with purities greater than 99.5 per cent. [07-042] It uses proprietary, AI-controlled solvent extraction cells designed and fabricated in-house.

**Relevance to MICMRC.** The SRC facility demonstrates that government-backed critical minerals processing infrastructure can progress from concept to commercial-scale production within a few years, and that the public investment case is compelling enough to attract over CAD 100 million in combined federal and provincial funding. [07-041]

## 2. Finland — GTK Mintec

### Overview

Finland's GTK Mintec, located in Outokumpu, is one of the world's leading research platforms for mineral processing and circular economy. It is a unique facility combining mineral research laboratories with an industrial-scale pilot plant, spanning 5,500 square metres and operated by the Geological Survey of Finland (GTK) under the Ministry of Economic Affairs and Employment. [07-043]

GTK Mintec began operations in the mid-1980s and was transferred to GTK in 2004. Today it employs approximately 50 staff and processes around 100 projects annually, including 8 to 12 industrial-scale test runs. [07-044] Its mineral engineering methods have been verified for processing industrial minerals, base metals, battery minerals, critical minerals, precious metals, and industrial side streams. [07-044]

### Services and Capabilities

GTK Mintec offers services across the full mineral processing chain: crushing and grinding, hydrometallurgy, gravity and heavy medium separation, magnetic separation, flotation, process chemistry, mineralogy (including Mineral Liberation Analyser technology), and recycling and environmental studies.

The pilot plant has a feeding capacity of approximately 0.2 to 5 tonnes per hour, with typical sample requirements of 50 to 500 tonnes per test run. [07-045] A smaller minipilot operates at 10 to 50 kg/hr for sample sizes of 400 to 2,000 kg. [07-045] The facility also includes a 1.5-hectare tailings test area for studying long-term behaviour of extractive waste.

### Funding and Investment

The Finnish government has made substantial and increasing investments in GTK Mintec. In September 2021, the Ministerial Finance Committee committed EUR 11 million for new laboratory and office facilities, with GTK investing an additional EUR 4 million in laboratory equipment and digitalisation. [07-046] Personnel were planned to grow from 40 to 60 person-years by 2025. [07-046]

In September 2024, the Finnish government proposed a total investment of EUR 55 million for 2025-2028 to renew the pilot plant, with EUR 18 million in government mandate funding. [07-047] The Director General of GTK, Kimmo Tiilikainen, stated: *"The investment in GTK Mintec's new pilot plant will enable the production of the necessary research data for the production of raw materials for the mining and circular economy."* [07-047]

### Strategic Significance

Finland's Minister Mika Lintila explicitly linked the Mintec investment to national industrial strategy, stating that the decision "supports the goal of creating a well-functioning and sustainable battery cluster in Finland." [07-048] This framing — connecting shared-use R&D infrastructure to national supply chain objectives — is directly parallel to the case for the MICMRC within Australia's critical minerals strategy.

**Relevance to MICMRC.** GTK Mintec is the closest international analogue to the proposed MICMRC in terms of scale, governance model (government research agency), service mix (laboratory plus pilot plant), and strategic positioning (enabling a national critical minerals supply chain). The EUR 55 million investment commitment from Finland demonstrates ongoing government confidence in this infrastructure model. [07-047]

## 3. United States — National Laboratory Network

### Critical Materials Innovation Hub (CMI)

The United States operates the largest coordinated critical minerals R&D effort globally through the Department of Energy's national laboratory system. The Critical Materials Innovation Hub (CMI), led by Ames National Laboratory since 2013, includes more than 300 people in leadership and research roles across nine national laboratories, over a dozen universities, and over thirty industry partners. [07-049]

Over its first decade, the CMI Hub generated 50 U.S. patents, 202 invention disclosures, 635 peer-reviewed publications, 50 technology licences, and 12 R&D 100 Awards, while training more than 400 students and postdoctoral scholars. [07-050] The hub also received over USD 80 million in additional government awards beyond its core funding. [07-050] Its research spans four streams: Enhancing and Diversifying Supply, Developing Substitutes, Unlocking Secondary Sources (recycling), and Crosscutting Research.

### METALLIC — Critical Materials Supply Chain Research Facility

In 2024, the DOE established METALLIC, a federated critical materials research facility led by NETL with USD 75 million in funding. [07-051] METALLIC spans nine national laboratories and is explicitly designed as a shared-use platform — its director, Tom Tarka, described it as "a facility where users can access equipment and expertise rather than a place for national labs to do their own research." [07-051]

METALLIC is organised into four operational centres: Feedstock Beneficiation, Extractions and Separations, Refining, and Alloy Development and Advanced Manufacturing. [07-052] Idaho National Laboratory maintains rare earth ore beneficiation activities given its radiological waste management capabilities, while NETL leads battery mineral beneficiation and brine testbed research. [07-053]

### Governance Model

The U.S. model differs from the MICMRC proposal in scale but shares the same fundamental principle: government-funded infrastructure accessible to industry users. DOE national laboratories operate under a Government-Owned, Contractor-Operated (GOCO) model managed by universities or private entities on behalf of the federal government. Industry accesses the facilities through Cooperative Research and Development Agreements (CRADAs) — a partnership model that parallels CanmetMINING's cost-recovery approach and the MICMRC's proposed fee-for-service model.

**Relevance to MICMRC.** The U.S. commitment of USD 75 million to METALLIC alone — a single program within a much larger national laboratory infrastructure — demonstrates the scale at which major economies invest in shared-use critical minerals R&D. [07-051] The explicitly shared-use design of METALLIC is particularly relevant as a governance model comparator.

## 4. South Africa — Mintek

### Overview

Mintek is South Africa's national mineral research organisation, established in 1934 and governed by the Mineral Technology Act No. 30 of 1989. [07-054] Its statutory mandate is to "promote mineral technology and foster the establishment and expansion of industries in the field of minerals and products derived therefrom through research, development and technology transfer." [07-054] It is the longest-operating facility profiled in this document, with over 90 years of continuous operation.

### Facilities and Scale

Mintek operates from a 23-hectare campus in Randburg, Johannesburg, with world-class laboratories and piloting facilities. [07-055] The campus includes experimental furnaces, including a 3.2 MV-ampere plasma furnace. [07-055] The organisation has approximately 837 employees and reported a 6% revenue increase in the 2022/23 financial year. [07-057]

### Services and Research Clusters

Mintek is organised into three research clusters: [07-056]

1. **Mineral Processing and Characterisation** — mineralogy, analytical chemistry, and mineral processing services including desktop studies, laboratory testing, pilot plant operations, and numerical simulation
2. **Extractive Metallurgy** — biometallurgy, hydrometallurgy, and pyrometallurgy
3. **Mining, Materials and Automation** — measurement and control systems, advanced materials including nanotechnology, and mining and mineral economics

### Governance and Funding

Mintek operates as a statutory body under the Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy, governed by a Board of Directors. [07-054] Its funding comes from a combination of government grants (parliamentary allocation) and commercial revenue from contract research, product sales, and technology licensing. This mixed-funding model — where a government base allocation is supplemented by fee-for-service revenue — is the most common funding structure among the facilities profiled in this document.

### Notable Outcomes

Mintek's patented technologies operate in over 40 countries globally, including DC arc smelting technology (deployed at Palmiet Ferrochrome in 1988), FloatStar flotation circuit control (commercialised in 1994), and the iGoli gold extraction process. [07-058] These commercial successes demonstrate that government-funded mining R&D infrastructure can generate technologies with global market application.

Mintek's evolution from a university-based Minerals Research Laboratory (1934) through to a statutory independent body (National Institute for Metallurgy Act, 1965) and its current form as the Council for Mineral Technology (1975) provides a useful governance template for how the MICMRC might evolve over time. [07-059]

**Relevance to MICMRC.** Mintek demonstrates the long-term viability of government-funded mineral research organisations. Its 90-year track record, 837-strong workforce, global technology licensing, and mixed-funding model provide the strongest evidence that this type of institution can be self-sustaining and nationally significant. [07-057] [07-058]

## 5. Chile — CIMM and Codelco

### CIMM — Centro de Investigacion Minera y Metalurgica

Chile established the Centro de Investigacion Minera y Metalurgica (CIMM) in 1970 as a non-profit corporation with founding members CORFO (Chile's economic development agency), ENAMI (national mining enterprise), and COCHILCO (copper commission), with CODELCO as an initial cooperating member. [07-060] CIMM represents a public-private model: created by state entities but operating as a private-law corporation.

CIMM's commercial subsidiary, CIMM Tecnologias y Servicios (CIMM T&S), grew to employ over 2,000 staff and generate revenues exceeding US$65 million annually by 2011, providing metallurgical testing at bench and pilot scale, analytical services, environmental monitoring, and plant operation services across six Chilean locations. [07-061] In 2011, SGS acquired CIMM T&S for US$37 million — a transaction that demonstrated both the commercial value created by the facility and the risk that successful government-initiated research infrastructure can be privatised away from its public-interest mandate. [07-061]

### Codelco's Innovation Infrastructure

Chile's mining R&D is also supported through state-owned copper producer Codelco, which invests around USD 100 million annually in technology development. [07-062] In 2016, Codelco consolidated its technology subsidiaries — the Institute for Innovation in Mining and Metallurgy (IM2), BioSigma (biotechnology for mining), and Codelco Lab — into a single division, Codelco Tech, focusing on pyrometallurgy, hydrometallurgy, water management, energy, automation, and new mineral applications. [07-062]

### Government Returns on Investment

Chile's broader experience with public geoscience investment provides compelling evidence for the MICMRC's business case. Public geoscience investments through SERNAGEOMIN and Cochilco have returned an estimated US$11.5 in tax revenue per US$1 invested, demonstrating the high fiscal returns from government investment in mining research and geological infrastructure. [07-063]

**Relevance to MICMRC.** Chile's experience offers both positive and cautionary lessons. The positive lesson is that state-initiated mining research infrastructure (CIMM) can scale rapidly to employ thousands and generate significant commercial revenue. [07-061] The cautionary lesson is the 2011 privatisation of CIMM T&S — the MICMRC's governance structure should be designed to retain public ownership and mission alignment even as commercial revenues grow.

## 7. Implications for the MICMRC

The evidence from these five peer nations confirms several key points for the MICMRC feasibility study:

1. **Shared-use mineral processing R&D infrastructure is a proven global model.** Every major mining nation operates government-supported facilities that provide metallurgical testing, pilot plant services, and analytical capabilities on a shared-use basis. The MICMRC is not proposing something novel — it is filling an Australian gap in a model that is well-established internationally.

2. **Government funding is the foundation.** In every case, government funding provides the base for these facilities — whether through direct budget allocation (Canada, Finland), statutory authority with parliamentary funding (South Africa), state-owned enterprise investment (Chile), or federal research appropriations (USA). Fee-for-service revenue supplements but does not replace public funding. [07-038] [07-046] [07-054] [07-062]

3. **The investment quantum is significant and growing.** Finland has committed EUR 55 million to renew GTK Mintec. [07-047] Canada has invested CAD 101 million in the SRC rare earth facility. [07-041] The U.S. has allocated USD 75 million for METALLIC alone. [07-051] These figures provide useful benchmarks for the MICMRC's capital investment case.

4. **The model generates measurable returns.** The CMI Hub produced 50 patents and 50 technology licences in 10 years. [07-050] Mintek's technologies operate in over 40 countries. [07-058] Chile's public geoscience investments return an estimated US$11.5 per dollar invested in tax revenue. [07-063] These outcomes support the case that public investment in mineral processing R&D generates tangible economic returns.

5. **Governance design matters.** The facilities that have maintained their public-interest mission longest (Mintek, CanmetMINING) operate under statutory or departmental governance. [07-054] [07-059] Chile's experience — where the commercially successful CIMM T&S subsidiary was privatised — illustrates the importance of governance structures that protect the public mission even as commercial activity grows. [07-061]

6. **The MICMRC fills a gap in Australia's national infrastructure.** Australia is the only major mining nation among those profiled that does not operate a dedicated, government-supported, shared-use mineral processing and metallurgical testing facility focused on critical minerals. The MICMRC would bring Australia into line with the infrastructure model that peer nations have already adopted.
