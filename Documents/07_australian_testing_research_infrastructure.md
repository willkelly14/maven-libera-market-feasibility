# Australian Testing & Research Infrastructure Audit

## Purpose

This document maps Australia's existing minerals testing, characterisation, and research infrastructure to establish whether there is a domestic infrastructure gap — particularly in the North West Minerals Province (NWMP) — that the proposed Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre (MICMRC) would address. The audit covers public research agencies, university facilities, government-funded common-user facilities, and major commercial testing laboratories.

---

## 1. National Nuclear and Synchrotron Facilities (ANSTO)

### Australian Synchrotron (Clayton, VIC)

The Australian Synchrotron, operated by the Australian Nuclear Science and Technology Organisation (ANSTO) at Clayton in southeast Melbourne, is Australia's premier large-scale characterisation facility. [01-046] The facility currently operates 14 beamlines and accommodates more than 5,000 researchers and 6,000 research visits per year. [01-046] Of these beamlines, at least six are directly relevant to minerals characterisation: Powder Diffraction for crystalline phase identification, X-ray Fluorescence Microscopy for elemental mapping, X-ray Absorption Spectroscopy for oxidation state analysis, Medium Energy XAS for chemical environment analysis, and the new Advanced Diffraction and Scattering beamlines (ADS-1 and ADS-2) for high-energy X-ray diffraction and imaging of materials and minerals. [01-048]

The facility is undergoing significant expansion through Project BRIGHT, which has secured $94.1 million in funding to expand the beamline infrastructure, expanding the suite from the current beamlines to as many as 19 over the next decade. [01-047] While the Synchrotron provides world-class characterisation capabilities, it operates as a shared national facility with a proposal-based access model, and is not designed for routine commercial testing or pilot-scale mineral processing.

### ANSTO Minerals — Lucas Heights (Sydney, NSW)

ANSTO's minerals research division at Lucas Heights has received $13.9 million under the Australian Critical Minerals R&D Hub to accelerate the discovery, extraction, and processing of rare earth elements from clay-hosted and ionic adsorption deposits. [01-049] The centrepiece of this investment is a purpose-built pilot plant for processing low-grade ores from Australian clay-hosted rare earth deposits, due to be operational by Q2 2026. [01-050] This will be Australia's first government-backed rare earth processing facility.

The pilot plant will develop novel advanced separation flowsheets to reduce operating costs while achieving commercial-grade REE specifications. [01-051] According to ANSTO Minerals, the combined process know-how and testing facilities will "significantly reduce development time as well as technical and economic risk for companies." [01-051]

### Australian Critical Minerals R&D Hub

The Australian Critical Minerals R&D Hub, funded at $50.5 million and hosted by CSIRO, brings together CSIRO, ANSTO, and Geoscience Australia to scale up and commercialise critical minerals research, with $5 million allocated for international science diplomacy. [01-052] The Hub funds seven research projects across the value chain including Rare Earth Elements, High Purity Alumina, High Purity Silica/Quartz, High-Tech Metals and Materials, Critical Mineral By-Products, and Mineral Criticality Assessment. [01-053] The Hub represents the most significant coordinated effort to align Australia's public research agencies around critical minerals processing challenges.

## 2. CSIRO Mineral Resources

### Advanced Characterisation Facility

CSIRO's Mineral Resources unit is the largest minerals research and development organisation in Australia. The Mineral Processing program alone has around 100 researchers developing cutting-edge technologies, focused on critical minerals, decarbonisation, and preserving the economic prosperity of Australia's minerals industry. [01-054] CSIRO's core mineral processing capabilities span beneficiation, hydrometallurgy, pyrometallurgy, and electrochemistry.

The Advanced Characterisation Facility operates across six laboratories in Perth, Melbourne, and Adelaide, housing state-of-the-art equipment including QEMSCAN and TIMA automated mineralogy systems, XRD, XRF, electron microprobes (EPMA), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), X-ray computer tomography microscopy (HRXCT), nuclear microprobe (PIXE), and particle analysers. [01-055] The facility is primarily based at the Australian Resources Research Centre (ARRC) in Perth, with additional nodes in Melbourne laboratories, offering services and contract-based research to clients in business, industry, and government. [01-056]

### Geoscience Drill Core Research Laboratory (Perth)

In October 2021, CSIRO launched a $7 million Geoscience Drill Core Research Laboratory at the ARRC in Perth, housing the most advanced collection of instrumentation in Australia specifically assembled for the analysis of drill core specimens. [01-057] The facility features the world-leading Maia x-ray microprobe detector and imaging system, which provides high-definition, real-time elemental images of complex natural samples. [01-057]

### Advanced Resource Characterisation Facility (ARCF)

The Advanced Resource Characterisation Facility, established with a $12.4 million grant from the Science and Industry Endowment Fund (SIEF), brings together three major instruments across partner institutions in Perth: CSIRO hosts the Maia Mapper for core-scale chemical mapping, the University of Western Australia hosts the NanoSIMS for sub-micron elemental and isotopic mapping, and Curtin University hosts the Geoscience Atom Probe for sub-nanoscale characterisation. [01-058] The ARCF functions as a collaborative "one stop shop" for mineral characterisation research from core scale down to the atomic level.

## 3. University of Queensland — Sustainable Minerals Institute (SMI)

### Overview

The Sustainable Minerals Institute at the University of Queensland is the world's largest co-located minerals and resources interdisciplinary research institute, employing approximately 300 people and consistently ranked in the world's top five for mining and minerals research. [01-059] The institute spans seven specialist domains: geology, mineral processing, environmental management, health and safety, organisational effectiveness, social responsibility, and mineral security. SMI operates eight research centres, an International Centre of Excellence in Chile, and the technology transfer company JKTech. [01-060]

### Julius Kruttschnitt Mineral Research Centre (JKMRC)

The JKMRC, located at the UQ Experimental Mine Site in Indooroopilly, Brisbane, is a world-leading provider of postgraduate education and innovative applied research in mineral processing and geometallurgy. [01-060] The centre operates five primary research groups: Advanced Process Prediction and Control (APPCo), High-Temperature Processing (HTP), Energy Transition and Integration (ET&I), the Separation Group, and Flotation Chemistry. [01-061] It provides state-of-the-art pilot plant and laboratory facilities for both research and industry engagement. [01-061]

### JKTech

JKTech, SMI's technology transfer company, delivers laboratory services, specialised software, consultancy, and training to the global minerals industry. [01-062] JKTech offers industry-focused programs in comminution, flotation, process mineralogy, and statistics. [01-062] JKTech's drop weight testing is an industry standard for bankable hardness characterisation, and the proposed MICMRC facility plans to install JKTech-calibrated equipment for this purpose.

## 4. Queensland Resources Common User Facility (QRCUF)

### Overview

The Queensland Resources Common User Facility (QRCUF), located at Cleveland Bay Industrial Park in Townsville, is Australia's first shared mineral processing facility. [01-063] The project was established under a $150 million Queensland Government commitment announced in the 2022-2023 State Budget Update for common user infrastructure to support the development, extraction, and production of critical minerals. [01-064] Sedgman Pty Ltd, a Queensland-based CIMIC Group member, was appointed as managing contractor for design and delivery. [01-068]

### Capabilities and Focus

The QRCUF will enable mineral producers to trial production processes, research intermediate and end products, assess potential by-products and waste streams, and produce samples for investor attraction and off-take agreements. [01-065] Importantly, the QRCUF will not be used for any commercial operations or production — it is a testing and demonstration facility only. [01-065] The initial focus is vanadium processing, supporting Queensland's significant vanadium reserves (approximately 30% of global reserves, concentrated in the Julia Creek and Richmond regions). [01-066] The facility will have capacity to expand to encompass processing of other critical minerals including cobalt and rare earth elements. [01-066]

### Timeline and Economic Impact

Operations are expected to commence in late 2026. [01-063] The project is expected to generate more than 80 local jobs during construction and over 40 new local jobs once operational, supporting North Queensland's estimated $500 billion in critical mineral reserves. [01-067]

### Significance and Limitations

Queensland Resources Minister Dale Last stated that "The Queensland Resources Common User Facility will put Queensland at the forefront of resources innovation." [01-063] However, the QRCUF is located in coastal Townsville, approximately 900 km by road from Mount Isa, limiting its practical accessibility for NWMP-based operations. [01-079] Its initial vanadium-only focus also means it will not address the broader minerals testing needs of the province's diverse resource base.

### Tasmania — Emerging Common User Facility

Tasmania has also commenced planning for a common user processing facility, with the Tasmanian Government signing a federal funding agreement in October 2025 to carry out a feasibility study focused on northwest Tasmania using tin and tungsten as a working example, with findings expected to be delivered in 2026. [01-069] This development indicates growing national recognition that shared mineral processing infrastructure is needed in regional mining areas.

## 5. Western Australian Facilities

### Curtin University — WA School of Mines (WASM)

Curtin University's WA School of Mines, which ranks 5th in the world for Mineral and Mining Engineering (QS 2025), operates metallurgy and mining facilities across two campuses in Perth and Kalgoorlie-Boulder. [01-070] The Kalgoorlie campus houses a Comminution Laboratory capable of particle size reduction from 100 mm to ultrafine sizes, a Mineral Processing Laboratory with flotation columns and cells, hydrocyclones, and various separators for mineral separation based on surface chemistry, density, magnetism, conductivity, and particle size. [01-071] Chemical laboratories support hydrometallurgical processes including leaching, solution purification, and metal recovery, while high-temperature furnaces enable calcination and roasting work. [01-071]

WASM's Kalgoorlie campus also hosts the former CRC ORE "Kal Hub," providing an integrated research environment in the heart of Western Australia's gold and nickel mining belt. The Micro-Computed Tomography Laboratory, funded as part of the $48.4 million National Geosequestration Laboratory, provides 3D x-ray imaging at resolutions up to 700 nm.

### Murdoch University — Extractive Metallurgy Hub (Rockingham, WA)

Murdoch University launched an Extractive Metallurgy Hub in Rockingham, Western Australia, near Rio Tinto's green iron plant. [01-072] The hub focuses on extraction, processing, and recycling of critical minerals essential to battery technologies and green steel production, specialising in lithium, nickel, cobalt, rare earths, and vanadium from both primary sources (ores) and secondary sources (electronic waste, used batteries, mining residues). [01-073] Industry partners include Rio Tinto, IGO, Lynas, Tronox, and POSCO. [01-073]

### University of Adelaide — Critical Minerals Research Centre

The University of Adelaide established a Critical Minerals Research Centre within its Institute for Mineral and Energy Resources (IMER), covering end-to-end critical minerals research and education from prospectivity analyses to resource definition to mineral processing. [01-074] The Centre partners with BHP, Glencore, OZ Minerals, and over 20 METS companies.

### Commercial Laboratories — Perth Concentration

The commercial minerals testing sector is heavily concentrated in Perth. Intertek's Minerals Global Centre of Excellence, opened in August 2021, is the largest Intertek minerals laboratory in the world at 20,000 square metres, housing over 500 employees and eight robotic automated laboratory systems. [01-075] ALS operates a major hydrometallurgy centre of excellence in Perth. Bureau Veritas maintains extensive operations across Australia providing pit-to-port services for a wide range of resources and commodities.

## 6. Geographic Gap Analysis

### Infrastructure Distribution

Australia's minerals testing and research infrastructure is overwhelmingly concentrated in the southern and western capital cities. [01-077] The major facilities map as follows:

| City | Key Facilities |
|------|---------------|
| **Perth** | CSIRO ARRC, ARCF, Curtin WASM, Intertek CoE, ALS Hydromet, Murdoch Hub |
| **Melbourne** | Australian Synchrotron, CSIRO Clayton |
| **Brisbane** | UQ SMI/JKMRC, JKTech |
| **Adelaide** | CSIRO characterisation node, University of Adelaide IMER |
| **Sydney** | ANSTO Lucas Heights |
| **Kalgoorlie** | Curtin WASM campus |
| **Townsville** | QRCUF (under construction, late 2026) |

No advanced research or pilot-scale processing facility is located in inland or northern Australia outside Kalgoorlie and the under-construction Townsville facility. [01-077]

### The NWMP Gap

The North West Minerals Province — one of the world's richest mineral-producing regions — has no advanced mineral processing research, testing, or pilot-scale facility. [01-076] The only commercial laboratory presence is an ALS Minerals geochemistry and assay facility at Enterprise Road, Mount Isa, which provides basic geochemistry services but not advanced metallurgical testing, mineral characterisation, or pilot-scale processing. [01-076]

The nearest advanced mineral processing research facility is the QRCUF in Townsville, approximately 900 km by road, which will not be operational until late 2026 and focuses initially on vanadium processing only. [01-079] The nearest comprehensive metallurgical testing facilities are in Brisbane (UQ SMI, approximately 1,800 km) or Perth (approximately 3,900 km).

### Impact on Project Development

This geographic gap means that NWMP-based exploration and mining companies must ship drill core and ore samples thousands of kilometres to coastal laboratories for metallurgical testing, adding weeks to turnaround times and significant freight costs. For time-sensitive feasibility studies and environmental assessments, this logistical burden directly slows project development timelines and increases costs.

### Planned Facilities Elsewhere

Of 130 critical minerals major projects across Australia in 2025, 65 are in Western Australia and 22 in Queensland. [01-080] Despite this concentration of projects in remote regions, the two common-user mineral processing facilities under development — the QRCUF in Townsville and the ANSTO REE pilot plant in Sydney — are both in coastal cities, not in the inland mining regions where projects are actually situated. [01-080]

The NWMP Strategic Blueprint ($39 million, 2017) identified the need for common-user infrastructure but did not establish any minerals testing or research facilities in the region. [01-078] Tasmania has commenced a feasibility study for its own common-user facility, reinforcing the national trend toward recognising regional processing infrastructure gaps. [01-069]

## 7. Summary and Implications for MICMRC

### Key Findings

Australia possesses world-class minerals testing and research capabilities, but these are concentrated in a small number of urban centres, predominantly Perth, Melbourne, Brisbane, and Sydney. The infrastructure audit reveals:

1. **Characterisation excellence exists but is centralised.** CSIRO's Advanced Characterisation Facility (Perth/Melbourne/Adelaide), the Australian Synchrotron (Melbourne), and ANSTO (Sydney) provide advanced analytical capabilities, but all require samples to be shipped to capital cities. [01-055] [01-046] [01-050]

2. **Pilot-scale processing facilities are emerging but coastal.** The QRCUF (Townsville, late 2026) and ANSTO's REE pilot plant (Sydney, Q2 2026) are the first government-funded common-user processing facilities, but both are in coastal locations. [01-063] [01-050]

3. **University research is world-leading but urban.** UQ SMI/JKMRC (Brisbane), Curtin WASM (Perth/Kalgoorlie), Murdoch (Rockingham), and University of Adelaide (Adelaide) all maintain substantial minerals research capabilities — none in the inland mining provinces. [01-059] [01-070] [01-072] [01-074]

4. **Commercial testing is Perth-dominated.** Intertek, ALS, and Bureau Veritas all concentrate their advanced capabilities in Perth, with only basic geochemistry services available in Mount Isa. [01-075] [01-076]

5. **The NWMP has no advanced testing infrastructure.** Despite hosting one of Australia's richest mineral provinces, the NWMP has no mineral processing pilot plant, no advanced characterisation facility, no metallurgical testing laboratory, and no university-linked research capability. [01-076] [01-077]

### The Case for MICMRC

A minerals research and testing centre in Mount Isa would be the first facility of its kind in the NWMP, positioned to serve a region with an estimated $500 billion in critical mineral reserves. [01-067] It would address a clear infrastructure gap by bringing bench-scale testing, mineral characterisation, demonstration-scale processing, and industry training directly to the mining province — reducing the current dependence on distant coastal laboratories and accelerating the pathway from exploration to production for critical minerals projects in the region.
