# Critical Minerals Price Outlook & Investment Cycle

## Executive Summary

The critical minerals sector is at a pivotal juncture. Following the sharp price surges of 2021-2022, most battery and strategic minerals have experienced substantial price corrections, with lithium falling over 80% from its peak and nickel, cobalt, and graphite each declining 10-20% in 2024 [01-251]. Yet beneath this cyclical downturn, structural demand drivers remain firmly intact: the IEA projects lithium demand will grow fivefold to 2040, graphite and nickel demand will double, and cobalt and rare earth demand will increase 50-60% [01-252]. This creates a tension between today's depressed investment signals and tomorrow's supply requirements — a tension that government incentive mechanisms across allied nations are increasingly designed to bridge.

For the MICMRC feasibility study, this price and investment cycle analysis demonstrates that: (1) the current price downturn is creating a window in which mineral processors and researchers can establish capacity ahead of the next upcycle; (2) government incentives — particularly Australia's AU$7 billion Critical Minerals Production Tax Incentive [01-279] — are explicitly designed to sustain investment through bear markets; and (3) the structural supply gaps projected across multiple commodities by the 2030s will drive sustained demand for processing research and testing infrastructure.

## 1. Market-Wide Price Trends and Investment Context

### 1.1 The Post-Boom Correction (2022-2025)

The critical minerals market experienced a dramatic boom-bust cycle between 2020 and 2025. Pandemic-era supply chain disruptions, combined with accelerating EV adoption, drove prices for battery minerals to historic peaks in 2021-2022. Lithium prices surged eightfold during this period before collapsing over 80% as new supply, particularly from Australia, Chile, and China, flooded the market [01-251]. Graphite, cobalt, and nickel prices each declined 10-20% in 2024, with most battery minerals returning to pre-pandemic price levels [01-251].

This price correction has had tangible consequences for investment. The IEA reports that investment momentum in critical mineral development weakened in 2024, with spending rising by just 5% — down from 14% in 2023 — and adjusted for cost inflation, real investment growth was just 2% [01-249]. Exploration activity plateaued in 2024, marking a pause in the upward trend observed since 2020 [01-250]. Critically, while exploration spending continued to rise for lithium, uranium, and copper, it declined notably for nickel, cobalt, and zinc [01-250].

The Reserve Bank of Australia observes that critical minerals currently represent only about 6% of Australia's resource exports, projected to reach approximately 10% by 2030 [01-278]. Low prices during 2024 halted production at some Australian mines and delayed several late-stage projects, particularly in lithium and nickel [01-278].

### 1.2 Capital Requirements and the Investment Gap

The scale of capital required to meet projected demand is substantial. The IEA estimates that around USD 500 billion in new mining capital investment is needed between now and 2040 under the Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS), rising to USD 600 billion under the more ambitious Announced Pledges Scenario [01-253]. Today's low mineral prices are not providing the investment signal needed to attract this capital, with projects involving new entrants most affected by uncertainty.

The disconnect between future demand and current investment is stark. Resource Capital Funds estimates that to meet electric vehicle battery demands by 2030 alone, the world needs 50 additional lithium mines (a six-fold increase), 60 additional nickel mines (double current production), and 17 additional cobalt mines (a 75% increase) [01-277]. The correlation between commodity prices and mining investment spending is approximately 73% over the past decade [01-276], meaning the current price downturn is directly suppressing the exploration and development activity needed to prevent future supply deficits. Mining projects take 3-5 years to fund, construct, and ramp, with over 15 years on average from initial discovery to production [01-276].

### 1.3 The Price-Investment Cycle: Historical Patterns

The mining industry is characterised by pronounced procyclicality. Periods of high commodity prices drive surges in exploration expenditure and capital investment, while downturns — such as the current one — lead to sharp cutbacks. This procyclicality creates a structural tendency toward boom-bust dynamics: underinvestment during bear markets sows the seeds of future supply deficits, which in turn drive the next price spike.

Australia's exploration expenditure data (ABS 8412.0) demonstrates this pattern clearly. Supply chain disruptions during the pandemic elevated commodity prices, spurring drilling activity to a peak of over 12.5 million metres in 2021-22. As prices softened and inflationary pressures mounted, drilling activity retreated sharply over the following two years. Over the past five years, gold exploration expenditure reached a record $1,068 million in 2019, driven by elevated precious metal prices. The minerals boom that began in 2003 saw a sharp increase in commodity prices followed by unprecedented investment — at its peak in 2012, resources investment accounted for 60% of total investment in Australia.

A fivefold price spike in lithium, nickel, and graphite would increase battery pack costs by 20% globally, while a tenfold increase would raise costs by 40-50%, demonstrating how price volatility in these minerals directly impacts the economics of the energy transition [01-290].

## 2. Commodity-by-Commodity Price Outlook

### 2.1 Lithium

**Current position in cycle:** Trough / early recovery

Lithium experienced the most dramatic boom-bust cycle of any critical mineral. After surging eightfold in 2021-2022, prices collapsed over 80% [01-251], with lithium carbonate falling below US$9,550/mt in early 2025. Goldman Sachs forecasts battery-grade lithium carbonate climbing to US$11,000/mt in 2025 and US$13,250/mt in 2026, driven by rising EV penetration and the exit of high-cost producers [01-256].

**Supply-demand outlook:** Near-term markets appear well-supplied following the rapid expansion of Australian spodumene, Chilean brine, and Chinese lepidolite production. However, the IEA warns that rapidly growing demand is expected to push the market into deficit by the 2030s, with supply from announced projects covering only approximately 60% of requirements — a 40% shortfall [01-255]. JPMorgan projects global lithium demand to grow 16% year-over-year in 2026, with 58% from EVs and 30% from energy storage systems [01-291]. The IEA's STEPS scenario projects lithium demand growing fivefold from today to 2040 [01-252].

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Gradual recovery from 2025-2026 lows toward US$15,000-20,000/mt by the late 2020s as surplus narrows and structural deficits emerge. Long-term structural demand from EVs and energy storage supports pricing once supply deficits materialise around 2028-2032.

### 2.2 Nickel

**Current position in cycle:** Bear market / oversupply

Nickel remains in a prolonged bear market driven by massive Indonesian supply expansion. The International Nickel Study Group projects a 2025 market surplus of approximately 198,000 tonnes, with primary output of 3.735 million tonnes against consumption of 3.537 million tonnes [01-257]. Nickel traded at around US$14,900/tonne as of mid-2025, down from a March 2022 peak of US$45,795/tonne.

**Analyst consensus:** ING forecasts US$15,700/t, S&P Global US$15,730/t, and Sucden Financial US$14,600-15,600/t for 2025 [01-258]. Wood Mackenzie projects the market will not return to structural equilibrium until around 2030, with UBS expecting the surplus to persist through 2026 and beyond [01-259]. Indonesia's continued expansion — adding an estimated 8.5% to global production — is the dominant supply-side factor.

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Gradual recovery toward US$18,000-22,000/t by 2030 as Indonesian growth moderates and battery-grade nickel demand accelerates. Stainless steel demand (4-5% annual growth) and battery-grade demand (5.7% increase in 2025) provide support, but rebalancing is not expected before 2029-2030.

### 2.3 Cobalt

**Current position in cycle:** Trough / pre-deficit transition

Cobalt prices hit nine-year lows in early 2024, with cobalt hydroxide falling 15% and cobalt metal dropping 22% during the year. Global demand reached 222,000 tonnes in 2024, with battery applications accounting for 76% of total demand and 94% of demand growth [01-260].

**Supply-demand outlook:** The cobalt market is projected to shift from surplus to potential deficit by the early 2030s [01-261]. Demand is forecast to reach 400,000 tonnes by the early 2030s, growing at a 7% CAGR as battery demand outpaces supply growth [01-261]. The DRC maintains 76% of mined output (projected to decline to 65% by 2030), while Indonesia's share is expected to nearly double from 12% to 22% [01-260].

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Recovery from current lows as the surplus narrows through the late 2020s, with significant price appreciation likely once structural deficit conditions emerge in the early 2030s. A 60% price rebound was observed in 2025 following the DRC's export ban, demonstrating the market's sensitivity to supply disruptions.

### 2.4 Rare Earth Elements

**Current position in cycle:** Recovery / strategic premium emerging

Rare earth markets entered 2025 with positive momentum, driven by rising permanent magnet demand. NdPr oxide prices surged over 40% year-to-date in late 2025, with weekly gains of 13.6% for oxide and 14% for alloy, signalling a deepening scarcity premium [01-262]. The global rare earth elements market was valued at USD 3.95 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 6.28 billion by 2030, growing at a CAGR of 8.6% [01-264].

**Strategic pricing dynamics:** The US Pentagon has established a pricing floor of US$110/kg for NdPr in defence procurement [01-263], while China's export controls on dysprosium and terbium have reignited supply chain fears. Each electric vehicle requires approximately 200-400 grams of NdPr, and cumulative NdPr demand growth is projected at 300% over the next decade [01-262].

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Structurally higher prices driven by EV and wind turbine magnet demand, defence procurement support, and constrained ex-China supply. Neodymium accounts for the highest revenue share (30.3%) of the rare earths market and is expected to see sustained demand growth through 2035 [01-264].

### 2.5 Graphite

**Current position in cycle:** Trough / all-time lows

Graphite is the most depressed of the battery minerals. Flake graphite prices reached all-time lows by late 2025, with battery-grade flake graphite (94%, -100 mesh) at US$623/mt FOB Shanghai in Q1 2025 [01-271]. Oversupply, weak demand, and competition from synthetic graphite drove prices to historic lows, with significant Chinese capacity coming offline.

**Supply-demand outlook:** Despite current oversupply, graphite demand is projected to rise 310% by 2036, driven by EV battery production [01-272]. Natural flake graphite demand is forecast to grow from approximately 1.2 million tonnes in 2025 to almost 3 million tonnes by 2035 [01-272]. China controls approximately 80% of battery-grade graphite production and over 90% of battery-grade processing.

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Recovery from current lows expected by 2027-2028 as excess capacity exits and EV demand accelerates. Under the IEA's STEPS scenario, graphite demand doubles by 2040 [01-252]. The extreme supply concentration in China presents significant upside price risk from export restrictions or trade disputes.

### 2.6 Manganese

**Current position in cycle:** Flat / transition to battery demand

Manganese ore prices started 2025 flat, with production climbing to around 10.1 million metric tons in H1 2025 as South Africa, Australia, and Gabon resumed full shipments following earlier disruptions [01-273]. The traditional steel-making demand base (manganese is essential for steel production) is supplemented by growing battery applications.

**Battery-grade market:** The electrolytic manganese metal (EMM) market was valued at USD 2,177 million in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 3,063 million by 2032, growing at a CAGR of 4.5% [01-274]. China accounts for 70% of global EMM production [01-274]. The market for electrolytic manganese dioxide (EMD), essential for lithium-ion batteries, has grown by over 25% in the past two years.

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Modest price appreciation driven by growing battery-grade demand, with South Africa (40% of global ore production), Australia, and Gabon providing the majority of supply. The battery segment represents the primary growth driver for premium manganese products.

### 2.7 Vanadium

**Current position in cycle:** Depressed / awaiting VRFB demand inflection

Vanadium pentoxide traded in the range of US$9,300-13,000/mt across the US, China, and Europe in H1 2025 [01-269]. CRU Group projects prices to recover from late 2026 as supply tightens and vanadium redox flow battery (VRFB) demand grows.

**VRFB demand catalyst:** Global vanadium demand from VRFBs is forecast to reach 15,000 tonnes in 2025, up from less than 1,000 tonnes in 2020 — a transformational shift in the demand profile [01-270]. China's new rebar standards introduced in 2025 mandate higher vanadium intensity in steel, providing additional demand support.

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Recovery expected from late 2026, with VRFB deployment providing a structural new demand driver. The vanadium electrolyte market is projected to grow at a CAGR of 23% from 2025 to 2032, potentially transforming vanadium from a steel additive to a strategic energy storage mineral.

### 2.8 Tungsten

**Current position in cycle:** Recovery / supply-constrained

Tungsten APT prices have rebounded strongly from US$312/MTU in 2023 to US$415/MTU in early 2025, with forecasts projecting US$400-450/MTU as the new price floor through 2030 [01-265]. China accounts for approximately 82% of primary global supply but faces declining ore grades at state-owned mines [01-266].

**Supply dynamics:** December 2024 Chinese export restrictions targeting US dual-use technology applications have accelerated supply chain diversification [01-266]. Non-Chinese supply is emerging from Spain, Portugal, South Korea (Sangdong mine, ~7% of global supply upon 2025-2026 ramp-up), and Australia (Mt. Carbine under EQ Resources).

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Structurally tight market through 2030 driven by military restocking, Chinese resource depletion, and constrained new supply development. Industrial demand growth of approximately 1.3% CAGR through 2029, with defence applications providing a strategic demand floor [01-265].

### 2.9 Antimony

**Current position in cycle:** Supply crisis / record highs

Antimony has experienced the most dramatic market dislocation of any critical mineral. Prices surged from US$1,400/mt in July 2024 to US$38,000/mt in September 2024 — a 2,600% increase — following China's export restrictions [01-267]. By mid-2025, prices reached US$57,000-60,000/mt, the sharpest rally ever recorded in antimony markets since records began in 1980 [01-267].

**Supply crisis:** China's antimony production has declined from 100,000 tonnes in 2000 to 40,000 tonnes in 2024, with worldwide production at only 83,000 tonnes [01-268]. China's export restrictions, effective September 2024, resulted in an 80% drop in exports compared to 2024 levels. European stockpiles dwindled to critically low levels by early 2025, with some manufacturers reporting less than 60 days of inventory.

**Consensus price forecast (5-10 year):** Prices expected to remain elevated (US$25,000-40,000/mt range) as new non-Chinese supply takes years to develop. Strategic demand from defence (munitions, night-vision equipment), flame retardants, and semiconductors provides a price floor. The US Pentagon's stockpiling program includes US$245 million for antimony [01-288].

## 3. The Price-Investment Cycle and Exploration Expenditure

### 3.1 Historical Correlation

The mining industry exhibits a well-documented 73% correlation between commodity prices and investment spending over the past decade [01-276]. This procyclicality means that when prices fall — as they have across most critical minerals since 2022 — exploration and development spending contracts precisely when it needs to expand to prevent future supply deficits.

Australia's experience illustrates this pattern vividly. The mining boom that began in 2003 saw commodity prices drive unprecedented investment, with resources investment reaching 60% of total Australian investment at its peak in 2012. When the boom ended, exploration budgets were slashed. The pandemic-era price surge then drove drilling activity to a new peak of over 12.5 million metres in 2021-22, only to retreat again as prices softened.

### 3.2 Where We Are Now

The current period (2024-2026) represents a classic mid-cycle trough for most critical minerals. Prices are depressed, exploration activity has plateaued [01-250], and investment growth has slowed to just 2% in real terms [01-249]. This is occurring against a backdrop of rising structural demand that multiple authoritative bodies — the IEA, World Bank, and major investment banks — agree will drive significant supply deficits by the 2030s.

The World Bank's Commodity Markets Outlook (October 2025) projects copper and tin to reach new record highs in nominal US dollar terms by 2027 [01-275], while base metal markets are expected to remain "relatively tight through 2027." Global defence budgets rose 9% year-over-year in 2024 (compared to 2.7% average growth from 2017-2022), with NATO European countries projected to spend nearly 3% of GDP on defence by 2030 [01-289], adding a strategic demand dimension beyond the energy transition.

### 3.3 Implications for Processing Infrastructure

The current investment trough creates both a challenge and an opportunity for mineral processing infrastructure such as the MICMRC. The challenge is that depressed prices reduce near-term revenue potential. The opportunity is threefold:

First, establishing processing research capability during a price trough means infrastructure will be operational before the next upcycle, positioning the centre to capture demand from new projects that will be fast-tracked when prices recover.

Second, government incentive mechanisms are explicitly designed to sustain investment through bear markets (see Section 4), providing financial support to bridge the gap between today's depressed prices and tomorrow's structural demand.

Third, the lead times in mining — 3-5 years for construction, over 15 years from discovery to production [01-276] — mean that research and testing infrastructure must be established well ahead of when it will be most needed. Projects initiated during the current trough will come to fruition precisely as supply deficits emerge in the late 2020s and early 2030s.

## 4. Government Incentive Mechanisms

### 4.1 Australian Federal Incentives

#### Critical Minerals Production Tax Incentive (CMPTI)

The centrepiece of Australia's critical minerals incentive architecture is the CMPTI, enacted on 14 February 2025 via the *Future Made in Australia (Production Tax Credits and Other Measures) Act 2025* [01-280]. The incentive provides a 10% refundable tax offset on eligible processing and refining costs for Australia's 31 critical minerals, available for up to 10 years per project between 1 July 2027 and 30 June 2040 [01-279]. The Federal Budget estimates the CMPTI at AU$7 billion over the decade [01-279].

Critically, the CMPTI covers "processing and refining activities that substantially transform a feedstock containing a critical mineral through extractive metallurgical processing into a purer or more refined form" [01-280]. Mining and beneficiation are excluded, meaning the incentive specifically targets the downstream value-adding that a centre like the MICMRC would support through R&D and testing services.

#### R&D Tax Incentive

Australia's R&D Tax Incentive provides eligible entities with turnover under $20 million a refundable tax offset of 18.5 percentage points above the company tax rate (effectively 43.5%) [01-281]. However, most routine mineral exploration activities are excluded if their purpose is discovering or delineating deposits [01-281]. Developing new exploration technologies or processing methods does qualify, making this incentive directly relevant to mineral processing R&D activities at a facility like the MICMRC.

#### Junior Minerals Exploration Incentive (JMEI)

The JMEI, which operated from 2017-2024, provided $190 million in tax credits that generated a net-positive impact of $769 million in GDP value [01-282]. For every $1 spent on JMEI, $6.38 in capital raising and $2.41 in exploration activity was generated [01-282] — demonstrating the powerful multiplier effect of well-designed exploration incentives. The program's non-extension has been criticised as a "missed opportunity" for mineral security, though the Opposition has committed $100 million to reinstate it.

#### Northern Australia Infrastructure Facility (NAIF)

NAIF has earmarked $500 million for critical minerals projects under the Critical Minerals Strategy 2023-2030, with total investments reaching $4.4 billion across 32 projects in northern Australia [01-283]. Notable investments include $200 million for the Alpha HPA Project near Gladstone and $125 million for Pilbara Minerals' Pilgangoora lithium expansion.

#### Resourcing Australia's Prosperity

The Australian Government is investing $566.1 million over 10 years (part of a broader $3.4 billion commitment over 35 years) through Geoscience Australia to comprehensively map all 36 critical and strategic minerals [01-284]. This precompetitive geoscience data programme reduces exploration risk and supports private sector discovery — a critical upstream enabler for mineral processing demand.

#### Critical Minerals Strategic Reserve (CMSR)

Australia is establishing a CMSR with AU$1.2 billion in funding, comprising AU$1 billion for new transactions from the expanded AU$5 billion Critical Minerals Facility and AU$185 million for targeted stockpiling [01-285]. The Reserve initially focuses on antimony, gallium, and rare earths, and will operate by securing rights to minerals produced in Australia and on-selling those rights to meet allied demand.

### 4.2 International Comparisons

#### United States: Inflation Reduction Act Section 45X

The US Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit provides a 10% credit on production costs for critical minerals processed to specified purity levels (typically 99%+) within the United States [01-286]. This mirrors Australia's CMPTI in both rate (10%) and focus (processing rather than mining), reflecting convergent policy thinking across allied nations. The US has also committed nearly US$1 billion through the Department of Energy to critical mineral supply chains, with US$2.8 billion in approved loan guarantees [01-263].

The US Department of Defense is targeting a US$1 billion critical minerals stockpile, with specific allocations including cobalt ($500 million), antimony ($245 million), and tantalum ($100 million) [01-288]. US lawmakers have separately introduced legislation for a US$2.5 billion critical minerals stockpile [01-292].

#### Canada: Critical Mineral Exploration Tax Credit (CMETC)

Canada's CMETC provides a non-refundable 30% tax credit on specified mineral exploration expenses through flow-through shares, covering nickel, cobalt, lithium, graphite, copper, rare earths, vanadium, and other critical minerals [01-287]. Budget 2025 expanded eligibility to include bismuth, cesium, chromium, fluorspar, germanium, indium, manganese, molybdenum, niobium, tantalum, tin, and tungsten. Canada's approach of incentivising exploration (upstream) complements Australia's focus on processing (downstream).

#### European Union: Critical Raw Materials Act

The EU CRMA has allocated US$8.3 billion with a target of 10% domestic rare earth sourcing by 2030 [01-288]. Private capital deployment across 23 rare earth projects totalled US$4.7 billion in 2024-2025, indicating that public incentives are catalysing significant private investment.

### 4.3 Incentive Effectiveness Across Market Conditions

Government incentives play a qualitatively different role in bull versus bear markets:

**In bull markets** (2021-2022), private capital flows freely into mineral development, and government incentives have a marginal additive effect on investment levels that would largely occur anyway.

**In bear markets** (2024-2026), government incentives become critical to maintaining investment continuity. The CMPTI's 10% production offset, NAIF's concessional financing, and strategic reserve purchasing all serve to sustain project viability when market prices alone would not support development. The JMEI's demonstrated multiplier — $6.38 in capital raising per $1 of government support [01-282] — illustrates how modest public expenditure can sustain exploration activity through price downturns.

**The counter-cyclical case for MICMRC:** The convergence of bear market pricing, strong government incentive programmes, and projected structural deficits creates an ideal environment for establishing mineral processing research infrastructure. Government-supported investment in testing and processing R&D during the current trough will position Australia to capture value from the next upcycle, which multiple authoritative forecasters project will emerge by the late 2020s to early 2030s.

## 5. Synthesis and Outlook

### 5.1 The Paradox of Current Markets

The critical minerals market presents a paradox: the long-term demand outlook has never been more compelling, yet short-term prices are at multi-year lows. The IEA projects demand growth of 2-5x across key minerals by 2040 [01-252]. The World Bank expects copper and tin to reach new record highs [01-275]. JPMorgan projects 16% annual lithium demand growth [01-291]. Defence budgets are rising at the fastest rate in decades [01-289]. Yet investment growth has slowed to just 2% in real terms [01-249], exploration has plateaued [01-250], and Australian mines are closing [01-278].

### 5.2 When Supply Deficits Will Bite

The timeline for supply deficits varies by mineral:

- **Antimony:** Already in crisis; deficit ongoing [01-267]
- **Copper:** Potential 30% shortfall by 2035 [01-254]
- **Lithium:** Deficit expected by late 2020s/early 2030s [01-255]
- **Cobalt:** Shift to deficit by early 2030s [01-261]
- **Rare earths (NdPr):** 30% deficit on current ex-China production capacity [01-262]
- **Nickel:** Surplus persisting until approximately 2030 [01-259]
- **Vanadium:** Recovery from late 2026 as VRFB demand grows [01-269]
- **Graphite:** Recovery expected 2027-2028 as excess capacity exits [01-271]
- **Tungsten:** Structurally tight through 2030 [01-265]
- **Manganese:** Moderate growth driven by battery-grade demand [01-274]

### 5.3 Strategic Implications for MICMRC

This analysis supports the case for the MICMRC in several ways:

1. **Timing advantage:** Establishing during a price trough means the centre will be operational before the next supply crunch, with infrastructure in place to serve increased processing demand.

2. **Government alignment:** Australia's AU$7 billion CMPTI [01-279], AU$1.2 billion CMSR [01-285], AU$500 million NAIF critical minerals allocation [01-283], and AU$566 million geoscience mapping programme [01-284] all directly support downstream mineral processing — the core function that MICMRC would enable.

3. **Counter-cyclical resilience:** Government incentives are specifically designed to sustain investment through bear markets, providing revenue support for processing R&D during periods of depressed commodity prices.

4. **Multi-commodity diversification:** The MICMRC's coverage of multiple minerals (lithium, nickel, cobalt, rare earths, manganese, vanadium) provides natural hedge against single-commodity price risk, ensuring demand for testing services across different commodity cycles.

5. **Allied nation demand:** With the US, EU, Canada, and Australia all implementing parallel critical minerals incentive programmes — collectively representing over US$20 billion in government commitments [01-288] [01-286] [01-287] [01-292] — the demand for Australian mineral processing and testing capacity is underwritten by coordinated allied policy action.
