# Recycling, Circular Economy & Secondary Supply

## Introduction

As global demand for critical minerals accelerates — driven by the energy transition, electrification, and strategic supply chain diversification — secondary supply from recycling and waste reprocessing is emerging as an essential complement to primary mining. This document examines the current and projected contribution of recycling to the supply of minerals relevant to Queensland's North West Minerals Province (NWMP), assesses the rapidly evolving battery recycling industry, evaluates tailings reprocessing opportunities specific to the NWMP, and profiles Queensland's circular economy research and policy initiatives. For the proposed Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre (MICMRC), understanding these dynamics is critical: the centre's bench-scale metallurgical testing, hydrometallurgy, and beneficiation capabilities are directly applicable to developing recycling flowsheets and optimising secondary recovery processes.

## 1. Current Recycling Rates by Mineral

The contribution of recycling to global mineral supply varies enormously across NWMP-relevant commodities, reflecting differences in product lifetimes, collection infrastructure, processing economics, and end-use applications.

**Lead** stands as the exemplar of successful metals recycling. Lead-acid batteries maintain a 99% recycling rate in the United States, making them the most recycled consumer product in America, with more than 160 million batteries recycled annually [01-343]. A typical new lead battery is comprised of at least 80% recycled material, and the lead in lead batteries is infinitely recyclable with no loss of performance [01-344]. This success is underpinned by mature collection infrastructure, core-charge incentive systems, and well-established pyrometallurgical processing routes. Lead's recycling model demonstrates what is achievable when regulatory frameworks, economic incentives, and processing technology align.

**Copper** has a substantial but declining recycling contribution. More than 30% of global copper demand has been met with recycled copper over the last decade [01-341]. However, the IEA reports that the share of secondary copper supply fell from 37% in 2015 to 33% in 2023, indicating that recycled materials have not kept pace with the rapid growth in copper consumption driven by electrification and renewable energy deployment [01-340].

**Zinc** recycling accounts for approximately 30% of global supply [01-345]. Like copper, zinc benefits from well-established recycling routes through galvanised steel scrap and brass recycling, though the recycling rate has remained relatively stable rather than growing.

**Nickel** follows a similar pattern of declining secondary share despite high absolute recycling volumes. The recycled share of nickel supply decreased from 35% to 31% between 2015 and 2023 [01-342]. Most secondary nickel is recovered as nickel content in stainless steel scrap rather than as battery-grade material.

**Cobalt** presents a mixed picture. Production of recycled battery metals has seen rapid growth, with recovery rates surging to over 40% for nickel and cobalt relative to theoretically available feedstock in 2023 [01-348]. In China, recycled cobalt now supplies nearly 30% of domestic battery-grade cobalt requirements, and the global cobalt scrap recycling market was valued at USD 1.44 billion in 2024, projected to reach USD 3.48 billion by 2032 [01-377].

**Vanadium** has one of the highest recycling contributions among critical raw materials. The end-of-life recycling input rate of vanadium in the European Union amounts to 44%, driven primarily by recovery from spent industrial catalysts and steel slag — both of which are well-established secondary streams [01-347].

**Rare earth elements** represent the most acute recycling challenge. Less than 1% of rare earth magnets currently come from recycled sources globally, with separation economics and lack of dedicated collection infrastructure presenting major barriers to scaling [01-346]. This is despite magnetic REEs making up around 30% of overall REE volume but capturing more than 80% of the value [01-363].

**Lithium** recycling has grown rapidly from a near-zero base, with recovery rates reaching 20% of theoretically available feedstock in 2023, though secondary lithium still represents a marginal share of total global supply due to rapid demand growth [01-348].

## 2. Recycling Projections and Impact on Mining Investment

The IEA's landmark 2024 report on recycling of critical minerals provides the most comprehensive projections of how secondary supply could reshape mineral markets over the coming decades.

In the IEA's Announced Pledges Scenario, recycling reduces new mine development needs by 40% for copper and cobalt, and by 25% for lithium and nickel by 2050 [01-349]. By 2050, battery recycling could meet 20-30% of demand for lithium, nickel, and cobalt, while secondary copper supply could rise to nearly 40% of total demand [01-351]. Without the uptake of recycling and reuse, mining capital investment requirements through 2040 would need to be approximately one-third higher, representing hundreds of billions of dollars in additional investment [01-350].

The environmental case for recycling is equally compelling. Recycled energy transition minerals such as nickel, cobalt, and lithium incur 80% less greenhouse gas emissions than primary materials produced from mining, making secondary supply a key pathway to reducing the carbon footprint of the energy transition itself [01-352].

The market value of recycled battery metals experienced 11-fold growth between 2015 and 2023, with 40% of that growth occurring in the final three years [01-353]. The IEA projects the recycled mineral market could reach USD 200 billion by 2050, representing a major new economic sector [01-353].

However, these projections carry important caveats. Despite growing policy ambitions, the use of recycled materials has failed to keep pace with rising material consumption for most minerals. More than 30 new recycling policy measures have been introduced globally in the last three years, but only 3 of 22 surveyed countries and regions have comprehensive recycling frameworks [01-355]. E-waste collection rates vary dramatically: 40-50% in Europe and North America, 30% in Japan and Korea, below 5% in developing Asia and Latin America, and only 1% in Africa [01-376].

## 3. Battery Recycling: A Transformative Industry

The lithium-ion battery recycling sector is experiencing rapid growth as the first generation of electric vehicle batteries approaches end of life, and as regulatory mandates create structured demand for recycled materials.

### Regulatory Drivers

The EU Battery Regulation (2023/1542) is the most consequential policy intervention, mandating material recovery targets of 90% for cobalt, copper, lead, and nickel and 50% for lithium by 31 December 2027, increasing to 95% and 80% respectively by 31 December 2031 [01-356]. These targets create regulatory certainty for recycling investment and effectively mandate the development of advanced hydrometallurgical processing capacity across Europe.

### Capacity and Feedstock Dynamics

The IEA projects that by 2030, global battery recycling capacity could be seven times larger than available feedstock, as investment has outpaced the arrival of end-of-life batteries [01-359]. However, this dynamic reverses sharply: by 2040, announced capacity covers only 30% of feedstock in Europe and the US and 10% in India, as end-of-life EV batteries grow to represent over 90% of feedstock by 2050 [01-359]. Manufacturing scrap currently dominates recycling feedstock (approximately two-thirds in 2030), but end-of-life batteries will become the primary source as the EV fleet ages.

### China's Dominance

China is the global leader for battery pretreatment and material recovery and is expected to maintain more than 70% market share in both areas toward 2030 [01-354]. This concentration mirrors China's dominance in upstream mineral processing and presents a strategic vulnerability for Western nations seeking supply chain independence.

### Rare Earth Magnet Recycling

Global demand for magnetic rare earth elements is projected to triple from 59,000 metric tons in 2022 to 176,000 metric tons by 2035 [01-357]. The rare earth value chain could generate approximately 40,000 metric tons of pre-consumer scrap and 41,000 metric tons of post-consumer scrap annually, but the West faces a potential 30% supply shortfall unless these scrap flows are tapped at scale [01-358]. Recycling technologies including hydrogen decrepitation, robotic disassembly, and advanced hydrometallurgy are under development but remain largely at pilot scale.

### Australian Battery Recycling

In Australia, lithium-ion battery waste is growing by 20% per year and could exceed 136,000 tonnes by 2036, yet only 10% of Australia's lithium-ion battery waste was recycled in 2021 compared with 99% of lead-acid battery waste [01-360]. CSIRO estimates a domestic lithium battery recycling industry could be worth $603 million to $3.1 billion within a decade, with potentially 95% of battery components recoverable for reuse or new battery manufacture [01-361]. Envirostream, based in Victoria, is Australia's leading lithium-ion battery recycler with a processing capacity of 3,000 tonnes per annum, recovering around 95% of battery materials using mechanical and hydraulic separation techniques [01-362].

## 4. Tailings Reprocessing: From Waste to Resource

Legacy mine waste — particularly tailings — represents a large and growing opportunity for secondary mineral recovery, with particular relevance to the NWMP.

### Global Context

Global tailings inventories are estimated at approximately 282 billion tonnes, with around 16 billion tonnes produced annually [01-364]. Tailings are the second largest potential source of rare earth elements globally, and reprocessing is becoming increasingly attractive due to advances in extraction technologies and the declining grade of primary ores [01-365]. Technologies for tailing reprocessing include flotation, advanced gravity separation, high-intensity magnetic separation, leaching, roasting, bioleaching, and sensor-based sorting.

### NWMP Tailings Opportunities

The North West Minerals Province contains significant tailings resources with economic potential, including copper, lead, zinc, cobalt, and gold, with cobalt concentrations in tailings from major copper mines of particular interest [01-366]. A dedicated tailings audit commissioned by Queensland State Development has found that the economics of tailings retreatment in the NWMP are best when existing plant and infrastructure can be used at the end of primary mining activities, or when tailings can be treated simultaneously with primary mine feed [01-367]. The impending closure of the Glencore Mount Isa copper operations makes timing particularly relevant: as primary mining winds down, the opportunity to repurpose existing infrastructure for tailings reprocessing grows.

In a concrete example of this opportunity, Cobalt Blue Holdings signed an MOU with Mount Isa City Council in December 2024 to explore reprocessing pyrite tailings using its ReMine+ technology to produce sulphuric acid, addressing the supply shortfall expected after the Glencore copper smelter closure by 2030 [01-368]. Cobalt Blue's technology has been demonstrated at its Broken Hill facility, producing approximately 10 tonnes of high-purity elemental sulphur from pyrite feedstocks, with the process also enabling recovery of other contained metals.

### Phytomining Research

The Queensland Government has invested $1 million in phytomining research at UQ's Sustainable Minerals Institute, studying native hyperaccumulator plants for rare earth extraction from tailings at Phosphate Hill mine and the closed Mary Kathleen mine near Cloncurry in the NWMP [01-369]. Research has found that hyperaccumulator plants planted across a nickel-rich site such as a mine tailings dam could yield 300kg of nickel per hectare per year [01-370]. While phytomining remains at research stage, it represents an innovative low-energy approach to recovering critical minerals from legacy waste that is being actively investigated in the NWMP region.

## 5. Queensland Circular Economy Policy and Research

Queensland has positioned itself at the forefront of circular economy approaches to critical minerals through targeted policy and research investment.

### Critical Minerals and Circular Economy Research Alliance

The Critical Minerals and Circular Economy Research Alliance, a partnership between the Queensland Government and UQ's Sustainable Minerals Institute, oversees a research program (reported as $5.5 million by the department and $8 million by UQ, with the difference likely reflecting co-investment) over four years as a key action of the $315 million Queensland Critical Minerals Strategy [01-371]. The Alliance aims to address all stages of the critical minerals cycle — from discovery and mining through to processing, recycling, and manufacturing renewable technologies.

The Research Alliance has four active projects directly relevant to the NWMP circular economy: Social Considerations for Responsible Critical Minerals; Queensland's Secondary Prospectivity of Pyrite Resources; Optimizing Copper Production in Northwest Mineral Province; and the OreSand Knowledge Hub for zero waste mining [01-372]. These projects address the complete value chain from resource characterisation through social licence and operational optimisation.

UQ's Sustainable Minerals Institute has completed analysis of critical mineral recovery from mine waste at more than 35 sites across Queensland, building a comprehensive evidence base for secondary resource assessment [01-373]. The Geological Survey of Queensland has also re-analysed samples from mine tailings at established and historic mine sites throughout Queensland to identify critical mineral opportunities, examining the secondary prospectivity of mine tailings for cobalt and other critical metals [01-374].

### CSIRO Research

CSIRO research has identified the presence of rare earth elements in coal waste and mine tailings, investigating physical separation techniques including roasting, leaching, bioleaching and biosorption for REE extraction [01-375]. This research complements the Queensland Government's circular economy agenda and could eventually provide processing pathways applicable to NWMP waste streams.

## 6. Implications for the MICMRC

The recycling and circular economy landscape creates several direct implications for the proposed Mount Isa research centre.

**Testing Services for Recycling Flowsheets.** As the battery recycling, tailings reprocessing, and rare earth recycling industries scale up, they will require metallurgical testing to develop and optimise processing flowsheets. The MICMRC's bench-scale mineral processing laboratory, hydrometallurgy lab, and beneficiation testing capabilities are directly applicable to this work. The centre could serve as a regional testing hub for companies developing recycling processes for NWMP waste streams.

**Tailings Characterisation.** With the Geological Survey of Queensland actively assessing secondary prospectivity across the state [01-374], and UQ having already assessed 35+ mine waste sites [01-373], there is growing demand for detailed mineralogical characterisation and bench-scale recovery testing of tailings. The MICMRC's proximity to major NWMP tailings deposits — including those at Mount Isa, George Fisher, and Cannington — provides a logistical advantage for sample processing.

**Supporting the Glencore Transition.** The closure of Glencore's Mount Isa copper operations creates both a challenge and an opportunity. Tailings reprocessing, as demonstrated by the Cobalt Blue MOU [01-368], requires metallurgical testing to develop commercially viable flowsheets. The MICMRC could provide this capability locally, supporting the region's economic transition from primary mining to secondary resource recovery.

**Circular Economy R&D Alignment.** The MICMRC's mission aligns closely with the objectives of the Critical Minerals and Circular Economy Research Alliance [01-371], which explicitly targets innovation in mining, processing, and recycling. The centre could serve as an infrastructure partner for Alliance research projects, providing laboratory facilities for bench-scale testing of circular economy processes.

## Conclusion

Recycling and secondary supply are indispensable to the long-term sustainability and security of critical mineral supply chains, but they cannot replace primary mining — they complement it. The IEA's projections make clear that even with aggressive recycling scale-up, new mining investment through 2040 is required, with recycling reducing that figure by roughly one-third [01-350]. For NWMP commodities, the recycling picture is highly mineral-specific: lead recycling is mature and high-performing [01-343]; copper and zinc recycling is established but not keeping pace with demand growth [01-340] [01-345]; cobalt and nickel recycling from batteries is growing rapidly [01-348]; vanadium recycling from catalysts is well-established [01-347]; while rare earth and lithium recycling remain in early stages despite enormous projected demand [01-346].

For the MICMRC, the circular economy represents both a demand driver and a service opportunity. The centre's metallurgical testing capabilities are directly applicable to the growing need for recycling flowsheet development, tailings characterisation, and secondary recovery process optimisation. Queensland's investment in circular economy research — through the Research Alliance [01-371], phytomining studies [01-369], and GSQ tailings assessment [01-374] — creates a policy and funding environment that supports this work. The centre's location in the heart of the NWMP, surrounded by legacy tailings and transitioning mine operations, positions it uniquely to serve the emerging secondary supply sector.
