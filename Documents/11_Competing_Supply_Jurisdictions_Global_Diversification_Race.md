# Competing Supply Jurisdictions and the Global Diversification Race

## 1. Introduction: The Diversification Paradox

Despite unprecedented political attention and tens of billions of dollars in government commitments, the global critical minerals supply chain has become *more* concentrated since 2020, not less. The average market share of the top three refining nations for key energy minerals rose from around 82% in 2020 to 86% in 2024, with approximately 90% of supply growth coming from the single largest supplier alone — Indonesia for nickel and China for cobalt, graphite, and rare earths [01-378]. China remains the dominant refiner for 19 of the 20 energy-related strategic minerals analysed by the IEA, holding an average market share of around 70% [01-379].

This paradox — rising investment alongside rising concentration — defines the global diversification race. Capital costs for mining and refining projects in diversified regions outside the dominant producer are typically around 50% higher than for incumbents [01-380], and global critical minerals investment growth slowed to just 5% in 2024 (2% in real terms), down from 14% in 2023 [01-381]. Exploration activity has plateaued, and some 55% of critical minerals are now subject to some form of export control, with restrictions expanding beyond raw materials to processing technologies [01-384].

The IEA projects the average share of the top three refined mineral suppliers will decline only marginally to 82% by 2035 — effectively returning to 2020 concentration levels rather than reducing them [01-383]. Targeted policy incentives could reduce global market concentration by 7% by 2035, but this requires sustained, coordinated government action across multiple jurisdictions [01-382]. For Australia, and specifically for the MICMRC, this means the competitive landscape for processing and R&D capability is both crowded and time-sensitive.

## 2. Canada: The Integrated Strategy Leader

### Government Commitments

Canada has emerged as Australia's most direct competitor in the race to become a trusted allied critical minerals supplier. The Canadian government has committed over $4 billion through Budgets 2021-2024 for its Critical Minerals Strategy, including a $1.5 billion Critical Minerals Infrastructure Fund and a $192.1 million Research, Development and Demonstration program [01-385].

Budget 2025 substantially escalated this commitment with two new instruments: a $2 billion Critical Minerals Sovereign Fund (for equity investments, debt instruments, and offtake contracts) and a $1.5 billion First and Last Mile Fund (2026-2030) to support strategic mining and infrastructure projects [01-386]. Canada's total committed capital for critical minerals now exceeds any comparable Australian allocation on a per-capita or per-project basis.

### Project Pipeline

Canada's pipeline is among the deepest of any Western-aligned jurisdiction. Nearly 140 mining projects are planned or proposed from 2024 to 2034, representing a combined value of $117.1 billion, with approximately half processing or extracting critical minerals worth $72.4 billion [01-387]. As of mid-2024, Canada had 151 active advanced-stage critical minerals projects, 56 critical minerals mines, and an identified need for 19 new midstream processing facilities and 15 new mines to support four planned EV battery factories [01-388].

### Competitive Assessment

Canada's advantages over Australia include closer proximity to the US market, bilateral integration through USMCA trade structures, existing rare earth separation capacity at the Saskatchewan Research Council, and a more developed midstream processing pipeline. Canada's competitive weakness is its harsh climate, high labour costs in remote northern regions, and historically slow permitting — though recent reforms aim to compress federal impact assessments to two years. For the MICMRC, Canada demonstrates what is achievable when government strategy is backed by substantial, sustained capital allocation.

## 3. Indonesia: The Nickel and Cobalt Powerhouse

### The Export Ban Model

Indonesia has executed the most transformative resource nationalism strategy in the critical minerals sector. Its nickel ore export ban (implemented in 2020) turned the country from a low-value ore exporter into the world's dominant nickel processor. Indonesia now produces approximately 2.2 million tonnes of nickel annually — roughly half of global supply — and controls 61% of the global refined nickel market, up from just 2% a decade ago [01-389]. Processed nickel now exceeds coal as Indonesia's top export by value ($16.5 billion in the first half of 2025), while nickel ore valued at $60/tonne is converted to nickel matte worth $14,600/tonne [01-391].

### Processing Expansion

Indonesia's HPAL (high-pressure acid leaching) capacity for battery-grade nickel is forecast to nearly double to 862,000 tonnes per year in 2026, with multiple projects scheduled for commissioning including Vale's Pomalaa project (120 kt/year MHP, targeting mechanical completion around August 2026) [01-390]. CATL is investing $5.9 billion in a battery park in Karawang, West Java, with 15 GW initial capacity starting 2026 and expanding to 40 GW by 2028 [01-392].

### Strategic Implications

Indonesia's downstream expansion is overwhelmingly Chinese-funded, with dominant investors including Tsingshan, Huayou, GEM, and CNGR driving capacity across Sulawesi and Maluku industrial parks [01-393]. This creates a paradox for Western diversification: Indonesia diversifies supply away from Chinese geography but not from Chinese capital, technology, or operational control. For the MICMRC, Indonesia's success demonstrates the power of export bans to force downstream investment but also the risk that allied-nation supply chain objectives are not automatically served by geographic diversification alone.

## 4. Chile and Argentina: The Lithium Triangle

### Chile

Chile is projected to produce 282,000 tonnes of lithium carbonate equivalent (LCE) in 2025, with SQM accounting for approximately 65% and Albemarle 35% of production, operating primarily in the Salar de Atacama [01-394]. Chile's national lithium strategy transfers majority control of SQM's Atacama operations to state-owned Codelco through 2060, signalling a shift toward public-private partnerships and stronger state control over lithium resources [01-395]. Albemarle plans to expand its lithium mining operations using direct lithium extraction technology from 2028.

### Argentina

Argentina has adopted the opposite approach to Chile's state-led model, embracing a liberalised, investment-friendly regulatory environment under the Milei administration. Argentina aims to multiply its lithium production tenfold over 10 years, reaching 373,500 tonnes of LCE from a base of 37,500 tonnes, with potential investment across 19 mining projects totalling USD 6.47 billion [01-396]. Rio Tinto's $2.5 billion investment to expand its Rincon project to 60,000 tonnes per year exemplifies the scale of international commitment [01-397].

### Implications for Australia

The Lithium Triangle's expansion poses a direct competitive challenge to Australia's lithium sector, which has traditionally dominated hard-rock spodumene supply. South American brine operations have structurally lower operating costs than Australian hard-rock mines, and the expansion pipeline is now substantial. However, neither Chile nor Argentina has developed significant midstream processing — lithium hydroxide and carbonate refining still predominantly occurs in China. This processing gap is where Australian R&D capability, including facilities like the MICMRC, can add value.

## 5. Saudi Arabia: The Petrodollar Pivot

Saudi Arabia represents the most capital-rich new entrant in critical minerals. The kingdom announced it would mobilise SR375 billion ($100 billion) in new mining investments by 2035, with the mining sector's GDP contribution targeted to grow from $17 billion in 2024 to $75 billion by 2030 as part of Vision 2030 [01-398]. Saudi Arabia's mineral wealth is estimated at $2.5 trillion, and its state mining company Ma'aden will invest $110 billion over the next decade to triple its phosphate and gold business and double its aluminium business [01-399].

In rare earths, Ma'aden signed a binding term sheet with US-based MP Materials in November 2025 to build and operate a rare earth refining and separation facility in the kingdom, leveraging estimated 3.2 million tonnes of rare earth reserves [01-400]. The Public Investment Fund allocated an initial $15 billion for foreign mining acquisitions, later expanded to $25-30 billion over the next decade, including a $2.5 billion acquisition of nearly 10% of Vale's metals division through Manara Minerals [01-401].

Saudi Arabia's competitive advantages are its virtually unlimited capital, among the lowest energy costs globally, and direct access to underexplored mineral reserves. Its weaknesses are the absence of an established mining workforce, limited operational track record, and environmental sustainability concerns. For Australia, Saudi Arabia is less a direct competitor in mining R&D and more a potential partner or investor — but its ambition to become a top-seven global mineral processor by 2030 will draw capital and talent away from other jurisdictions.

## 6. Morocco: Phosphate Giant with Rare Earth Ambitions

Morocco holds the world's largest phosphate reserves at 50 billion metric tonnes, accounting for over 67% of global reserves, with state-owned OCP Group producing 30 million metric tonnes of phosphate in 2024 [01-402]. Morocco's interest for the diversification race lies in its rare earth potential: Rainbow Rare Earths, OCP, and UM6P are jointly developing a process to extract rare earths from phosphogypsum waste, with test results showing the feedstock contains all four magnet rare earths (NdPr at 14.3% of the basket) [01-403].

However, this project remains firmly in the testing phase with no commercial production timeline. Morocco's rare earth ambitions are a long-term proposition — significant for strategic planning but unlikely to affect supply chains within the next five to seven years.

## 7. DRC and Zambia: Africa's Copper-Cobalt Belt

### DRC: Resource Power Without Processing

The DRC produced 244 kilotonnes of cobalt in 2024 (a 38.9% increase year-on-year), accounting for approximately 78% of global cobalt production [01-404]. Yet the DRC has no active domestic cobalt refining capacity — its output is shipped to China for processing [01-404]. The government imposed a cobalt export ban in February 2025 (when prices hit a nine-year low), lifting it in October with strict quotas: 18,125 metric tonnes for the remainder of 2025 and 96,600 metric tonnes annually for 2026-2027, representing less than half the DRC's 2024 output [01-405].

This OPEC-style intervention signals the DRC's intent to leverage its resource dominance for better terms, but without domestic processing infrastructure, its ability to move up the value chain remains severely limited.

### Zambia: The Copper Expansion Play

Zambia targets tripling copper output to 3 million tonnes annually by 2031, up from 890,346 tonnes in 2025, with approximately $10 billion of private capital being invested in mine expansion including $1.25 billion by FQM for its Kansanshi S3 processing plant [01-406]. The Lobito Corridor — connecting DRC and Zambia's copper belt to Angola's Atlantic coast — is being developed with EUR 2 billion from the EU's Global Gateway Initiative and is central to Zambia's strategy to reposition from a landlocked to a "land-linked" nation [01-407].

### Implications

Africa's copper-cobalt belt will remain the world's dominant cobalt source and a growing copper producer, but value addition remains the key constraint. Neither the DRC nor Zambia has the R&D infrastructure or technical workforce to develop processing independently, creating opportunities for Australian expertise and MICMRC-type capabilities.

## 8. India: The Late but Determined Entrant

India launched its National Critical Mineral Mission in January 2025 with an allocation of INR 16,300 crore (approximately USD 1.9 billion), targeting global exploration and acquisition of 24 critical commodities by 2030-31 [01-408]. A broader sovereign fund of Rs 34,000 crore (approximately USD 4 billion) was allocated for the mission, including components for processing, overseas acquisition, and the Geological Survey of India's 1,200 targeted exploration projects [01-409].

India has removed customs duties on 12 critical minerals and on capital goods for critical mineral processing, signalling a strategic shift from raw extraction to high-purity processing [01-410]. India has significant base metal smelting expertise (copper, zinc) and proposes developing mineral processing parks modelled on Australia's METS sector.

India's competitive challenge is its late start: it has limited domestic deposits of most critical minerals, no established rare earth processing, and depends heavily on imports. However, its scale, growing manufacturing base, and bilateral partnerships (including with Australia) make it a significant emerging player over the medium term.

## 9. The EU and Other Emerging Suppliers

### European Union

The EU Critical Raw Materials Act sets 2030 targets for domestic extraction of 10%, processing of 40%, and recycling of 25% of strategic raw materials [01-411]. The Commission selected 60 strategic projects (47 within the EU, 13 outside) in the first call [01-411], and adopted the RESourceEU action plan in December 2025 mobilising up to EUR 3 billion by 2029 [01-412]. However, analysts note current financing volumes fall far short of what is required, and the EU remains almost entirely dependent on imports for primary critical mineral feedstock [01-412].

### Central Asia

Uzbekistan launched a US$2.6 billion initiative (2025-2028) targeting 76 projects across 28 critical minerals, with a dedicated state-owned company (TMK) tasked with developing over 100 investment projects across 25 strategic metals [01-413]. Kazakhstan is positioning itself as a rare earth supplier, with the Development Bank of Kazakhstan announcing multi-year billion-dollar financing for extraction and processing. However, many high-profile joint ventures in Central Asia are with Chinese state-owned firms, which brings capital and technology but reinforces rather than diversifies the China-centred supply chain.

## 10. Australia's Competitive Position

### Advantages

Australia enters the diversification race with world-class geological endowments, a globally recognised mining sector built over 60 years, political stability, rule of law, and Five Eyes/allied nation credentials that provide preferential access to US, EU, Japanese, and Korean markets. The US-Australia Framework Agreement and the $13 billion bilateral commitment provide a structural demand signal that few competitors can match.

Australia deployed $4 billion through the Critical Minerals Facility, $15 billion through the National Reconstruction Fund, and committed to establishing a $1.2 billion Critical Minerals Strategic Reserve [01-416]. Iluka Resources' AU$1.8 billion Eneabba rare earths refinery (targeting 2027 commissioning) and Arafura's AU$1.8 billion Nolans project represent the most advanced allied-nation rare earth processing projects outside China [01-418].

### Disadvantages

The window of opportunity is narrowing. Australian critical minerals market capitalisation declined 20% to $37.1 billion in FY25, with operating cash flows falling 71% to $0.9 billion, even as gold deals surged 216% [01-415]. Only 7 new investable critical minerals projects were added in the past year, out of 124 that had defined resources but no Final Investment Decision [01-414].

Processing and refining remain the critical bottleneck [01-417]. Rare earths require highly complex, capital-intensive separation processes, and Australia's processing pipeline — while more advanced than most competitors — remains thin relative to the scale of the opportunity. High construction costs, energy costs, skills shortages, regulatory uncertainty, and long approval timelines continue to blunt Australia's competitive edge.

### The Window of Opportunity

As PwC's 2025 Aussie Mine report observed: "the window to convert Australia's resource advantage into production leadership is open, but it won't stay open indefinitely" [01-419]. The IEA projects a 30% copper supply shortfall by 2035 [01-420] and warns that excluding the largest supplier, remaining global production meets only half of projected demand for battery metals and rare earths [01-422]. A sustained supply shock could increase global battery pack prices by 40-50% [01-421].

This supply vulnerability is the strategic foundation for facilities like the MICMRC. While competing jurisdictions are investing heavily in extraction and primary processing, few are investing in the R&D and testing infrastructure needed to develop new processing routes, optimise recovery from complex ores, and qualify new supply sources. Australia's advantage is not merely that it has minerals — many countries do — but that it can combine geological endowment with the technical, institutional, and regulatory foundations to develop processing capability that allied nations can trust and rely upon.

## 11. Conclusion: Implications for the MICMRC

The global diversification race is real, well-funded, and accelerating. Canada's $7.5 billion+ in direct government funding [01-385][01-386], Indonesia's transformative export ban strategy [01-389][01-391], Saudi Arabia's $100 billion ambition [01-398], and India's National Critical Mineral Mission [01-408] all represent serious competitive challenges. Yet the IEA data shows that despite this activity, meaningful diversification of processing remains years away — the world is producing more raw material in more places, but refining it in fewer [01-378][01-383].

This is precisely the gap that the MICMRC is positioned to address. The centre's focus on bench-scale processing, metallurgical testing, and beneficiation R&D targets the bottleneck that every competing jurisdiction faces: converting geological resources into processed, qualified materials that battery manufacturers and industrial users can incorporate into their supply chains. The NWMP's polymetallic deposits — copper, cobalt, vanadium, rare earths, zinc, lead — require the kind of complex metallurgical work that cannot be done in generic commercial labs.

Two-thirds of global battery recycling capacity growth since 2020 has occurred in China [01-423]. China produces 75% of purified phosphoric acid for LFP batteries and 95% of high-purity manganese sulphate, with announced projects meeting only 55% of expected 2035 demand [01-424]. These gaps represent structural demand for exactly the kind of processing R&D that the MICMRC would provide. The diversification race is not just about who has the minerals — it is about who can process them. That is where Australia, and the MICMRC, can compete.
