# Critical Minerals Investment Landscape & Capital Flows

## 1. Introduction

The global critical minerals sector is experiencing an unprecedented wave of public and private capital mobilisation, driven by intersecting imperatives: energy transition demand, geopolitical supply chain diversification, and national security concerns. Yet despite headline-grabbing investment announcements, the actual pace of capital deployment has slowed relative to the scale of the challenge. Global investment in critical mineral development grew by just 5% in 2024, down from 14% in 2023, with real growth of just 2% after adjusting for cost inflation [01-425]. Exploration activity plateaued in the same year, marking a pause in the upward trend seen since 2020, with spending continuing to rise for lithium, uranium and copper but declining notably for nickel, cobalt and zinc [01-427].

The International Energy Agency estimates that between USD 500 billion and USD 600 billion in new mining capital investment is required between 2024 and 2040 to meet energy transition goals — a figure that rises further when midstream processing and refining are included [01-426]. Capital costs for projects in diversified (non-incumbent) regions are typically around 50% higher than for incumbent producers, creating a structural barrier to the supply chain diversification that allied nations are seeking [01-428].

This report examines how capital is flowing into critical minerals development globally and into Australia specifically, assessing the adequacy of current investment trends against the scale of the supply challenge.

## 2. Development Finance Institution (DFI) Commitments

### 2.1 United States: DFC and Federal Agencies

The United States has dramatically expanded its critical minerals financing toolkit. The DFC reauthorisation expanded its contingent liability cap from approximately USD 60 billion to USD 205 billion, while the Office of Strategic Capital mandate grew from approximately USD 1 billion to USD 200 billion [01-432]. This expanded capacity is being deployed across several landmark transactions.

The DFC committed USD 600 million to the Orion Critical Mineral Consortium, a USD 1.8 billion public-private partnership with Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund ADQ and Orion Resource Partners, with a target fund size of USD 5 billion [01-429]. The consortium will prioritise investments in existing or near-term producing assets rather than frontier exploration, aiming to accelerate supply to the US and allied markets [01-431].

Beyond the consortium, the DFC approved a USD 565 million loan for the Pela Ema rare earths mine in Brazil and issued letters of interest for up to USD 700 million for the Severniy Katpar tungsten mine in Kazakhstan [01-430]. The Department of Energy provided a USD 2.3 billion loan for Lithium Americas' Thacker Pass processing facilities in Nevada, while the Office of Strategic Capital provided a USD 150 million loan to MP Materials with a 15% government equity stake and 10-year price floor and offtake commitments [01-433].

A USA Rare Earth package combined USD 1.6 billion in debt and equity investment, and a Korea Zinc/DOW partnership announced a USD 7.4 billion polymetallic smelter [01-463]. The US also proposed a USD 2.5 billion Strategic Resilience Reserve to buy and store key metals used in clean energy, defence systems and advanced electronics [01-464].

The US government has effectively shifted from traditional project lender to market maker for critical minerals, combining long-tenor debt, direct equity stakes, strategic stockpiles, long-term offtake contracts, and administered price floors to shape market conditions [01-453].

### 2.2 European Investment Bank (EIB)

The EIB adopted a critical raw materials strategic initiative in 2025 targeting EUR 2 billion in CRM financing, supported by a new CRM Task Force and a dedicated one-stop shop for pipeline management [01-434]. Specific project commitments include EUR 250 million for Vulcan Energy's Lionheart lithium project in Germany's Upper Rhine Valley and EUR 150 million for Keliber, a Finnish lithium mining and battery-material processing company [01-435]. The EIB signed a cooperation agreement with Australia in November 2025 on critical raw materials, extending its financing activities to resource-rich allied nations [01-474].

### 2.3 Germany, France and Italy

Germany's KfW established a EUR 1 billion Raw Materials Fund in 2024, offering equity investments of EUR 50-150 million per project in mining, processing, or recycling of EU-designated strategic raw materials [01-436]. Collectively, Germany, France, and Italy outlined EUR 2.5 billion in critical minerals funds, demonstrating a coordinated European approach to raw materials security [01-467].

### 2.4 Japan Bank for International Cooperation (JBIC)

JBIC signed an MOU with the Queensland Government on 25 November 2025 to strengthen partnership and encourage Japanese companies' investments in critical minerals, alumina, renewable energy, and other sectors [01-437]. This agreement positions Queensland — and by extension the North West Minerals Province — as a priority destination for Japanese critical minerals investment.

### 2.5 International Finance Corporation (IFC)

The IFC partnered with Appian Capital Advisory to launch a USD 1 billion critical minerals and metals fund for emerging markets, anchored by an initial IFC contribution of USD 100 million [01-438]. This is the first dedicated mining fund focused exclusively on emerging markets and will deploy capital through equity, credit and royalty investments.

### 2.6 Export Finance Australia and the Critical Minerals Facility

The Australian Government committed AUD 1.2 billion to establish a Critical Minerals Strategic Reserve, including AUD 1 billion in transactions from the expanded AUD 5 billion Critical Minerals Facility and AUD 185 million for selective stockpiling [01-439]. The initial focus minerals are antimony, gallium and rare earth elements. Australia's total critical minerals incentives are valued at AUD 7 billion over the decade, including a Critical Minerals Production Tax Incentive that halves the effective tax rate from 30% to 15% for qualifying processing operations [01-440].

Export Finance Australia has provided AUD 400 million in loans for Alpha HPA's high-purity alumina processing facility in Gladstone, Queensland, and AUD 185 million in conditional approval for Renascor's Siviour graphite project [01-442]. The landmark AUD 1.25 billion non-recourse loan for Iluka Resources' Eneabba rare earth refinery (total capex AUD 1.7-1.8 billion) demonstrates the role of concessional finance in de-risking midstream processing [01-458].

The US-Australia critical minerals supply framework will support a pipeline of projects valued at around USD 13 billion (approximately AUD 20 billion) [01-441].

### 2.7 Northern Australia Infrastructure Facility (NAIF)

NAIF earmarked AUD 500 million specifically for critical minerals projects in northern Australia [01-443]. Major commitments include up to AUD 200 million for Arafura Rare Earths' Nolans project in the Northern Territory (a AUD 2.6 billion project targeting 4,440 tonnes per annum of NdPr oxide) and up to AUD 50 million for Element 25's Butcherbird manganese project in Western Australia's Pilbara region [01-443] [01-444].

## 3. Sovereign Wealth Fund Activity

Sovereign wealth funds and state-backed capital have emerged as decisive forces in critical minerals finance, fundamentally reshaping ownership, governance, and risk distribution across mining and processing assets [01-447]. Abu Dhabi's ADQ committed USD 600 million to the Orion Critical Mineral Consortium alongside the US DFC [01-429], with Middle Eastern SWFs broadly using critical minerals to diversify beyond hydrocarbons and secure long-term exposure to battery materials.

Canada launched a CAD 2 billion Critical Minerals Sovereign Fund in 2025 to provide equity investments, loan guarantees, and offtake agreements for strategic mining projects [01-445]. The United States has quietly built the early architecture of a minerals sovereign wealth fund, holding equity positions in several strategic critical minerals companies across multiple agency balance sheets — most notably a 15% stake in MP Materials [01-446].

These developments represent a structural shift: governments are no longer content to regulate and incentivise private investment but are taking direct equity positions and creating state-backed investment vehicles specifically for critical minerals.

## 4. Private Equity and Venture Capital Trends

Private equity and venture capital transactions in global metals and mining fell to USD 4.76 billion through September 2024, down more than 50% from the USD 10.52 billion recorded in full year 2023, with only 59 deals announced in the first three quarters [01-448]. Higher acquisition debt financing rates and reduced venture capital deployments contributed to lower totals. In Q3 2023, the mining sector had seen an unprecedented surge in PE deals totalling approximately USD 5.2 billion — a 1,377% increase compared with the previous quarter — driven by EV and battery metal enthusiasm that subsequently cooled [01-449].

The EU bucked the global trend: PE deal value in EU metals and mining reached USD 4.19 billion in the first ten months of 2024, nearly double the USD 2.23 billion for full year 2023 [01-450]. This reflects policy-driven momentum from the EU Critical Raw Materials Act, which identifies 51 materials as strategic or critical and provides incentives to reduce dependence on Chinese imports.

Start-up funding for critical minerals ventures is also showing signs of a slowdown, though the sector is becoming a genuine venture capital market for the first time as new business models and non-dilutive capital sources emerge [01-470].

Mining projects typically require more than USD 1 billion to construct a greenfield mine, with processing similarly capital-intensive [01-451]. Aspiring producers face difficulty accessing debt at competitive interest rates due to limited initial cash flow and uncertain profitability. Two principal challenges constrain PE deployment: the large cheque sizes required to advance projects to production, and the mismatch between PE fund lifespans and long mining development cycles.

## 5. Green Bonds and Sustainable Finance

Less than USD 15 billion in thematic bonds has been issued by mining companies focused on critical minerals, representing only around 0.3% of the total thematic bond market — indicating enormous room for growth [01-452]. European banks pour nearly EUR 8 billion per year into critical minerals mining through conventional lending, but dedicated green and sustainability-linked instruments remain nascent [01-468].

The ICMA Green Bond Principles now include guidance on Green Enabling Projects, allowing capital to flow to activities such as rare-earth mining, refining, and magnet manufacturing through green bond instruments [01-471]. However, mining companies issuing green bonds or other thematic bonds face high compliance costs while typically realising no cost savings from issuance, as there is a lack of a pricing premium (greenium) relative to conventional bonds [01-472]. The absence of clear definitions and harmonised standards for responsible and sustainable mining across ESG criteria, decarbonisation pathways, and timelines further constrains issuance [01-473].

## 6. Project Finance Conditions and Bankability

Achieving bankability in the critical minerals space has been a key obstacle to project development [01-454]. Many critical minerals — rare earths in particular — lack established markets, experience high price volatility, and require high initial capital investment, undermining financiers' confidence in debt repayment.

Long-term offtake agreements with financially reputable counterparties and clear take-or-pay obligations have become a core bankability requirement [01-455]. A surge of 10-year-plus offtake contracts between 2024 and 2026 has begun to reshape international supply chains for lithium, graphite, rare earth elements, and battery-grade chemicals.

Companies often face a CAD 20-30 million funding gap during feasibility, permitting and construction phases — too large for high-net-worth investors and too risky for institutional investors — which encourages asset sales rather than mine development [01-462]. Canada has channelled just 11% of its mining capital into critical minerals over the past 25 years, supplying about 2% of global output, though this could rise to 14% by 2040 if all identified projects proceed [01-460].

## 7. Announced vs. Deployed Capital

Despite tens of billions in headline commitments, the gap between announced investment and actual capital deployment remains wide. The current mine project pipeline points to a potential 30% copper supply shortfall by 2035 due to declining ore grades, rising capital costs, limited resource discoveries and long lead times [01-465]. Investment momentum weakened in 2024, and exploration spending plateaued [01-425] [01-427] — suggesting that the urgent rhetoric of government announcements has not yet translated into proportionate capital at the project level.

China controls about 70% of global refining capacity for 19 of the 20 most critical minerals, supported by state-backed capital, lower costs and deliberate overcapacity that pressures competitors [01-461]. China's advantage is not simply dominance but integration — processing capacity linked to manufacturing demand and supported by patient capital operating at scale — which Western nations cannot replicate through piecemeal project-by-project financing [01-459].

## 8. Capital as a Binding Constraint

The binding constraint in critical minerals supply chains sits in the midstream — chemical separation, refining and precursor capability — where large, lumpy investments are exposed to price cycles, construction risk and policy inconsistency [01-456]. A circular dependency compounds the problem: magnet manufacturers need assured feedstock at predictable pricing, processors need binding offtake to unlock finance, and each node waits for the others, deferring investment decisions across the value chain [01-457].

The gap in critical minerals financing is not a lack of instruments but the lack of a coordinated template that aligns risk-sharing, offtake underwriting and export credit as a standing playbook rather than one-off bargains [01-469]. RBC proposes developing mineral corridors that cluster mining and shared processing facilities, supported by government loan guarantees and anchor offtake agreements, to improve project economics [01-466].

## 9. Implications for MICMRC

The investment landscape described in this report creates strong structural tailwinds for the Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre:

**Government financing is flowing to critical minerals processing and R&D.** Export Finance Australia, NAIF, and the expanded Critical Minerals Facility are actively seeking bankable projects in northern Australia [01-439] [01-443]. JBIC has specifically targeted Queensland through its November 2025 MOU [01-437]. The EIB has signed a cooperation agreement with Australia on critical raw materials [01-474].

**The midstream gap creates demand for pre-commercial R&D.** As ASPI Strategist notes, the binding constraint is in the midstream — the chemical separation, refining and precursor capability that converts ore into usable industrial inputs [01-456]. MICMRC's bench-scale processing, metallurgical testing, and flowsheet development capabilities directly address this bottleneck by de-risking processing technology before full-scale investment.

**Bankability requirements favour proven processing routes.** Projects cannot access project finance without demonstrated metallurgical flowsheets and offtake agreements [01-454] [01-455]. MICMRC provides the testing infrastructure needed to develop bankable processing parameters — a prerequisite for the project finance that DFIs and private capital providers require.

**Shared infrastructure models are gaining policy support.** RBC's recommendation for mineral corridors with shared processing facilities aligns directly with MICMRC's concept of a shared-use research and testing facility serving multiple explorers and miners across the NWMP [01-466].

**AUD 7 billion in incentives creates a funding runway.** The combination of the Critical Minerals Production Tax Incentive, the expanded Critical Minerals Facility, and the Strategic Reserve creates a multi-year funding environment that supports both MICMRC's establishment and the pipeline of critical minerals projects it would serve [01-440].
