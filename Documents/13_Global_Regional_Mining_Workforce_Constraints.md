# Global & Regional Mining Workforce Constraints

## 1. Introduction

The global mining industry faces a workforce crisis that threatens to constrain the very mineral supply expansion the energy transition demands. As nations accelerate investment in critical minerals projects to diversify supply chains away from China, they are discovering that capital and geological endowment alone are insufficient — without a skilled workforce of geoscientists, metallurgists, mineral processing engineers, and laboratory scientists, projects cannot be developed. This document examines the scale and nature of workforce constraints across major mining jurisdictions and within the North West Minerals Province (NWMP), assessing how these gaps shape both the risk and the opportunity for the proposed Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre (MICMRC).

---

## 2. Global Mining Workforce Shortage Scale

### 2.1 The Energy Sector Labour Crisis

The International Energy Agency's *World Energy Employment 2025* report documents a systemic labour crisis across the global energy and minerals sector. Global energy sector employment reached 76 million people in 2024, up more than 5 million from 2019, with energy job growth of 2.2% nearly double the wider economy growth rate [01-475]. Yet this expansion has outpaced the workforce pipeline: over 60% of energy companies report labour shortages putting timelines, system reliability, and cost control at risk, with more than half of 700 surveyed companies, unions, and training institutions reporting critical hiring bottlenecks [01-476].

The crisis is most acute in applied technical roles — electricians, pipefitters, plant operators, and engineers — which account for over half the global energy workforce. These roles added 2.5 million positions since 2019, but the training pipeline has not kept pace: economy-wide demand for applied technical workers grew 16% between 2015 and 2022, yet vocational programme graduations increased by only 9% [01-508].

Over 180,000 jobs have been added in critical mineral mining in the last three years alone, reflecting the accelerating demand for energy transition minerals [01-479]. Yet the IEA warns that to prevent the skills gap from widening further by 2030, the number of new qualified entrants globally would need to rise by 40%. The investment required — approximately USD 2.6 billion annually in expanded training capacity — represents less than 0.1% of global public education spending [01-478].

### 2.2 Industry Executive Confidence

The scale of the problem is reflected in executive surveys. EY's *Risks and Opportunities for Mining and Metals in 2026* — based on a survey of 500 senior mining leaders from organisations with at least US$1 billion in revenue — found that 75% of mining executives are not confident in their ability to resolve labour shortages for onsite operations [01-480]. Workforce challenges have risen to the 6th-ranked business risk for the sector, up sharply from 13th the previous year, with the survey noting that the sector's struggle to fill key roles in mine planning, process engineering, sustainability, closure, and regulatory compliance "undermines productivity and safety and threatens future supply" [01-481].

Separately, 71% of mining leaders believe the talent shortage hampers achievement of production targets and strategic goals, while 86% of executives report greater difficulty recruiting and retaining talent compared to two years prior [01-482].

### 2.3 Jurisdiction-Specific Workforce Gaps

Workforce shortages are a global constraint across all major mining jurisdictions:

- **Australia** needs an estimated 24,000 new mining workers by 2026, but only approximately 16,000 are expected to be available — an 8,000-worker shortfall. The mining vacancy rate reached 5.2% in late 2023, up from 3.3% three years prior [01-483].
- **United States** is projected to lose 221,000 workers to retirement by 2029 — more than half the total mining workforce — while mining employment has declined 20.4% over the past decade. US mining programs have fallen from 25 in 1982 to just 15 in 2023 [01-485].
- **Canada** needs 80,000 to 120,000 new mining workers by 2030 [01-486].
- **Chile** requires more than 34,000 new workers by 2032 [01-486].
- **Western Australia** alone requires more than 11,000 new workers by around 2029 to support proposed resource projects [01-504].

The OECD's 2025 report *Mining for Talent* examined these workforce challenges systematically, finding that rapid technological change, soaring global demand for critical minerals, and the green transition are transforming job requirements, while significant gaps persist in both worker availability and the attractiveness of the sector and the regions where mining occurs [01-484].

## 3. Workforce Demographics & Pipeline

### 3.1 Aging Workforce

The mining industry's demographic profile creates an urgent succession challenge. According to the OECD, 72% of miners across the world are over 35 years old [01-488]. In advanced economies, the IEA reports 2.4 energy workers nearing retirement for every new entrant under 25 — a ratio that deepens to 1.7:1 in nuclear professions and 1.4:1 in grid-related roles [01-477]. Between today and 2035, two of every three new hires will be needed just to replace retiring workers [01-524].

The age profile varies by jurisdiction but the trend is consistent:

- **Globally**, 50% of mining engineers will reach retirement age within the next decade (Deloitte), with Baby Boomers comprising approximately 22% of the mining workforce and Generation X about 35%, occupying experienced management and technical roles [01-487] [01-488].
- **United States**: The average age of a mine worker is 46 years old, with 221,000 workers projected to retire by 2029 [01-487].
- **United Kingdom**: 80% of mining engineers are over 50, with 40% at least 60 years old [01-487].
- **Australia**: 25% of metal ore mining workers are 50 years of age or older, in an industry that contributed more than $400 billion to the economy in 2024 and employs more than 280,000 people [01-489].

### 3.2 The Education Pipeline Collapse

The most alarming dimension of the workforce crisis is the collapse of the education pipeline. The data across every major mining nation tells a consistent story of precipitous decline:

**Australia:**
- Mining engineering bachelor-level completions declined 98% between 2014 and 2023, with postgraduate completions falling 76% [01-491].
- Male student numbers dropped 83% (from 466 to 80) and female completions fell to zero by 2023 (from 53 in 2014) [01-491].
- First-year mining engineering enrolments dropped from 267 in 2012-2013 to just 34 in 2018. At UNSW, enrolments fell from 120 in 2014 to just 6 in 2018 [01-492].
- Metallurgy first-year enrolments reached just 24 in 2018, the lowest since 2000 [01-492].
- The Australian Geoscience Council reported a 40% decline in geoscience degree completions over eight years, with enrolments dropping 43% between 2013 and 2020 [01-493].
- Seven of 21 Australian geoscience departments have been hit with substantive reductions in staffing and curriculum offerings [01-493].
- Enrolments in RII mining qualifications decreased from 51,319 in 2015 to 31,493 in 2020 (a 39% drop), before partially recovering to 35,087 in 2023 [01-498].

**United States:**
- Mining and mineral engineering degrees awarded fell to 314 in 2021, a 39% decline since 2016 [01-495].
- Mining programs declined from 25 in 1982 to 15 in 2023, with faculty numbers falling from approximately 120 to 70 over the same period [01-485].

**United Kingdom:**
- A-level Geology enrolments fell from 4,000 students in the early 1980s to 1,100 in 2024, with only approximately 90 schools currently offering Geology [01-499].
- The Camborne School of Mines halted undergraduate mining engineering intake from 2020 to 2025, producing zero new cohorts for five years [01-499].

**Contrast with China:**
- China has 38+ mineral processing schools and 44+ mining engineering programs. Central South University alone produces 1,000 undergraduates and 500 graduate students — more than the entire combined output of the US, Australia, and the UK [01-495].

### 3.3 University Program Closures

The enrolment decline has triggered a wave of institutional restructuring [01-494]:

- **Macquarie University** closed its School of Earth and Planetary Sciences in July 2021, letting go 18 of 21 academics.
- **Australian National University** laid off 20 permanent staff from its Research School of Earth Sciences in December 2020, closing its mechanical workshop and significantly reducing its SHRIMP mass spectrometer laboratory capability.
- **University of Newcastle** dropped its geology major entirely.
- The Federal Government cut funding for Earth and environmental science courses by 29%, while Australian universities were forecast to lose AU$19 billion between 2020 and 2023.

### 3.4 The Perception Problem

The pipeline crisis is compounded by a severe perception problem. Among young Australians, 42% are unaware of earth sciences as an area of study, 55% are unaware of geoscience, and 67% are unaware that mineral processing is a career path [01-497]. Over 65% of Generation Z view the mining sector negatively, and only 7% aspire to join the industry, with 56% associating mining with environmental damage [01-510].

In Canada, the problem is equally acute: 70% of people aged 15-30 said they would "definitely" or "probably" not work in mining, with only 4% saying they "definitely would." Young Canadians described the industry with words like "hard," "dangerous," "dirty," and "pollution" [01-496].

## 4. Workforce Constraints on Project Development

### 4.1 The Gap Between Investment and Execution

The disconnect between the scale of planned mineral investment and available workforce capacity threatens to become the binding constraint on new supply. The Australian Resources and Energy Employer Association forecasts 96 mining and energy projects likely to proceed by 2030, creating 22,279 jobs and requiring AU$129.5 billion in investment [01-500]. Queensland alone needs 26,810 additional employees by 2029 — a 9.4% workforce increase — with 107 major projects valued at $130.9 billion expected to proceed [01-501].

Yet the training pipeline cannot deliver at this scale. Mining skills shortage across Australian occupations increased from 34% in 2021 to 63% in 2022, making mining the second-most acute skills shortage of all industries from 2022 to 2024, behind only construction [01-502]. The Mining Engineers fill rate was just 42% in 2021 — meaning for every two vacancies advertised, fewer than one was filled [01-503].

### 4.2 Competition Between Jurisdictions

As every major mining nation simultaneously pursues critical minerals development, competition for the same limited pool of skilled workers has intensified. Mining and METS sectors contribute an estimated 15% of Australia's GDP and create more than 1.1 million jobs, with approximately 30% more mining vacancies in May 2024 versus pandemic levels [01-509]. Western Australia alone requires 11,000 new workers by 2029 [01-504], while McKinsey estimates that converting spodumene to lithium hydroxide alone requires nearly 4,000 additional workers by 2030 in Western Australia [01-505].

At the organisational level, 10% of Australian mining companies report experiencing an extreme skills gap, 35% a moderate gap, and 39% a minor gap. 52% face scarcity of necessary skills within their teams, with 43% reporting difficulty sourcing talent [01-507].

### 4.3 Impact on Project Timelines

Skill shortages in critical areas of engineering, drilling, and heavy machinery slowed project ramp-ups across Australia in 2025, with these constraints expected to continue into 2026 [01-506].

The IEA notes that nearly 50% of companies now recruit from neighbouring industries or increase in-house training to fill gaps — an approach that addresses immediate needs but risks diluting technical competence and safety standards in an industry where both are critical [01-524].

## 5. NWMP & Mount Isa Workforce Context

### 5.1 Workforce Transition

Mount Isa's workforce is undergoing a profound structural transition. Glencore's underground copper mining operations (Enterprise, X41, and Black Rock mines) and the copper concentrator are closing in H2 2025 after more than 60 years of continuous operation, resulting in approximately 500 redundancies after 144 employees were redeployed [01-511]. Townsville Enterprise estimates over 17,000 jobs across the region are connected to the copper industry supply chain, with the copper smelter — one of only two operating in Australia — agreed to remain open until 2030 but facing an uncertain future beyond that without government support [01-513]. The ASPI Strategist described the closure as "a strategic own goal for Australia," noting that up to 3,000 jobs across north-west Queensland are affected at a time when copper demand is expected to double over the next decade [01-515].

However, continuity remains: Glencore's George Fisher mine (zinc, lead, silver) is expected to operate until at least 2042, and the Black Star Open Cut mine pre-feasibility study could create 300-400 jobs starting around 2027 with a possible 20-year mine life [01-512]. Glencore has invested $1.8 billion in its Queensland Metals operations over the past five years, including $653 million in zinc mining and processing assets and $87 million in regional exploration [01-522].

### 5.2 Population and Liveability

Mount Isa LGA had a population of 18,727 at the 2021 Census, with labour force participation at 68.3% of those aged 15+. Mining dominates local employment, with copper ore mining and silver-lead-zinc ore mining representing 16.9% and 9.0% of employment respectively — substantially higher than state and national averages, reflecting a community whose economic identity is overwhelmingly defined by the resources sector [01-514].

The NWMP spans more than 31,000 residents across 10 local government areas [01-518], with the region containing approximately $500-650 billion in estimated critical and rare-earth mineral resources, contributing upwards of $2 billion annually to Australia's economy [01-519]. Yet the region faces the workforce attraction, retention, and recruitment challenges common to remote mining communities — limited social amenities, housing constraints, energy pricing, and constrained access to healthcare, childcare, and transport.

### 5.3 FIFO Dynamics

The FIFO (fly-in, fly-out) model, while essential for staffing remote operations, carries significant human costs that affect retention and productivity. Over a third of FIFO workers experience anxiety, depression, or stress above the clinical cutoff, with 28% reporting high or very high psychological distress — nearly three times the 10.8% rate for the general population [01-520]. The ASPI Strategist identified "policy paralysis and jurisdictional fragmentation" as barriers to sustaining the region's processing infrastructure, noting that without local smelting capability, transport and processing costs would rise and threaten the viability of regional mines [01-523].

### 5.4 Training Infrastructure and Government Response

The Queensland Government has responded with targeted investments. A $50 million comprehensive support package for Mount Isa comprises a $30 million Mining Acceleration Program (funding Eva Copper at $20.7 million, Paradise South Phosphate at $5.77 million, and Vecco Critical Minerals at $2.33 million) and a $20 million Transition Fund for economic adjustment [01-516] [01-517].

On skills development, $7.4 million from the $200 million Regional Economic Futures Fund was allocated for TAFE to deliver renewable energy and critical minerals training, including $3.2 million for a Critical Minerals Training Hub at the Mount Isa TAFE campus [01-518]. TAFE Queensland's Mount Isa campus already features purpose-built facilities including construction workshops, metal fabrication facilities, and specialised automotive, diesel fitting, and machining workshops, with Glencore donating operational mining equipment for student training [01-521].

James Cook University maintains a presence through its Mount Isa Centre for Rural and Remote Health, though this is focused on health sciences rather than mining or minerals disciplines. The absence of tertiary mining engineering, metallurgy, or geoscience teaching capacity in the NWMP represents a structural gap that the MICMRC's Critical Minerals Learning Centre could help address.

## 6. Implications for the MICMRC

The convergence of global workforce constraints and the NWMP's local transition creates a compelling case for the MICMRC's Critical Minerals Learning Centre and training capabilities:

1. **Training demand is structural, not cyclical.** The 98% decline in Australian mining engineering completions [01-491] and the 40% decline in geoscience graduates [01-493] are long-term trends that will take a decade or more to reverse. The IEA estimates a 40% increase in qualified entrants is needed globally by 2030 [01-478] — creating sustained demand for training facilities.

2. **The NWMP needs a skills anchor institution.** With no tertiary mining, metallurgy, or geoscience teaching capacity in the region, the MICMRC's Learning Centre could serve as the NWMP's equivalent of the Kalgoorlie School of Mines — providing demonstration labs, professional development, and industry-standard training in a location where the minerals are.

3. **Workforce transition creates opportunity.** The approximately 500 workers being displaced from copper mining at Mount Isa [01-511] possess transferable metallurgical and processing skills. A research centre with training capabilities could support their transition into critical minerals processing — a field where Australia needs precisely these skills.

4. **Regional training reduces FIFO dependency.** By building local training capacity, the MICMRC could support a shift toward residential workforce models, addressing the mental health and community sustainability challenges that FIFO dependence creates [01-520].

5. **The perception problem requires proximity.** With 67% of young people unaware that mineral processing is a career path [01-497], physical training infrastructure in a mining region — where students can see operational mines and processing facilities — provides a tangible pathway that no urban university campus can replicate.

---

## 7. Conclusion

The global mining workforce crisis is not a transient cyclical shortage but a structural transformation driven by decades of underinvestment in education, an aging workforce approaching mass retirement, and a perception problem that deters the next generation. The numbers are unambiguous: 98% fewer mining engineering graduates in Australia [01-491], 75% of executives unable to resolve labour shortages [01-480], and a 40% increase in qualified entrants needed globally by 2030 just to prevent the gap from widening [01-478].

For the NWMP, these global constraints intersect with a local transition as Mount Isa's copper operations wind down and the region seeks to reposition around its $500-650 billion critical minerals endowment [01-519]. The MICMRC's Critical Minerals Learning Centre is positioned to serve a dual function: as a training anchor institution for the NWMP and as a contributor to the national effort to rebuild Australia's depleted minerals workforce pipeline. In an industry where workforce availability has become the binding constraint on new supply, training infrastructure is not a secondary consideration — it is essential enabling infrastructure.
