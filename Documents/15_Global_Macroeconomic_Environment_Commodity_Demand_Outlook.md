# Global Macroeconomic Environment & Commodity Demand Outlook

## Executive Summary

The global macroeconomic environment in 2025-2026 presents a complex but ultimately supportive backdrop for new mineral supply investment. While headline GDP growth is subdued — the World Bank describes the 2020s as potentially the weakest decade for global growth since the 1960s [01-578] — the underlying drivers of mineral demand are structural rather than cyclical. Energy transition investment, AI and data centre buildout, defence spending, and central bank reserve diversification are generating demand for metals that is largely decoupled from traditional GDP-commodity correlations [01-611]. Meanwhile, persistent cost inflation, declining ore grades, and constrained exploration budgets are limiting new supply, creating a tightening market for several NWMP-relevant commodities [01-596].

For the proposed Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre (MICMRC), the macroeconomic environment is net supportive. Monetary easing across most major economies is reducing the cost of capital for resource projects [01-580], precious metals are at record highs generating strong cash flows for miners [01-612], and structural supply deficits in copper, rare earths, and silver point to sustained demand for exploration and development services [01-613] [01-618] [01-624]. However, China's property-driven slowdown weighs on iron ore and base metals broadly [01-582], and margin pressure on lithium and nickel producers constrains near-term investment in those commodities [01-599].

## 1. Global GDP Growth Outlook

### 1.1 Consensus Forecasts

The three major international economic institutions — the IMF, World Bank, and OECD — paint a consistent picture of modest but positive global growth through 2027, with notable divergence across regions.

The IMF's October 2025 World Economic Outlook projects global GDP growth slowing from 3.3% in 2024 to 3.2% in 2025 and 3.1% in 2026, with advanced economies growing around 1.5% and emerging market and developing economies just above 4% [01-575]. The January 2026 update revised 2026 growth slightly upward to 3.3%, citing technology investment, fiscal and monetary support, and private sector adaptability offsetting trade policy shifts [01-576].

The World Bank is more conservative, projecting global growth at 2.6% in 2026 and 2.7% in 2027 [01-577]. The Bank notes that global growth weakened to 2.3% in 2025, more than a percentage point lower than the average of the 2010s, and that the 2020s are on track to be the weakest decade for global growth since the 1960s [01-578].

The OECD projects global GDP growth of approximately 3.2% for 2026, broadly aligning with the IMF's forecast, and expects G20 consumer price inflation to ease from 3.4% in 2025 to 2.8% in 2026 and 2.5% in 2027 [01-580].

### 1.2 Regional Growth Dynamics

The World Bank projects South Asia as the fastest-growing region at 6.2% in 2026 and 6.5% in 2027, followed by East Asia and Pacific at 4.4% and Sub-Saharan Africa at 4.3% [01-579]. India's real GDP is projected to grow by 7.4% in FY2025-26 according to MoSPI First Advance Estimates, up from 6.5% in FY2024-25, reinforcing its position among the fastest-growing major economies globally [01-583].

China's trajectory is the most consequential for mineral markets. While official GDP grew 5.0% in 2025, independent analysis from Rhodium Group estimates actual growth at 2.5-3.0%, noting that China has faced ten consecutive quarters of persistent deflation inconsistent with 5% real growth [01-581]. China set its lowest growth target in decades for 2026 at 4.5-5.0%, with Goldman Sachs forecasting 4.8% and UBS forecasting 4.5% [01-582]. The property sector has entered its fifth year of crisis, with new construction starts more than 75% below their 2021 peak [01-582]. China's retail sales growth collapsed from 6.4% year-on-year in May to just 1.3% in November 2025, while consumer prices have not increased on net in three years [01-594].

China's fixed asset investment declined 11% nominally from July to November 2025 versus the prior year, while manufacturing investment growth slowed sharply from 9.2% in 2024 to 1.9% [01-617]. However, China's trade surplus surpassed nearly US$1 trillion through November 2025, driven by 10% export volume growth, even as US-bound exports declined 20-30% year-on-year since April due to trade tensions [01-622].

Australia's GDP growth is projected at 2.1% in 2026, up from 1.8% in 2025, though inflation is not expected to return to target until the second half of 2026 [01-584]. The eurozone projects modest growth of 1.2% in 2026, though Germany's 500-billion-euro multi-year infrastructure package provides a significant stimulus [01-593].

### 1.3 Implications for Mineral Demand

The divergent growth picture has distinct mineral demand implications. India's infrastructure buildout and the broader South Asian growth trajectory support demand for copper, zinc, and steel inputs [01-579] [01-583]. China's property crisis depresses iron ore demand [01-619] but its export-oriented manufacturing and clean energy investment sustain demand for energy transition minerals [01-622]. Advanced economy infrastructure spending — notably Germany's fiscal package and US infrastructure programmes — supports base metals demand in Western markets [01-593].

## 2. Interest Rate & Monetary Policy Environment

### 2.1 The Federal Reserve

The Federal Reserve cut interest rates by a cumulative 175 basis points between September 2024 and December 2025, bringing the federal funds rate to 3.50-3.75%, before holding steady at its January 2026 meeting [01-586]. The December 2025 FOMC Summary of Economic Projections shows the median federal funds rate declining to 3.4% in 2026, 3.1% in 2027, with a longer-run neutral rate of 3.0% [01-585]. US GDP is projected at 2.3% in 2026, with PCE inflation declining from 2.9% in 2025 to 2.4% in 2026 and 2.1% in 2027 [01-591].

The path of further easing remains data-dependent. Markets price in roughly two quarter-point cuts later in 2026, though some analysts expect rates to hold given persistent inflation and labour market resilience.

### 2.2 The European Central Bank

The ECB held its deposit facility rate at 2.00%, main refinancing rate at 2.15%, and marginal lending facility at 2.40% as of December 2025 [01-587]. Economists expect rates to remain at 2% throughout 2026 in what is considered a neutral stance. The ECB's staff projections forecast eurozone headline inflation averaging 1.9% in 2026, declining to 1.8% in 2027 [01-588].

### 2.3 The Reserve Bank of Australia

The RBA diverged from the global easing trend, raising the cash rate by 25 basis points to 3.85% in February 2026 [01-589]. The Board cited that inflation had picked up materially in the second half of 2025, that capacity pressures were greater than previously assessed, and that private demand was growing more quickly than expected [01-589]. Further rate rises to 4.10% are expected by May 2026. This tighter monetary environment in Australia has implications for the cost of capital for domestic mining projects and research infrastructure.

### 2.4 The Bank of Japan

The Bank of Japan is expected to raise its policy rate from 0.5% to 1.0% through 2026, while Japan's GDP growth is projected at only 0.4% in 2026, reflecting its ongoing structural challenges [01-590].

### 2.5 Implications for Mining Investment

The OECD expects central banks to lower monetary policy rates to around neutral levels by end-2026, with real interest rates declining as inflation continues to fall and labour market pressures ease [01-580]. Lower interest rates reduce the cost of capital for resource projects, improve project IRRs, and make marginal deposits economically viable.

However, the RBA's tightening cycle stands in contrast [01-589]. Higher domestic interest rates increase the hurdle rate for Australian mining projects and research infrastructure investment, though this is partially offset by the Commonwealth and Queensland governments' concessional finance mechanisms (NAIF, Export Finance Australia, Critical Minerals Facility).

## 3. Inflation & Mining Input Costs

### 3.1 Global Inflation Trajectory

Global inflation is on a clear disinflationary path. The IMF projects global inflation easing to 4.2% in 2025 and 3.6% in 2026 [01-595]. The OECD projects G20 consumer price inflation declining from 3.4% in 2025 to 2.8% in 2026 and 2.5% in 2027 [01-595]. The World Bank projects global inflation declining to 2.6% in 2026 [01-592]. The FOMC projects US core PCE inflation declining from 3.0% in 2025 to 2.5% in 2026 and 2.1% in 2027 [01-591].

While general inflation is moderating, mining-specific cost pressures are more persistent.

### 3.2 Mining Cost Environment

S&P Global's Mine Cost Outlook 2026 identifies a new, elevated cost standard being forged across all metals by persistent inflation, rising energy costs, and declining ore grades [01-596]. The mining sector faces structural upward pressure on all-in sustaining costs (AISC) from equipment and labour costs, the need for more technically complex processing methods to treat lower-grade ore bodies, and stricter environmental and social permitting requirements [01-596].

The copper mining stripping ratio has rebounded from a historical low of 1.47:1 in 2021 to 1.78:1 in 2024, while declining ore quality forces operators to process more material to yield the same amount of copper [01-598]. S&P projects a 543,000 metric ton copper concentrate deficit in 2026, lifting its price forecast to US$11,400 per metric ton [01-598].

The mining sector is bifurcating: precious metals and manganese producers are capitalising on prices outpacing cost inflation, while lithium and nickel producers are being forced to cut costs to survive a period of oversupply [01-605]. The average nickel AISC is forecast to rise 3.9% in 2026 to US$5.89/lb, with a squeezed margin of just US$1.60/lb at consensus prices [01-600]. Lithium and nickel face severe margin pressure, with 47% of lithium and 14% of nickel production likely unprofitable at consensus 2026 prices [01-599].

Gold producers, by contrast, are positioned for record margins of approximately US$2,800/oz in 2026, supported by a projected 24% increase in gold prices and relatively stable AISC around US$1,521/oz [01-597].

### 3.3 Energy Cost Relief

World Bank energy price forecasts provide some offset to mining cost pressures. Brent crude is forecast to fall from US$68/barrel in 2025 to US$60 in 2026 — a five-year low — with overall energy prices declining 12% in 2025 and a further 10% in 2026 [01-603]. Lower diesel and electricity costs benefit open-pit mining operations and processing facilities, though the benefit varies by jurisdiction and energy mix.

## 4. Commodity Price Outlook & Market Dynamics

### 4.1 Headline Commodity Trends

Global commodity prices are projected to fall to their lowest level in six years in 2026, marking the fourth consecutive year of decline, driven by weak global growth, a growing oil surplus, and persistent policy uncertainty [01-601]. However, this headline masks sharp divergence between commodity groups.

Precious metals are at the opposite end of the spectrum. Gold prices rose 42% in 2025 and are projected to increase a further 5% in 2026 [01-604]. Silver prices rose 34% in 2025 with a further 8% projected for 2026, remaining in structural deficit for the fifth consecutive year [01-604] [01-624].

Base metals present a nuanced picture. The World Bank projects base metal prices to rise 3% in 2025, remain broadly stable in 2026, and increase 2% in 2027 [01-602]. Copper and tin are projected to reach new record highs in nominal US dollar terms, while iron ore prices are expected to fall below 2019 levels [01-602] [01-619].

### 4.2 Supply-Side Constraints

Base metals production growth was subdued through the first three quarters of 2025 and is expected to remain weak [01-620]. Output is constrained by operational disruptions and production limits for aluminum and copper, with China nearing its self-imposed 45 million metric ton annual aluminum cap [01-620].

Baker Steel reports that copper faces its largest supply deficit in 22 years in 2026, with mine lead times grown to over 15 years from discovery to production [01-613]. Treatment charges are at negative US$45/t compared to a typical US$50+/t in balanced markets, indicating severe concentrate tightness [01-613].

Rare earth magnet metals are in structural deficit projected to widen to around 22% of supply by 2030, with EV demand currently representing 33% of magnet REE demand and forecast to approach 60% by 2035 [01-618].

Lithium demand is expected to grow by nearly 150% by 2030, with the market currently in surplus but early recovery signs emerging as high-cost producers curtail output [01-625].

## 5. Macroeconomic-Mining Investment Nexus

### 5.1 Historical Price-Investment Correlation

McKinsey finds that the correlation between commodity prices and mining investment spending is 73% over the past decade, with companies typically underinvesting during downcycles and overinvesting during upcycles — especially for junior mines and exploration projects [01-606]. Mining has experienced five commodity price cycles since 2000, with peaks in 2001, 2007, 2010, 2013, and early 2015 followed by abrupt drops [01-607]. McKinsey recommends that miners save during upcycles and invest during downcycles, though most do the opposite [01-607].

### 5.2 Current Exploration Expenditure

Global nonferrous mineral exploration spending fell 3% to US$12.5 billion in 2024, the second consecutive year of decline [01-608]. Junior mining company funding fell 12% to US$10.3 billion — the lowest in five years [01-608]. Grassroots exploration allocation fell 8% to a record low of just 22% of global budgets (US$2.79 billion), signalling reduced pipeline replenishment [01-609].

The commodity-specific exploration picture is divergent. Gold budgets fell 7% to US$5.6 billion despite record prices, while copper rose 2% to US$3.2 billion and lithium surged 30% to over US$1 billion [01-610]. Nickel exploration budgets fell 30% to US$534 million and cobalt fell 35% to US$51.1 million, while uranium surged 33% to US$331 million [01-623].

### 5.3 Mining Sector Capital Expenditure

The combined capital expenditure of the top 19 mining companies reached US$71.0 billion in 2024, a rise of 10.6% compared with 2023, though project spending appears to be peaking and may slow as moderate base metals prices constrain activity [01-616]. Major M&A activity in 2025 — including the proposed US$50+ billion Anglo American-Teck Resources merger and the US$3.3 billion Northern Star-De Grey acquisition — confirms that producers prefer to buy rather than build given the challenges of developing new mines [01-615].

Baker Steel reports that gold rose 64.6% in 2025 with gold miners surging 163.0%, yet the mining sector trades at just 7x EV/EBITDA and accounts for only 1% of global listed equities, suggesting significant undervaluation relative to commodity fundamentals [01-612].

### 5.4 Structural Investment Themes

Baker Steel identifies three converging investment themes for the mining sector in 2026: the New Industrial Revolution (AI, data centres, electrification), geopolitical and industrial strategy (critical minerals security), and shifting macroeconomic conditions (monetary easing, inflation hedging) [01-611]. The US expanded its critical minerals list in November 2025 to include copper, silver, potash, and uranium, signalling the growing strategic importance of minerals previously considered purely industrial commodities [01-621].

Central bank gold reserves increased by 15% as a percentage of global foreign reserves over the past five years, while US dollar holdings declined 3%, reflecting a structural shift in reserve management that supports sustained gold demand [01-614].

## 6. Assessment: Net Impact on Mineral Supply Investment

### 6.1 Supportive Factors

Several macroeconomic conditions actively support new mineral supply investment and, by extension, the business case for MICMRC:

- **Monetary easing across most major economies** is reducing the cost of capital, improving project viability for marginal deposits that require R&D support [01-580] [01-585].
- **Structural supply deficits** in copper (22-year deficit) [01-613], silver (fifth consecutive deficit year) [01-624], and rare earths (projected 22% supply gap by 2030) [01-618] create sustained demand for exploration, development, and processing services.
- **Record precious metals prices** are generating strong cash flows for miners, funding exploration programs and project development in precious metals-rich provinces like the NWMP [01-604] [01-612].
- **Energy transition and AI demand** are decoupled from traditional GDP cycles, providing a structural demand floor for copper, lithium, rare earths, and other minerals central to the MICMRC's focus [01-611].
- **Declining energy prices** (Brent crude at five-year lows) reduce mining input costs, partially offsetting labour and equipment inflation [01-603].

### 6.2 Constraining Factors

- **China's structural slowdown** depresses iron ore demand and weighs on base metals broadly [01-619] [01-617]. China's property crisis and deflationary spiral are the single largest headwind for global mineral demand [01-582] [01-594].
- **Margin pressure on lithium and nickel** — with 47% and 14% of production respectively unprofitable at consensus prices — constrains investment in these critical minerals in the near term [01-599].
- **Australia's tighter monetary policy** (RBA hiking to 3.85% and potentially 4.10%) increases the cost of capital for domestic projects relative to global peers [01-589].
- **Declining exploration budgets** — grassroots exploration at record lows — signal constrained pipeline replenishment, though this also strengthens the long-term case for R&D support infrastructure [01-609] [01-608].

### 6.3 Net Assessment

On balance, macroeconomic conditions are **moderately supportive** for new mineral supply investment. The structural drivers of mineral demand — energy transition, AI infrastructure, geopolitical supply chain diversification — are powerful enough to sustain investment even in a subdued GDP growth environment [01-611]. The key risk is a deeper-than-expected Chinese economic contraction, which would temporarily depress base metals prices and exploration budgets globally [01-581] [01-582].

For MICMRC specifically, the environment is favourable. The centre's value proposition — providing shared-use R&D infrastructure to reduce the cost and risk of bringing new mineral supply online — is most compelling precisely when: (a) new supply is urgently needed but difficult to bring online (as is the case for copper and rare earths) [01-613] [01-618]; (b) ore grades are declining and processing complexity is increasing (requiring more R&D) [01-596] [01-598]; and (c) exploration and development budgets are constrained (making shared infrastructure more attractive than proprietary investment) [01-608] [01-609]. All three conditions prevail in the current macroeconomic environment.
