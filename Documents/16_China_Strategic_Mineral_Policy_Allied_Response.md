# China's Strategic Mineral Policy and Allied Response Dynamics

## Executive Summary

China has built the most comprehensive critical mineral supply chain control system in modern history. Across 19 of 20 strategic minerals analysed by the International Energy Agency, China is the leading refiner, holding an average market share of approximately 70% [01-626]. This dominance is not accidental — it is the product of decades of state-directed investment in domestic exploration, processing infrastructure, strategic stockpiling, technology development, and overseas resource acquisition. Since 2023, Beijing has progressively weaponised this advantage through a cascade of export controls targeting gallium, germanium, graphite, antimony, tungsten, and rare earth elements [01-639] [01-640] [01-641] [01-642]. Allied nations — led by the United States, European Union, Japan, and Australia — have responded with emergency stockpiling, legislative frameworks, bilateral agreements, and innovation programmes. However, the scale of the challenge remains immense: by 2035, the top three mineral refining nations' combined market share is projected to decline only marginally from 86% to 82%, returning to 2020 levels and indicating minimal diversification progress [01-638]. This report maps the dynamics of this strategic competition and assesses its trajectory.

## 1. China's Downstream Processing Dominance

China's control over critical mineral supply chains extends far beyond mining. The country has systematically built dominant positions across the entire value chain — from ore processing through to finished components — creating dependencies that cannot be easily replicated or replaced.

### 1.1 Refining and Processing Scale

China is the leading refiner for 19 out of 20 important strategic minerals analysed by the IEA, with an average market share of around 70% [01-626]. The concentration is intensifying: the average market share of the top three refining nations of key energy minerals rose from 82% in 2020 to 86% in 2024, with approximately 90% of supply growth coming from the top single supplier alone — Indonesia for nickel, and China for cobalt, graphite, and rare earths [01-627].

In rare earth elements, the dominance is particularly stark. China refines approximately 91% of global rare earth separation and refining production and controls 94% of sintered permanent magnet manufacturing, up from approximately 50% two decades ago [01-628]. In 2018, China produced 138,000 tons of neodymium-boron-iron magnets; by 2024, output had grown to approximately 300,000 tons [01-636]. China controlled 99% of global heavy rare earth processing until 2023, with the only competing refinery in Vietnam shut down due to a tax dispute [01-683].

### 1.2 Battery Supply Chain Control

China's dominance extends throughout the battery materials value chain. China dominates 80% or more of midstream and downstream battery supply chain segments globally, with near-monopoly shares of 95% or above in precursor cathode materials and LFP cathode materials [01-630]. The country manufactures 75% of global purified phosphoric acid used in LFP batteries and 95% of high-purity manganese sulphate, a critical component for sodium-ion battery chemistries [01-629]. Two-thirds of global battery recycling capacity growth since 2020 has been in China [01-631].

China controls approximately 60% of global lithium processing capacity, 77% of battery cell manufacturing, and produces 56% of the world's electric vehicles [01-637]. China accounts for 77% of natural graphite production, over 95% of synthetic graphite production, and nearly 100% of graphite refining [01-644].

### 1.3 Concentration Outlook

Even under optimistic diversification scenarios, China's dominance will persist for decades. By 2035, the top three refined material suppliers' average market share is projected to decline only marginally from 86% to 82%, returning to 2020 concentration levels [01-638]. This suggests that current diversification efforts, while necessary, will take many years to meaningfully shift the structural dependency.

## 2. China's Domestic Exploration, Stockpiling and Technology Investment

### 2.1 Domestic Exploration

China has pursued aggressive domestic mineral exploration under its Five-Year Plan framework. During the 14th Five-Year Plan period (2021-2025), China achieved significant growth in reserves of uranium, copper, gold, lithium, and potash through domestic exploration [01-634]. China's 2024 rare earth production quota of 270,000 tonnes of rare earth oxides reflects centralised state management of the sector, balancing domestic supply security with strategic export leverage [01-633].

### 2.2 Strategic Stockpiling

China's revised mineral resources law, entering into force on 1 July 2025, establishes a legal framework for a national critical mineral stockpiling system combining product reserves (physical stockpiles of processed minerals), production capacity reserves (mechanisms to ensure rapid scale-up of output during emergencies), and resource site reserves (designated mineral-rich areas reserved for emergency exploitation) [01-632]. This three-tier system represents the most comprehensive mineral reserve framework of any nation, integrating government and enterprise participation.

### 2.3 Technology and Recycling Investment

China has invested heavily in processing technology, particularly in rare earth separation, battery materials production, and recycling infrastructure. China established the state-owned China Resources Recycling Group Ltd. in 2024 specifically to secure end-of-life critical mineral materials, as part of plans to recycle and refine 75% of global battery materials by 2030 [01-635]. Chinese facilities combine gravity separation, magnetic concentration, flotation chemistry, and solvent extraction into optimised sequences tailored to specific ore types.

## 3. Export Controls: Timeline and Escalation

China has progressively deployed export controls as a strategic instrument since 2023, expanding from niche semiconducting materials to encompass a growing share of the critical mineral supply chain. These restrictions serve as both retaliatory measures against US technology export controls and as strategic signals of China's capacity for economic coercion.

### 3.1 Chronological Escalation

- **3 July 2023**: Export controls imposed on gallium and germanium (licensing requirements), in retaliation for US semiconductor export controls [01-639]. China has a near-total monopoly over gallium production, accounting for 98% of the world's primary supply [01-643].

- **20 October 2023**: Graphite export controls announced, taking effect December 2023 [01-639].

- **15 August 2024**: Dual-use export restrictions on antimony announced, effective 15 September 2024. Antimony shipments from China subsequently dropped 97% while prices rose 200% [01-640].

- **3 December 2024**: China banned exports of gallium, germanium, antimony, and superhard materials to the United States in principle, tightening previous licensing requirements [01-641]. A US Geological Survey report estimated that a total export ban on gallium and germanium could result in a USD 3.4 billion loss in GDP for the US economy [01-645].

- **4 February 2025**: Retaliatory export controls imposed on tungsten [01-642].

- **4 April 2025**: Export controls extended to seven rare earth elements — samarium, gadolinium, terbium, dysprosium, lutetium, scandium, and yttrium [01-642]. European prices subsequently reached up to six times those in China [01-651].

- **9 October 2025**: Expanded export controls requiring licences for parts, components, and assemblies containing Chinese-sourced rare earths — effective immediately for China-made products, expanding to internationally-made products from 1 December 2025 [01-646].

- **November 2025**: Further expansion to five additional rare earth elements (holmium, erbium, thulium, europium, ytterbium), bringing total controlled elements to 12 of 17 known rare earth elements [01-647].

### 3.2 Scope and Impact

More than 50% of energy-related minerals globally now face some form of export controls, according to the IEA [01-648]. China's controls include extraterritorial jurisdiction over products manufactured outside China if they contain Chinese-origin rare earth materials at a value threshold of 0.1% or higher [01-649].

In 2024, China exported 58,000 tonnes of rare earth magnets — sufficient to manufacture millions of cars, industrial motors or aircraft, or thousands of strategic military systems [01-650]. Defence systems directly affected include F-35 fighter jets (containing over 900 pounds of REEs), Virginia-class submarines (approximately 9,200 pounds), and Arleigh Burke destroyers (approximately 5,200 pounds) [01-675].

### 3.3 Partial De-Escalation

Following the US-China trade agreement negotiated in October 2025, China suspended six of its October 2025 export control announcements on 7 November 2025, for one year through 10 November 2026 [01-652]. However, controls on seven medium and heavy rare earth elements, tungsten, and the prohibition on exports to US military end-users remain firmly in force. The legal framework remains intact and could be reinstated at any time.

## 4. Strategic Resource Acquisitions Abroad

### 4.1 Scale and Geographic Focus

China's overseas mining investment totalled US$21.4 billion in metals and mining under the Belt and Road Initiative in 2024, a 10% increase from 2023 and the most active year since at least 2013 [01-653]. Chinese firms increasingly prioritise equity investments in mining, with 10 overseas mining deals worth more than USD 100 million in 2024 — the most since 2013 [01-662]. Chinese mining investments span Africa (primarily cobalt in the DRC, copper in Zambia, and rare earths), Southeast Asia (focusing on nickel in Indonesia), and Latin America (targeting lithium in Chile/Argentina and copper), with the metals and mining sector reaching approximately USD 32.6 billion in BRI engagement in 2025 [01-655].

### 4.2 Africa — Cobalt and Copper

China's most strategically significant overseas mineral position is in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. Chinese state-owned enterprises and policy banks control 80% of the DRC's total cobalt output, while the DRC produces 80% of the world's cobalt [01-654]. Of 33 cobalt exporters in the DRC, 24 are Chinese, including 8 farm-in deals and 14 joint ventures with state-owned Gecamines [01-654]. The Tenke Fungurume Mine, the world's third-largest cobalt producer, is majority-owned by Chinese company CMOC Group Limited [01-663].

### 4.3 Latin America — Lithium

Chinese companies have invested over USD 16 billion in South American lithium projects between 2018 and 2024, with Chinese FDI in lithium extraction across the region quadrupling since 2020 [01-656]. Tianqi Lithium and Ganfeng Lithium collectively control nearly 40% of global lithium production through South American operations [01-657]. Tianqi Lithium holds a USD 4.1 billion stake in Chile's SQM company, one of the world's largest lithium producers [01-660]. Zijin Mining purchased a 25% stake in Rio Tinto's USD 2.5 billion Rincon lithium project in Argentina in 2024 [01-658]. CATL and YPF signed a USD 1.4 billion agreement in February 2025 targeting 30,000 tons of lithium production annually by 2028 [01-659].

The value-chain dynamic reinforces China's processing dominance: Argentina directed 43% of its lithium exports to China in 2023, with China processing the raw material and exporting it back as higher-value battery materials [01-661].

## 5. Allied Nation Countermeasures

### 5.1 United States — Emergency Powers and Stockpiling

The United States has responded with a combination of emergency executive powers, legislative appropriations, and innovation investment. On 20 March 2025, President Trump signed an executive order invoking emergency powers to accelerate domestic mineral production, covering the Department of Interior's critical minerals list plus copper, uranium, gold, and potash [01-664].

Congress appropriated USD 2 billion to the National Defense Stockpile Transaction Fund through the One Big Beautiful Act, on top of a prior fund balance exceeding USD 958 million [01-665]. The Department of Defense announced plans to procure up to USD 1 billion in stockpile materials during 2025, with requests for information issued for scandium, tungsten, graphite, samarium, dysprosium, terbium, indium, bismuth, and vanadium [01-667]. The Trump Administration subsequently announced a USD 12 billion critical minerals stockpile accessible to private industry, financed through private institutional investors and the US Export-Import Bank [01-666].

On the technology front, the US DOE committed USD 500 million to the Battery Materials Program and USD 134 million specifically for rare earth recovery, while ARPA-E awarded USD 17.5 million to Niron Magnetics for rare-earth-free iron nitride magnets [01-678]. However, the scale gap remains vast: MP Materials' projected US magnet output by end-2025 of 1,000 tons represents less than 1% of current Chinese production of approximately 300,000 tons [01-674].

### 5.2 European Union — Critical Raw Materials Act

The EU Critical Raw Materials Act, adopted in March 2024, sets 2030 benchmarks of 10% domestic extraction, 40% processing, and 25% recycling, while capping single-country dependencies at 65% [01-668]. The European Commission approved 60 strategic projects under the CRMA by 2025, spanning 13 EU member states and 13 third countries, covering 14 of the 17 strategic raw materials [01-669].

Projected outcomes are significant: dependency on a single country for rare earth extraction is projected to fall from 95% to 42%, and EU dependency on gallium imports from 71% to 17%, with potential full germanium supply independence by 2030 [01-670]. The European Commission committed EUR 3 billion through the RESourceEU Action Plan to diversify critical materials supply [01-671].

### 5.3 Japan — Diversification Pioneer

Japan provides the most successful case study of reducing China dependency. Japan reduced rare earth imports from China from 85% in 2009 to 58% in 2020 through technological innovation to reduce dysprosium use and alternative sourcing from the US and Australia [01-672]. However, Japan remains vulnerable: on 6 January 2025, China banned dual-use exports to Japan that could enhance defence capabilities, representing the first country-specific ban extending beyond the United States [01-673].

### 5.4 Multilateral Coordination

The Minerals Security Partnership (MSP) comprises 14 countries plus the EU, representing approximately 2.79 billion people — more than double China's population [01-676]. In February 2026, the MSP evolved into the FORGE initiative, marking a transformation from multilateral coordination to an institutional alliance approach [01-681]. Australia's 2024 directive ordering divestment from Northern Minerals by Chinese-linked investors serves as a precedent for allied foreign investment screening in critical minerals [01-682].

## 6. Strategic Competition Trajectory

### 6.1 Escalation Signals

Several indicators suggest continued strategic escalation:

- China's export controls now cover 12 of 17 rare earth elements with extraterritorial reach [01-647] [01-649]
- The legal framework for reinstating suspended controls remains intact through November 2026 [01-652]
- China's overseas mining investment continues accelerating, with BRI mining investment reaching record levels [01-653] [01-655]
- China's 2025 revised mineral resources law institutionalises strategic stockpiling capacity [01-632]
- The transition from MSP to FORGE signals allied willingness to create institutional structures that mirror China's approach [01-681]

### 6.2 De-Escalation Signals

Partial de-escalation has occurred through:

- The November 2025 one-year suspension of certain October 2025 export controls [01-652]
- Continued bilateral trade negotiations between the US and China
- China's recognition that sustained export restrictions erode long-term market position by incentivising alternative supply development

### 6.3 Assessment

The Council on Foreign Relations assesses that "the United States cannot out-mine and out-process China" and should instead "leapfrog China's dominance by scaling disruptive innovation, recovery, and recycling" [01-677]. Unilateral US action is insufficient because America represents only 1.7% of global rare earth consumption and 13.6% of germanium consumption [01-680]. The CSIS proposes harmonised tariffs and phased sourcing quotas — 10% from anchor market countries in year one, 25% by year three, and 60% by year ten — as the most viable path to creating a parallel market system [01-679].

The trajectory indicates that this competition will intensify over the medium term. China's structural advantages in processing capacity, integrated supply chains, and overseas resource positions will take allied nations at least a decade to meaningfully counterbalance [01-638]. For mineral-rich allied nations like Australia, this creates a window of strategic opportunity to develop domestic processing capacity while demand for non-Chinese supply sources grows.

## 7. Implications for the MICMRC

This strategic competition directly supports the business case for the Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre:

1. **Processing R&D Demand**: Allied nations' urgent need to develop alternative mineral processing capacity creates sustained demand for the type of bench-scale metallurgical testing and process flowsheet development that the MICMRC will provide.

2. **Technology Sovereignty**: China's export controls on rare earth processing technology (since December 2023) mean that non-Chinese nations must develop indigenous processing knowledge — exactly the capability the MICMRC's advanced metallurgy lab and mineral beneficiation testing facilities will build.

3. **Government Funding Alignment**: The scale of allied government investment (US: $12B+ stockpile; EU: EUR 3B RESourceEU; Australia: $1.25B Iluka refinery loan) demonstrates that public funding for mineral processing infrastructure is expanding rapidly [01-666] [01-671].

4. **NWMP Strategic Value**: The North West Minerals Province's endowment of copper, cobalt, vanadium, rare earths, and zinc positions it to supply the diversified critical mineral processing capacity that allied nations are actively seeking.

5. **Training Pipeline**: The acute global shortage of mineral processing expertise, combined with China's technology export controls, increases the value of the MICMRC's Critical Minerals Learning Centre as a training facility for the next generation of non-Chinese processing capability.
