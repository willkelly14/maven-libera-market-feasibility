# Transport, Energy, and Water Infrastructure Constraints on Mineral Supply Response

## Introduction

The global push to scale critical mineral production confronts a fundamental physical reality: minerals cannot be mined, processed, or exported without adequate transport, energy, and water infrastructure. While demand projections and geological endowments attract policy attention and investment capital, the rate at which supply can actually respond is often determined not by the mineral deposit itself but by whether the port can handle the throughput, whether the rail can carry the tonnage, whether the power grid can supply the load, and whether there is enough water for processing. This report assesses whether physical infrastructure constitutes a binding constraint on mineral supply response — globally and in Australia's North West Minerals Province specifically — and examines the implications for the MICMRC feasibility study.

## 1. Port Capacity and Shipping Route Congestion

### Australia's Mineral Export Port Network

Australia's mineral export infrastructure is anchored by a network of specialised ports. The Port of Townsville is Northern Australia's largest multi-cargo port and the nation's number one port for exports of copper, zinc, and lead, handling more than 30 commodities with trade valued at $10 billion in 2023-24 [01-684]. Dry bulk cargo, including mineral concentrates and ores, comprises approximately 70% of the port's total throughput, with major mineral exports originating from northwest Queensland mining regions including Glencore, South32 (Cannington), and MMG (Dugald River) [01-686].

The port's $1.6 billion Port Expansion Project is a 30-year development plan that will see annual trade triple to 30 million tonnes per annum by 2050, with an Outer Harbour Development valued at $690 million [01-685]. This long-term expansion positions Townsville to absorb projected growth from the NWMP's mineral development pipeline.

At the other end of the scale, Port Hedland — the world's largest bulk export terminal — handled 577.7 Mt of cargo in FY2025 and achieved 49.2 million tonnes of iron ore exports in January 2026 alone, demonstrating the throughput volumes Australia's port infrastructure can achieve when purpose-built for mineral export [01-687].

### Global Shipping Route Vulnerabilities

The vulnerability of maritime supply chains was starkly demonstrated in early 2024, when simultaneous disruptions to the Suez and Panama Canals reduced Suez Canal trade by 50% and Panama Canal trade by 32% from a year earlier [01-688]. The Suez Canal's freight tonnage declined 45% as more than 80% of container vessels were diverted around the Cape of Good Hope [01-688]. Container shipping costs surged over 150%, rising from approximately US$1,400 in December 2023 to nearly US$3,800 by January 2024 [01-689].

The Panama Canal ordinarily carries approximately 40% of all U.S. container traffic, and drought conditions in 2023-24 reduced daily transits from a normal 36 to just 22 vessels per day — a 36% decline [01-690]. With between 80-90% of world goods transported by sea, these disruptions demonstrated that shipping route congestion and climatic vulnerability represent real risks to mineral supply chains [01-689].

For Australia, these shipping disruptions are less directly damaging to mineral exports (which predominantly ship through the Indian and Pacific Oceans rather than through the Suez or Panama Canals) but they illustrate the broader fragility of maritime logistics and can affect the cost and timing of equipment imports for mining operations.

## 2. Rail and Road Logistics in Major Mining Provinces

### The Mount Isa-Townsville Rail Corridor

The Mount Isa Line extends over 1,000 kilometres from Mount Isa to the Port of Townsville, serving as Queensland's primary mineral export corridor for copper concentrate, zinc-lead concentrate, phosphate rock, and fertiliser [01-691]. Queensland Transport Minister Bart Mellish has described the line as "the backbone of the North West economy" [01-691].

The corridor faces a significant capacity challenge. Future demand on the line is estimated at 20 million tonnes per year under a moderate scenario, compared to 2011 when the line carried 6 million tonnes with a theoretical capacity of 7.5 million tonnes — a nearly three-fold increase in projected demand over existing capacity [01-693]. Despite this gap, the Mount Isa-Townsville Rail Corridor Upgrade was excluded from Infrastructure Australia's 2026 Infrastructure Priority List, with the proponent required to complete business case development for future consideration [01-696].

The Queensland Government is addressing rail competitiveness through a new 10% subsidy on below-rail access charges for four years, part of a $300 million critical minerals investment package [01-692]. For a phosphate customer moving 0.5 million tonnes per annum, this subsidy delivers approximately $2.80 per tonne savings ($5.6 million over four years) [01-692]. The Department of Transport and Main Roads has also committed $2 million to investigate barriers to rail usage, focusing on challenges for small and medium-sized critical mineral producers and incentives to shift freight from road to rail [01-695].

A key bottleneck at the Townsville end is being addressed through the proposed Townsville Eastern Access Rail Corridor (TEARC) — an 8.3 km rail freight line that would connect the North Coast rail line directly into the Port of Townsville, creating capacity for growing tonnage demand and reducing freight trains through Townsville's city centre [01-694].

### Global Comparisons: Infrastructure as a Binding Constraint

Globally, transport infrastructure is among the most severe constraints on mineral supply in developing jurisdictions. The DRC's mining sector is severely constrained by inadequate road networks, outdated rail systems, and underdeveloped ports [01-697]. Aging rail infrastructure forces some companies to use more expensive alternatives, while congestion at key export hubs like Matadi port further impacts logistics efficiency [01-697].

The Lobito Corridor — a revitalised 1,300 km railway from Angola's Lobito port to the DRC copperbelt at Kolwezi — represents the most significant infrastructure response to this constraint [01-698]. Backed by over US$10 billion in Western and private investment, including a US$553 million DFC loan and US$200 million from the Development Bank of Southern Africa, the corridor aims to provide a direct and efficient export route for copper and cobalt from the DRC and Zambia to Atlantic markets [01-698].

The contrast is instructive: in mature mining jurisdictions like Australia, transport infrastructure exists but requires upgrading; in frontier jurisdictions like the DRC, it must be built from scratch at enormous cost, significantly delaying supply response.

## 3. Energy Supply Adequacy and Cost for Mineral Processing

### The NWMP Energy Challenge

Energy supply is arguably the single most significant infrastructure constraint facing the North West Minerals Province. The North West Power System (NWPS) has a total generation capacity of 454 MW serving Mount Isa and Cloncurry, with energy demand of 300 MW [01-699]. Critically, Glencore accounts for 51% of this demand, with domestic usage representing just 9% [01-699].

Mount Isa's electricity prices are currently double the average for NEM-connected users, with the region's overreliance on liquefied natural gas leaving it vulnerable to volatile pricing that has risen since 2011 [01-700]. Energy constraints are explicitly identified as "a handbrake on development and liveability" [01-700].

The energy cost burden has had tangible consequences. Glencore declared its Mount Isa copper smelter unviable, citing unprecedented smelting market conditions, high energy, gas and labour costs, and a shortage of copper concentrates [01-706]. The smelter is expected to continue operating only to 2030 subject to additional capital investment approval [01-706]. The closure of Glencore's underground copper operations in H2 2025 will slash demand on the NWPS — potentially creating spare capacity but also undermining the economic case for existing gas-fired generation.

### CopperString 2032: The Transformative Investment

CopperString 2032 is the region's defining infrastructure response. The project will build 840 km of new high-voltage transmission line from south of Townsville to Mount Isa, connecting the NWMP to the National Electricity Market [01-701]. The Eastern Link — 350 km of 330 kV line from Reid River to Hughenden — will support 1.7 GW of transfer capacity and 2.9 GW of hosting capacity [01-701].

CopperString is expected to reduce delivered electricity costs by 40% and average wholesale prices by 50% for North West Queensland [01-703]. The $225 million Flinders Substation received approval in December 2025 and is proceeding to construction, while the Hughenden Workforce Accommodation Facility was completed in November 2025 [01-704]. Procurement has been active, with 55 works packages released, 3,163 expressions of interest received, and 876 suppliers registered (345 local, 716 with Queensland offices) [01-727].

However, CopperString's cost escalation is a cautionary example of infrastructure capital challenges. The estimated cost has risen from $1.8 billion in 2020 to $5 billion in 2023 to $9 billion post-election to nearly $14 billion including essential network connections [01-702]. The Queensland Government has locked in $2.4 billion in the 2025 Budget [01-702]. A $200 million North West Energy Fund will support local renewable energy, battery storage, and microgrids in the interim [01-705], while a $30 million community legacy fund will flow from 2025-26 onwards [01-725].

### Global Energy Constraints on Mineral Processing

The challenge of powering mineral processing in remote regions is not unique to Australia. Indonesia's nickel industry consumed approximately 100 billion kilowatt-hours of electricity in 2023, with 97% coming from coal-fired captive power plants built outside the national grid [01-707]. This captive coal capacity has increased 800% since 2013 to roughly 20 GW, projected to grow to 29 GW by the end of the decade [01-708]. It now accounts for 23% of total national coal capacity and is responsible for more than half of additional coal capacity being commissioned nationally [01-708].

More broadly, electricity costs for mining operations typically range from $0.08-0.15/kWh in grid-connected regions but can exceed $0.30/kWh at remote diesel-dependent mines, where diesel fuel can represent up to 40% of an underground mine's energy costs [01-709].

## 4. Water Availability Constraints in Arid Mining Jurisdictions

### The Global Water-Mining Nexus

Water scarcity represents one of the most fundamental physical constraints on mineral supply expansion. According to the World Resources Institute, at least 16% of the world's land-based critical mineral mines, deposits, and districts are located in areas facing high or extremely high water stress, with an additional 8% in arid, low-water-use areas vulnerable to future stress [01-710]. By 2050 under business-as-usual projections, 20% of critical mineral locations would face high or extremely high water stress [01-710].

The IEA notes that in 2024, 7% of global copper supply was at risk of disruption due to floods or droughts, a figure set to rise as climate change intensifies [01-712]. The most water-stressed critical mineral jurisdictions include the United States, Australia, South Africa, India, China, Mongolia, Chile, and Namibia.

### Chile: The Desalination Imperative

Chile's copper mining industry provides the clearest example of water scarcity forcing massive infrastructure investment. In the Salar de Atacama region, lithium and copper extraction have reportedly consumed over 65% of the local water supply [01-711]. Chilean miners have responded with billion-dollar desalination investments: Antofagasta Minerals inaugurated a more than US$2 billion desalination plant in 2024 for Los Pelambres, while BHP spent more than US$3 billion on Escondida's desalination plant processing 2,500 litres per second [01-713].

The addition of seawater desalination to a large-scale mining project adds at least US$1 billion to project capital expenditure, with desalinated water costing between US$0.50 and US$2 per cubic metre — up to ten times more expensive than groundwater [01-714]. These costs fundamentally alter project economics and create a significant barrier to supply expansion in arid regions.

### Australia: Olympic Dam and the Great Artesian Basin

BHP's Olympic Dam uses 35 megalitres of Great Artesian Basin water daily — the largest industrial use of underground water in the southern hemisphere — pumped from bore fields 110 km and 200 km north of the mine [01-715]. The proposed Northern Water Project plans a 260 ML/day desalination plant and 600 km pipeline to serve mining, defence, and pastoral industries, demonstrating the scale of infrastructure required to support mineral processing in inland Australia [01-715].

### The NWMP Water Position

Mount Isa's water supply is comparatively well-established for a remote mining region. Lake Julius, completed in 1978 on the Leichhardt River 70 km northeast of Mount Isa, has a full supply capacity of 107,500 ML and supplies bulk water to Mount Isa Mines and Mount Isa City Council [01-716]. High priority allocations stand at 100% for 2025-26 [01-716]. The Mount Isa Water Board, established in 1973 following a historic 1971 drought, purchased the remaining water assets from Mount Isa Mines in 2010 and now operates independently [01-726].

The 2024-25 Queensland state budget included $7.9 million to improve Mount Isa Water Board's water infrastructure, including electricity upgrades at Lake Julius and renewal of the Fred Haigh Pump Station [01-717].

While Mount Isa's water position is adequate for current and near-term needs, any significant expansion of mineral processing activity beyond current levels would need to be assessed against available water allocations. For a research and testing facility like the MICMRC, water requirements are modest relative to large-scale mining operations, and the existing water infrastructure is more than sufficient.

## 5. Lead Times and Capital Costs for Infrastructure Expansion

### Mine Development Timelines

The time required to bring new mineral supply online has lengthened dramatically. S&P Global data shows the average lead time for new mine development jumped to 17.9 years for mines started in 2020-23, up from 12.7 years for 2005-09 mines [01-718]. The lengthening is driven by extended exploration, permitting, and studies phases, and by longer periods between feasibility study completion and construction start (attributed to obtaining financing and construction permits) [01-718].

Lead times vary significantly by mineral and jurisdiction. Copper mines are among the slowest to develop, taking an average of 24.1 years globally (31.8 years in the US), while gold mines are fastest at 20.8 years [01-719]. The US average lead time of 19.1 years exceeds the global average of 15.5 years [01-719].

### Capital Cost Challenges

Capital cost performance in mining is poor. McKinsey analysis of 80 global mining projects (budgets from US$0.3 billion to over US$5 billion) found that cost and scheduling challenges affect 83% of recent major mining and metals projects, with capital expenditure overruns of more than 40% and schedule delays of 20-30% [01-720]. Large projects over US$1 billion have worse predictability, with only 8-10% avoiding cost overruns and schedule delays (compared with 20% for smaller projects) [01-721]. Copper projects specifically underperform, with 33% experiencing schedule delays of more than 30% versus 13% for iron projects [01-721].

The IEA estimates around US$500 billion in new capital investment is required for mining between now and 2040 under its Stated Policies Scenario, rising to approximately US$600 billion under the Announced Pledges Scenario [01-722]. Critically, capital costs for projects in diversified regions are typically around 50% higher than for incumbent producers — creating a significant barrier to supply chain diversification [01-722]. Investment momentum weakened in 2024, with spending rising by just 5%, down from 14% in 2023 [01-723].

### The NWMP Infrastructure Investment Programme

The NWMP benefits from a substantial committed and planned infrastructure investment programme that significantly differentiates it from greenfield mining jurisdictions:

- **CopperString 2032**: $2.4 billion committed in the 2025 Budget, with total project cost approaching $14 billion for the full 840 km transmission line [01-702]
- **Port of Townsville Expansion**: $1.6 billion, 30-year development plan with $690 million Outer Harbour [01-685]
- **Mount Isa Rail Line**: $300 million critical minerals investment package including rail subsidies [01-692]
- **TEARC**: 8.3 km rail freight corridor to eliminate the port bottleneck [01-694]
- **Water infrastructure**: $7.9 million for Lake Julius and pump station upgrades [01-717]
- **North West Energy Fund**: $200 million for renewable energy, battery storage, and microgrids [01-705]
- **Community Legacy Fund**: $30 million from 2025-26 for community infrastructure and housing [01-725]

The North West Minerals Province is estimated to contain $500 billion in critical mineral deposits including copper, lead, zinc, silver, phosphate, and rare earth elements — providing the demand justification for this infrastructure investment [01-724].

## 6. Implications for the MICMRC

The infrastructure analysis reveals several important implications for the feasibility of a critical minerals research centre in Mount Isa:

**Energy transformation underway**: CopperString 2032 will connect the NWMP to the NEM by 2032, reducing electricity costs by 40-50% and enabling access to renewable energy [01-703]. This fundamentally changes the operating cost structure for any new facility in the region, including the MICMRC.

**Transport infrastructure adequate**: The Mount Isa-Townsville rail corridor and the Port of Townsville provide established export pathways for the NWMP's mineral production [01-691] [01-684]. While capacity upgrades are needed [01-693], the basic infrastructure exists — unlike in frontier mining jurisdictions where it must be built from scratch [01-697].

**Water supply secure**: Lake Julius's 107,500 ML capacity with 100% high-priority allocation provides a secure water supply [01-716]. The MICMRC's water requirements for laboratory and pilot-scale processing are minimal compared to full-scale mining operations.

**Timing advantage**: With 17.9-year average mine development lead times [01-718] and 83% of major projects experiencing cost overruns [01-720], mining companies face significant incentives to de-risk projects early. A research centre that can provide bench-scale metallurgical testing, mineral characterisation, and process flowsheet development reduces the time and cost of project feasibility studies — directly addressing the lead-time bottleneck.

**Infrastructure investment signals commitment**: The $2.4 billion CopperString commitment [01-702], $1.6 billion port expansion [01-685], and $300 million rail investment [01-692] signal long-term government commitment to the NWMP as a minerals province. This infrastructure pipeline provides confidence that the region will remain a productive mining jurisdiction for decades, supporting the MICMRC's long-term business case.

## Conclusion

Physical infrastructure is indeed a binding constraint on the pace at which global mineral supply can respond to demand — but the nature and severity of the constraint varies dramatically by jurisdiction. In frontier mining regions like the DRC, infrastructure deficits are so severe that billions of dollars in corridor-scale investment are required before minerals can reach markets [01-697] [01-698]. In mature but remote mining provinces like Australia's NWMP, the infrastructure exists but requires upgrading and supplementation.

The NWMP's infrastructure position is strengthening. CopperString 2032 addresses the region's most critical constraint (energy cost and availability) [01-701], the Port of Townsville expansion ensures export capacity will keep pace with production growth [01-685], and rail subsidies and corridor investments maintain the competitiveness of the inland-to-port supply chain [01-692]. Water supply, while always requiring careful management in arid regions, is adequate for current operations and foreseeable expansion [01-716].

For the MICMRC specifically, the infrastructure environment is favourable. The centre's energy, water, and transport requirements are modest relative to mining operations, and the committed infrastructure investment programme ensures that the region's broader industrial ecosystem will support — not constrain — a research facility. The centre's core purpose of reducing project development lead times and costs directly addresses what this analysis identifies as the most consequential infrastructure constraint of all: time.
