# Climate Physical Risk to Mineral Supply Chains

## Executive Summary

Climate change poses a material and growing threat to global mineral production and supply chain reliability. Extreme weather events -- floods, cyclones, droughts, and heatwaves -- are already disrupting mining operations across every major producing jurisdiction, with documented production losses running into billions of dollars annually. The International Energy Agency reports that in 2024, 7% of global copper supply was at risk of disruption from floods or droughts alone, a figure projected to rise as climate hazards intensify [01-728]. For the North West Minerals Province (NWMP) and Mount Isa region specifically, the convergence of cyclone exposure, flood-prone logistics corridors, and extreme inland heat creates a distinctive risk profile that must be factored into any long-term infrastructure investment decision -- including the proposed Mount Isa Critical Minerals and Rare Earth Research Centre (MICMRC). Paradoxically, the very climate risks that threaten mineral supply also strengthen the case for diversified, resilient research infrastructure located close to mineral deposits, reducing supply chain vulnerability and supporting adaptive mining practices.

## 1. Global Mining Region Climate Exposure

### 1.1 The Scale of Exposure

The mining industry's exposure to climate physical risk is both widespread and accelerating. Heavy rainfalls, withering droughts, and other extreme weather patterns are denting miners' profits and crimping supply of iron ore, copper, and other widely-used minerals as climate change affects yet another industry [01-735]. The IEA's Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2025 identifies extreme weather alongside technical failures and trade disruptions as key sources of supply shock vulnerability, warning that a sustained disruption could increase global average battery pack prices by 40-50% [01-729].

Between 30 and 50 percent of global production of copper, gold, iron ore, and zinc is already concentrated in areas where water stress is high [01-730]. Mining sites accounting for roughly $150 billion in total annual revenues are clustered into seven water-stress hot spots: Central Asia, the Chilean coast, eastern Australia, the Middle East, southern Africa, western Australia, and a large zone in western North America [01-733]. These hot spots are projected to worsen in the coming decades.

### 1.2 Commodity-Specific Vulnerability

Different minerals face different dominant climate hazards:

- **Iron ore and zinc** are the most exposed to extremely high flood occurrence, at 50 percent and 40 percent of global production volume, respectively [01-732].
- **Copper** faces acute water scarcity risk: in Chile, the world's largest copper producer, 80 percent of production is already located in extremely high water-stressed and arid areas, and by 2040 this will be 100 percent [01-731]. Antofagasta Plc has already forecast reduced copper production due to an ongoing drought lasting more than a decade [01-737].
- **Nickel** supply is increasingly threatened by the environmental consequences of rapid expansion, particularly in Indonesia, where mining has cleared nearly 80,000 hectares of forest, contributing to increasingly common flooding events and mining-related disasters such as landslides and flash floods [01-785].
- **Manganese** demonstrated acute cyclone vulnerability when Tropical Cyclone Megan in March 2024 forced South32 to withdraw its FY2024 manganese output forecast for its GEMCO operation -- which accounts for 87% of Australia's manganese production -- after flooding mining pits and causing major structural damage to wharf and port infrastructure [01-745].

### 1.3 Heat Stress and Worker Productivity

Extreme heat in already hot mining regions -- particularly Australia, China, and parts of North and West Africa -- decreases worker productivity and raises cooling costs, while also putting workers' health at risk [01-786]. Most mining infrastructure is still being built based on historical weather assumptions rather than the more extreme conditions projected for a warmer planet, with many facilities relying on seven-day weather forecasts that leave insufficient preparation time for extreme events [01-765].

## 2. Historical Production Disruptions

### 2.1 The 2022 Global Weather Disruption

The year 2022 illustrated the breadth of climate-related mining disruptions across multiple continents simultaneously:

- **Anglo American** slashed its dividend after heavy rains hurt iron ore production in Brazil, coal mining in Australia, and platinum mining in South Africa. CEO Duncan Wanblad stated: "The extremes that we saw in quarter one of this year outpaced all reasonable forecasting ability that we had" [01-736].
- **Rio Tinto's** iron ore shipments from Australia's Pilbara region fell 2% in H1 2022 compared with the same period in 2021, partly due to "significantly higher than average rainfall in May" [01-738].
- **Vale SA**, one of the world's largest iron ore miners, reported output of steel-producing mineral dropped in Q1 2022 due to torrential rains in Brazil [01-741].
- **Lundin Mining** cut its 2022 copper production forecast following heavy rains at its Chapada mine in Brazil, with CEO Peter Rockandel stating: "We are reviewing a few different scenarios to adjust to the likelihood that there are further strange weather patterns" [01-742].
- **ArcelorMittal's** steel production in South Africa fell nearly one-third due to severe KwaZulu-Natal flooding in April 2022, which killed over 440 people [01-740].
- **Trevali Mining's** zinc mine in Burkina Faso experienced flash floods in April 2022 that killed 8 miners [01-739].
- **Sibanye Stillwater** shut its Montana platinum mines after rapid snowmelt damaged roads and bridges [01-739].

### 2.2 Queensland Floods 2010-2011

The 2010-2011 Queensland floods remain the most significant weather-related mining disruption in Australian history. Queensland was losing up to A$100 million per day in coal exports, with about 15% of the state's annual coal output lost [01-743]. The disruption resulted in a 32% decrease in Australia's metallurgical coal exports in the March quarter and a corresponding A$2-2.5 billion decrease in the sector's overall export revenues, while driving hard coking coal to a record price [01-744].

### 2.3 Cyclone Megan and GEMCO (2024)

Tropical Cyclone Megan's impact on South32's GEMCO manganese operation on Groote Eylandt in March 2024 provides a contemporary case study in cyclone vulnerability [01-745]. The cyclone caused flooding in mining pits and significant damage to a critical haul road bridge connecting northern pits to the processing plant, alongside major structural damage to the wharf and port infrastructure. South32 committed $125 million to repair the damage, with Australian manganese saleable output down 34% to 2.32 million tonnes in FY2024 and Australia's overall manganese production projected to have decreased by 32.1% in 2024 to 4.6 million tonnes [01-746]. The cyclone also caused a noticeable dip in South32's share price [01-749].

### 2.4 Australian Cyclone History and Mining

Australia's mining regions have a long history of cyclone disruptions:

- **Cyclone Bobby (1995)** disrupted gold and mineral mining in southern Western Australia, closing landing strips at Leinster and Wiluna and impeding nickel mining at Mount Keith, causing estimated damage of $11 million (1995 AUD) [01-747].
- **Cyclone Vance (1999)** struck the Pilbara coast causing significant damage to Exmouth and numerous isolated mining camps, with total damage of $100 million (1999 AUD) [01-748].
- **Cyclone Yasi (2011)** crossed the coast as a Category 5 system with estimated maximum gusts of 285 km/h, maintaining cyclonic intensity further inland than normal and finally dissipating near Mount Isa, causing AU$3.5 billion in total damage [01-784].

### 2.5 Quantifying Production Losses

At the individual operation level, experts have observed 10 percent annual production losses from wet weather at open-pit coal mines [01-750]. Looking forward, around 15% of production from selected gold mines could be compromised by 2030 under worst-case climate scenarios [01-750]. Multiple lines of scientific evidence show that sub-daily rainfall extremes have already increased in intensity compared to previous decades due to human-caused global warming, according to University of Melbourne climate scientist Dr Andrew Dowdy [01-788]. CSIRO has stated that "as climate changes, extreme weather events are growing in frequency and will inevitably lead to productivity, safety, and environmental challenges for mines" [01-789].

## 3. Insurance and Investor Climate Risk Pricing

### 3.1 Mining Insurance Market Trends

The mining insurance market is undergoing a structural shift in how climate physical risk is assessed and priced. Mining insurers are intensifying scrutiny of weather resilience, with Lockton noting that "weather related events are increasing in severity and insurers are focusing on how clients manage, and plan to respond to, potential heavy rain / flooding events" [01-751].

The mining industry experienced significant claims activity throughout 2022, 2023, and the first half of 2024, with the main areas of concern for insurers being tailings dam failures, climate-related issues, severe events impacting operations and supply chains, and geopolitical instability [01-755]. Mining insurers are now applying commodity price caps of 15-25% and Business Interruption Volatility Clauses of 10-15% on mining insurance policies, reflecting the growing financial exposure from weather-related operational disruptions [01-752].

### 3.2 Global Insurance Capacity and Natural Catastrophe Trends

Global capacity for any single mining risk is assessed at approximately $1.5 billion in 2025, according to the WTW Mining Risk Review 2025 [01-753]. However, the broader natural catastrophe insurance context is increasingly challenging: natural catastrophes caused more than $100 billion in insured losses in 2025, the sixth consecutive year above that threshold, with WTW warning that the apparent respite masks a structurally higher "risk floor" for insurers [01-754].

### 3.3 ESG and Climate Disclosure Requirements

ESG performance now directly influences mining insurance underwriting decisions, with investors and insurers closely assessing ESG credentials and weaker credentials risking reduced coverage and capacity [01-756]. The TCFD framework, now incorporated into the ISSB's IFRS S2 Climate-related Disclosures Standard, requires mining companies to disclose both acute and chronic physical climate risks, with major miners Anglo American, BHP, and Rio Tinto among the first to publish TCFD-aligned reports [01-757].

However, significant gaps remain in how financial institutions translate physical climate risk into pricing. Nearly two-thirds of British and European investors disclosed some form of climate scenario analysis, but only about one in five disclosed financial impacts for physical risks and one in ten for transition risks [01-758]. This gap between risk awareness and financial quantification means that climate physical risk may be systematically underpriced in mining asset valuations -- a concern for long-lived infrastructure investments in exposed regions.

## 4. Projected Climate Hazard Changes Through 2040

### 4.1 Global Temperature and Extreme Weather Trajectories

There is more than a 50% chance that global temperature rise will reach or surpass 1.5 degrees C between 2021 and 2040 across studied scenarios, according to the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report [01-759]. At 2 degrees C of global warming, heat extremes would more often reach critical tolerance thresholds for agriculture and health [01-760]. Already, the very high monthly maximum temperatures that occurred under 2% of the time in 1960-1989 are now occurring 11% of the time -- about 6 times as often [01-760].

Extreme weather is now ranked as the second most likely cause of a global crisis in 2025 according to the World Economic Forum's Global Risks Report, with the World Meteorological Organization indicating an 86% chance that at least one year between 2025 and 2029 will exceed 1.5 degrees C above pre-industrial levels [01-766].

### 4.2 Water Stress Projections for Mining Regions

McKinsey's analysis using the MineSpans database covering more than 3,000 mines worldwide projects significant shifts in water stress by 2040 [01-734]:

- 5 percent of current gold production will likely shift from low-medium water stress to medium-high
- 7 percent of zinc production could move from medium-high to high water stress
- 6 percent of copper production could shift from high to extremely high water stress
- Chile's copper sector will move from 80% to 100% of production in extremely high water-stressed areas [01-731]

These projections confirm that the availability of key inputs such as water and energy will physically and financially constrain the establishment of new mining operations or make existing operations uneconomical as climate change alters supply patterns [01-787].

### 4.3 Australian Climate Projections

CSIRO projects continued warming in Australia with more extremely hot days and fewer extremely cool days, with temperature increases generally expected to be greater in the interior of Australia than near the coast -- directly relevant to inland mining regions like the NWMP [01-761]. The projections also include more intense short-duration heavy rainfall events, even in regions where average rainfall decreases or stays the same, alongside likely increases in the average duration of drought and aridity in the south and east [01-762].

## 5. NWMP-Specific Climate Exposure

### 5.1 Queensland Climate Context

Queensland has already warmed by approximately 1.5 degrees C since records began in 1910, with three of the five years from 2019 to 2023 amongst the state's top ten warmest on record and most warming concentrated in the last 50-60 years [01-780]. Under a high emissions scenario (RCP8.5), Queensland's average annual temperature is projected to increase by a further 1.3-2.5 degrees C (central estimate 1.9 degrees C) by mid-century [01-763].

The state faces a paradoxical combination of both flood and drought risk: Queensland has experienced a net increase in the number of heavy to extreme rain events since 1951, while 60% of the state has consistently been under drought declaration over the past decade [01-790] [01-781]. Inland Queensland experiences approximately 3,000mm of annual evaporation compared to less than 2,400mm in coastal areas, demonstrating the intense water stress facing inland mining regions [01-781].

### 5.2 Cyclone and Flood Risk

According to the 2023 Queensland State Disaster Risk Report, flooding is the first-priority hazard for Queensland, followed by tropical cyclones as the second-priority hazard [01-768]. Eight severe tropical cyclones impacted Queensland between January 2020 and April 2024 alone, with Severe Tropical Cyclone Kirrily in 2024 producing heavy rainfall and widespread flooding in western Queensland [01-783].

The frequency of tropical cyclones is projected to decrease up to 2060, but cyclones are projected to be of significantly higher intensity on average, with cyclones travelling slower and southward and increasing rainfall intensity giving rise to potentially extreme associated flooding [01-767]. Queensland is also projected to experience 40% more very high fire danger days and longer fire seasons [01-764].

### 5.3 Heatwave Projections for Mount Isa

Climate models project that the frequency of heatwaves may exceed 18 days per year by 2030 in Mount Isa, with individual heatwave events lasting up to 9 days [01-770]. Heatwaves are the fifth highest risk management priority for Queensland and claim more lives than any other natural hazard in Queensland and Australia, with an observable increase in heatwaves of all severities since 1958 [01-782]. By 2090, heatwaves in tropical Queensland may experience massive intensification, occurring one-third of the year and lasting more than 50 days in Townsville [01-771].

### 5.4 The Townsville Logistics Corridor

The Mount Isa-Townsville logistics corridor -- comprising approximately 900 km of rail and road connecting NWMP mining operations to the Port of Townsville -- has demonstrated acute flood vulnerability:

**2019 Townsville Floods:** A monsoon trough in late January 2019 caused unprecedented flooding, severely damaging the Mount Isa Rail Line across 307 kilometres of track, with extreme erosion at 204 sites, approximately 40 kilometres of major track washouts, and 20 kilometres of track scouring [01-772]. Recovery required 47 kilometres of rail replacement, 120,000 tonnes of ballast, 160,000 hours of labour from over 400 workers, and took 11 weeks to reopen to freight traffic [01-773]. The broader floods caused insured property losses of A$1.24 billion and an estimated total economic impact between $2.5 and $5.2 billion [01-775].

**2025-2026 Disruptions:** Parts of the Mount Isa rail line were again closed from December 29, 2025 due to monsoonal rain causing severe flooding, followed by the impacts of ex-Tropical Cyclone Koji, demonstrating the recurring vulnerability of the corridor [01-778].

**2023 Northwest Queensland Floods:** Record-breaking floods occurred across northwest Queensland in 2023 from Tropical Low 16U, with flood waters leading to evacuations and significant inundation of rural properties and some towns [01-769].

The Queensland Government has committed $380 million over five years for the Mount Isa to Townsville rail line, including $6 million in flood resilience works on a 320km stretch from Hughenden to Cloncurry for bridge abutment and scour repairs and gabion flood protection [01-774].

### 5.5 Townsville Infrastructure Vulnerability

The City of Townsville has a ranking of "severe" for infrastructure exposure and vulnerability to tropical cyclones [01-779]. Flood risk to homes in Townsville is projected to double under climate change, with the region tracking towards a 130% increase in flood risk by 2100 under current emissions trajectories [01-776]. Climate Valuation's Dr Karl Mallon has noted that severe floods in Townsville "were previously expected to happen with a one per cent probability per year. Now, we calculate that they're already about 20 per cent more likely than that due to climate change" [01-777].

## 6. Implications for the MICMRC

The evidence assembled in this document establishes that climate physical risk is a material and growing factor for mineral supply chain reliability globally and acutely within the NWMP. Several implications are directly relevant to the MICMRC feasibility assessment:

1. **Supply disruption strengthens the case for proximate research infrastructure.** Climate-related production disruptions -- from cyclone damage to drought-induced water stress -- will become more frequent and severe [01-789]. A research centre located within the mining province reduces the logistical exposure inherent in transporting samples and research outputs over long, climate-vulnerable corridors.

2. **Adaptation research demand.** Mining companies operating in the NWMP will need increasing support for climate adaptation -- from flood-resilient mine design to water recycling technologies to heat-stress management protocols [01-787]. The MICMRC could position itself as a centre of expertise in climate-adapted mineral processing.

3. **Insurance and bankability considerations.** The tightening of mining insurance terms and the growing requirement for TCFD/ISSB-aligned climate risk disclosure will create demand for rigorous physical risk assessment services [01-757] [01-756]. A locally-based research centre could provide climate risk data, site-specific hazard modelling, and adaptation verification services.

4. **Infrastructure resilience investment creates opportunities.** The Queensland Government's $380 million commitment to the Mount Isa-Townsville rail line, along with ongoing flood resilience works, demonstrates government commitment to maintaining the logistics corridor -- reducing (though not eliminating) the risk that climate events will isolate the region [01-774].

5. **The "dual paradox" of climate and minerals.** The minerals required to build the clean energy infrastructure that mitigates climate change are themselves threatened by the physical impacts of climate change [01-728] [01-729]. This creates both urgency and strategic value in developing resilient mineral research and processing capability in well-governed jurisdictions like Australia.
