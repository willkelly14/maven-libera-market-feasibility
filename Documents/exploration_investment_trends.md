# Exploration and Mining Investment Trends: National and Regional

## Executive Summary

Mineral exploration expenditure is a leading indicator of future mining activity and, by extension, demand for the testing and analytical services that the MICMRC is designed to provide. This document tracks exploration investment trends at the national, Queensland, and North West Minerals Province (NWMP) levels, drawing primarily on ABS quarterly data (catalogue 8412.0), Geoscience Australia's annual Mineral Resources assessments, and Queensland Government policy data. The evidence shows that while national exploration spend has nearly doubled since 2018 and reached record levels, the NWMP is entering a new phase of investment driven by critical minerals policy, infrastructure commitments, and a multi-billion-dollar project pipeline — all of which strengthen the business case for a research and testing facility in Mount Isa.

---

## 1. National Mineral Exploration Expenditure

### 1.1 Annual Trends

Australia's total mineral exploration expenditure has grown substantially over the past decade:

| Calendar Year | Total Expenditure | Year-on-Year Change |
|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 2018 | $2,184 million | — |
| 2019 | $2,648 million | +21% |
| 2020 | $2,809 million | +6% |
| 2021 | $3,596 million | +28% |
| 2022 | $4,055 million | +13% |
| 2023 | $4,270 million | +5% |
| 2024 | $3,949 million | -8% |

The 2024 easing from the 2023 peak reflects a normalisation rather than a structural decline. [07-001, 07-002] On a financial-year basis, FY2024 reached a record $4.2 billion (up 1.6% on FY2023). [07-033] The September quarter 2025 saw renewed momentum, with expenditure reaching $1.068 billion — up 5.7% on the June quarter and the highest quarterly spend since Q4 2023. [07-003]

### 1.2 Greenfield vs Brownfield

A critical structural concern for the industry is the declining share of greenfield (new deposit) exploration relative to brownfield (existing deposit) work:

| Year | Greenfield | Brownfield | Greenfield Share |
|------|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| 2020 | $957 million | $1,852 million | 34% |
| 2022 | $1,270 million | $2,785 million | 31% |
| 2023 | $1,330 million | $2,940 million | 31% |
| 2024 | $1,126 million | $2,823 million | 29% |

AMEC has warned that greenfield exploration spending declined approximately 50% over five years to FY2024, and that "exploration spending on existing projects is now three times that of greenfields or new discoveries." [07-008] Metres drilled in greenfield areas fell 14% in 2023 despite increased expenditure, suggesting rising costs per metre drilled. [07-035] This greenfield deficit is significant for the MICMRC business case: the pipeline of new discoveries that would eventually need analytical and processing services is being constrained, making it more important to support the projects that do emerge with accessible, high-quality testing infrastructure.

### 1.3 Commodity Trends

The commodity mix of exploration has shifted notably towards energy transition minerals:

**2023 National Expenditure by Commodity:**
- Gold: $1,250 million (29% of total, down 18%)
- Other metals (including critical minerals): $767 million (up 58%)
- Iron ore: $700 million (down 2%)
- Copper: $667 million (up 17%)
- Uranium: $55 million (up 148%)

**2024 National Expenditure by Commodity:**
- Gold: $1,187 million
- Iron ore: $770 million
- Other commodities (lithium, vanadium, REE, manganese): $668 million
- Copper: $575 million
- Cobalt and nickel: $209 million

The 58% surge in critical minerals exploration in 2023 and the sustained $668 million in 2024 demonstrate strong and growing industry commitment to the minerals that the MICMRC's laboratories are designed to process. [07-032, 07-010] Copper exploration — a primary commodity for the NWMP — remained robust at $575-667 million nationally. [07-009, 07-010] In Q3 2025, gold exploration surged 45% year-on-year to $431.5 million, driven by record gold prices. [07-011]

## 2. Queensland Exploration Expenditure

### 2.1 Queensland's Position

Queensland is Australia's second-largest mineral exploration state, maintaining a consistent 14-15% share of national expenditure:

| Calendar Year | Queensland Expenditure | National Share |
|--------------|----------------------|----------------|
| 2019 | $354 million | ~13% |
| 2020 | $408 million | 15% |
| 2022 | $553 million | 14% |
| 2023 | $592 million | 14% |
| 2024 | $566 million | 14% |

Queensland exploration expenditure more than doubled in nominal terms between 2019 and 2023, growing from $354 million to $592 million. [07-013] The slight decline to $566 million in 2024 mirrored the national trend. [07-012]

On a rolling 12-month basis, overall mineral exploration expenditure in Queensland reached $919.7 million in the 12 months to June 2024, a significant increase of $148.3 million on the previous year (this figure includes petroleum exploration). [07-014]

### 2.2 Recent Quarterly Performance

Queensland showed renewed momentum in late 2024 and 2025:
- Q2 2024: $139.9 million, up 10.2% quarter-on-quarter [07-015]
- Q3 2025: 26.9% quarter-on-quarter increase in greenfield expenditure [07-015]

These quarterly figures signal that exploration companies are increasing their commitment to Queensland, likely in response to the suite of government incentives discussed below.

### 2.3 Competitive Context

While Queensland's 14% national share is significant, the state faces competitive pressure from other jurisdictions that are growing faster:
- New South Wales reached a record $367 million in 2023 (up 5%) [07-031]
- South Australia surged 78% to $293 million in 2023 [07-031]

This competitive dynamic reinforces the importance of differentiated infrastructure — such as the MICMRC — that can provide explorers with locally accessible analytical services and reduce the time and cost of sending samples to distant laboratories.

## 3. NWMP Investment and Project Pipeline

### 3.1 Regional Overview

The North West Minerals Province encompasses approximately 375,000 square kilometres of North West Queensland, including Mount Isa, Cloncurry, and Julia Creek, and contains one of the world's richest deposits of critical minerals essential for emerging technologies. [07-029] The NWMP Strategic Blueprint was backed by $39 million in state funding over four years as part of the 2017-18 State Budget, covering a range of actions across three strategic priorities to secure the long-term future of the region. [07-028]

### 3.2 Project Pipeline

The Queensland Government maintains a pipeline of approximately 20 projects across the NWMP with an identifiable combined capital investment exceeding $21 billion. [07-023] Key resource projects include: [07-024]

| Project | Company | Capital Investment | Status |
|---------|---------|-------------------|--------|
| CopperString 2032 | Powerlink | $13,900 million | Construction underway |
| Julia Creek Vanadium & Energy | QEM Limited | $1,096 million | Bankable feasibility |
| Eva Copper Project | Eva Copper Mine | $836 million | Financial investment decision |
| Vecco Critical Minerals | Vecco Group | $798 million | Bankable feasibility |
| Richmond-Julia Creek Vanadium | Richmond Vanadium Technology | $512 million | Bankable feasibility |
| Saint Elmo Vanadium/HPA | Multicom Resources | $350 million | Construction underway |
| Paradise Phosphate | North West Phosphate | $300 million | Construction underway |

The diversity of this pipeline — spanning copper, vanadium, phosphate, gold, and critical minerals — directly aligns with the MICMRC's planned analytical and processing capabilities. Each project at the feasibility or construction stage will require ongoing testing services, from assays during drilling campaigns to metallurgical testwork during process design and operational quality control.

### 3.3 CopperString 2032

CopperString 2032 is the single most transformative infrastructure investment for the NWMP: a $5 billion, 1,100 km high-voltage electricity transmission line from Townsville to Mount Isa. [07-022] The Queensland Government has committed $1.3 billion to advance construction. [07-022] The project will connect the NWMP to the national electricity grid for the first time, providing energy certainty to the region's critical minerals sector with reliable, affordable, and renewable power. CopperString is expected to be complete by 2029 and is the largest expansion to the power grid in Australia.

The connection of the NWMP to the grid is expected to catalyse a wave of new mining and processing investment, as energy costs — historically the region's greatest constraint — are reduced. This investment wave will generate demand for the testing and analytical services that the MICMRC is positioned to supply.

## 4. Government Policy and Investment Drivers

### 4.1 Queensland Critical Minerals Strategy

The Queensland Government released its Critical Minerals Strategy in June 2023, initially backed by $245 million of initiatives, subsequently expanded to $315 million. [07-016, 07-017] The strategy is structured around four objectives: move faster and smarter; maximise investment; build value chains; and foster research and ESG excellence.

### 4.2 Zero Rent for Exploration Permits

From 1 September 2023, the Queensland Government reduced the rent for new and existing exploration permits for minerals to $0 for five years (until 31 August 2028), representing approximately $55 million in forgone revenue. [07-018] The policy is explicitly designed to "support companies to redirect funds towards their exploration activities," removing a financial barrier to critical minerals exploration. [07-018]

### 4.3 Collaborative Exploration Initiative

The CEI has $17.5 million in funding available through June 2027 under the QRIDP, providing grants of up to $250,000 per project. [07-019] Nine rounds have been completed. [07-019] The most recent round (Round 9) allocated $5 million across approximately 18 projects, with nearly 75% focused on copper discovery. [07-020] Successful projects include Graphinex's discovery of one of Australia's largest graphite deposits at the Esmeralda project near Croydon.

### 4.4 QRIDP Exploration Funding

The Queensland Resources Industry Development Plan allocated $22.6 million over five years to support exploration companies to discover future mines, with funding available until June 2027. [07-030] This funding supports the CEI and other geoscience programs.

### 4.5 GSQ Geoscience Programs

The Geological Survey of Queensland conducts geoscience surveys at no cost to industry, including airborne magnetic surveys, gravity gradiometry, and magnetotelluric surveys targeting cobalt, copper, and rare earth elements in the NWMP region. [07-021] GSQ also maintains drill core libraries at the Exploration Data Centre (Brisbane) and the John Campbell Miles Facility (Mount Isa), where historical core samples can be re-analysed for critical mineral opportunities. [07-034]

### 4.6 Junior Minerals Exploration Incentive (JMEI)

At the federal level, the JMEI allocated approximately $190 million in tax credits between 2017 and 2024, driving $404 million in greenfield exploration activity, $1.2 billion in capital raising, $769 million in GDP contributions, and $391 million in government revenue. [07-025] The program demonstrated a powerful multiplier effect, with more than $6 raised in capital markets for every dollar invested through the scheme.

However, the JMEI was not renewed in the 2025-26 Federal Budget, creating uncertainty for junior explorers. [07-026] AMEC has called for the program to be reinstated with $200 million in funding and transitioned to a permanent budget allocation. [07-027] The absence of the JMEI may slow greenfield exploration — precisely the type of activity that generates the greatest demand for analytical services from facilities like the MICMRC.

## 5. Implications for the MICMRC

### 5.1 Exploration Spend as a Leading Indicator

Mineral exploration expenditure is a leading indicator of demand for testing services. The progression from exploration to mining follows a well-defined pathway: discovery drilling generates samples for assay and analysis; feasibility studies require metallurgical testwork and process design; construction and commissioning involve quality control testing; and ongoing operations require routine analytical services.

The data in this document demonstrates that:

1. **National exploration spend is robust.** At approximately $4 billion per year, Australia's mineral exploration industry represents a large and growing market for analytical services. The recovery in Q3 2025 suggests the 2024 dip was temporary.

2. **Queensland's exploration spend has more than doubled since 2019** and the state maintains a consistent 14% share of national activity, representing a substantial regional market.

3. **Critical minerals exploration is surging.** The 58% increase in 'other metals' exploration in 2023, sustained spending on copper, and the commodity mix of the NWMP pipeline all point to growing demand for exactly the types of testing services the MICMRC will offer.

4. **The NWMP is entering a transformative phase.** The $21 billion project pipeline, CopperString 2032, and the suite of government incentives (zero rent, CEI grants, QRIDP funding) signal a step-change in regional investment. As these projects advance through feasibility to construction and operation, they will generate sustained demand for local testing services.

5. **The greenfield exploration deficit creates opportunity.** The decline in greenfield exploration nationally makes each new discovery more valuable and more likely to be fast-tracked to production, increasing the urgency of locally accessible testing infrastructure.

### 5.2 Competitive Advantage

The MICMRC's location in Mount Isa — at the heart of the NWMP — positions it to capture exploration-driven testing demand that currently leaks to laboratories in Brisbane, Townsville, or Perth. With a $21 billion project pipeline within the region, the argument for local testing infrastructure is not speculative; it is grounded in committed and advancing investment.

## 6. Data Limitations

Two data gaps should be noted:

1. **NWMP-specific exploration expenditure:** The ABS publishes exploration data at the state level but does not provide sub-state regional breakdowns. Queensland's $566 million (2024) cannot be disaggregated to the NWMP specifically. However, the NWMP project pipeline, CEI grant allocations, and GSQ geoscience programs all provide indirect evidence of strong regional exploration activity.

2. **Exploration permit statistics:** The Queensland Government publishes spatial data on mining and exploration tenures through the Queensland Open Data Portal (data.qld.gov.au), updated daily, but does not publish summary statistics on permits granted, renewed, or relinquished by year. The zero rent policy (07-018) provides an indirect signal — the government's willingness to forgo $55 million in rent revenue indicates that the permit base is large enough for this to be a significant investment. Obtaining precise permit counts would require analysis of the raw spatial datasets or a direct request to the Department of Resources.
